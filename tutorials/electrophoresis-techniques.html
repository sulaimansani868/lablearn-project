Electrophoresis Techniques: SDS-PAGE and Agarose Gel

Electrophoresis principle

Electrophoresis is the movement of charged molecules through a porous matrix under the influence of an electric field. The rate of migration depends on three factors: the charge of the molecule, its size and shape (which determine how easily it passes through the matrix pores), and the strength of the electric field applied. By controlling which of these variables is allowed to determine migration, different types of electrophoresis separate molecules by different properties.

In SDS-PAGE, proteins are denatured and coated uniformly with negative charge by SDS, so all migrate toward the positive electrode (anode) and separation is based purely on molecular size. In agarose gel electrophoresis, nucleic acids carry inherent negative charge from their phosphate backbone, and their native charge-to-mass ratio is approximately constant, so they too separate primarily by size.

SDS-PAGE (Sodium Dodecyl Sulphate-Polyacrylamide Gel Electrophoresis)

Principle and the role of SDS

Sodium dodecyl sulphate (SDS) is an anionic detergent that binds to proteins at an approximately constant ratio of about 1.4 g SDS per gram of protein. Because each SDS molecule carries a single negative charge, the protein-SDS complex has a large negative charge that is proportional to the protein’s molecular weight. This overwhelms any intrinsic charge the protein itself carries, so all SDS-denatured proteins migrate toward the anode at a rate determined solely by molecular size (more precisely, hydrodynamic radius, which correlates with molecular weight for denatured, rod-like complexes).

β-mercaptoethanol (BME) or dithiothreitol (DTT) is added to the sample buffer to break disulphide bonds, ensuring complete denaturation and linear extension of the polypeptide chain. Heating at 95°C for 5 minutes in SDS-sample buffer completes denaturation.

Gel composition and percentage selection

A polyacrylamide gel is formed by polymerisation of acrylamide monomers cross-linked by bis-acrylamide, initiated by ammonium persulphate (APS) and catalysed by TEMED. The pore size is determined by the total acrylamide concentration (%T) and the crosslinker ratio (%C). Higher %T produces smaller pores, which resolves smaller proteins better.

Acrylamide % (T)Optimal separation rangeTypical applications
6–8%60–250 kDaHigh molecular weight proteins: myosin, collagen, large antibody subunits
10%20–100 kDaGeneral protein analysis; most common choice
12%14–70 kDaEnzymes, immunoglobulin chains, mid-size proteins
15%10–45 kDaSmall proteins, peptides, histone analysis
Gradient (4–20%)10–250 kDaBroad range; good for complex mixtures

The Laemmli system uses a discontinuous buffer system with a stacking gel (low %T, pH 6.8) layered above the resolving gel (higher %T, pH 8.8). The stacking gel concentrates all sample proteins into a sharp, thin band before they enter the resolving gel, improving resolution dramatically compared to a single-buffer system.

Casting the gel

⛔ Acrylamide is a neurotoxin

Unpolymerised acrylamide monomer is a cumulative neurotoxin absorbed through skin. Always wear nitrile gloves when handling acrylamide solutions. Polymerised acrylamide is non-toxic. Dispose of unpolymerised acrylamide solution as chemical waste, not down the drain.

Gel casting procedure
1
Assemble the casting frame. Clean glass plates thoroughly with ethanol and lint-free tissue. Assemble with spacers of the desired thickness (0.75 mm, 1.0 mm, or 1.5 mm). Seal the bottom with a clamp or casting stand.
2
Prepare resolving gel. Mix acrylamide/bis, Tris-HCl pH 8.8, SDS, water. Degas under vacuum for 5 minutes to remove dissolved oxygen (which inhibits polymerisation). Add APS and TEMED immediately before pouring — these initiate polymerisation.
3
Pour resolving gel to approximately 1.5 cm below the top of the short plate. Overlay immediately with water-saturated isobutanol or water to exclude oxygen from the surface (oxygen inhibits polymerisation). Allow 30–45 minutes to polymerise. A sharp refractive index boundary between the gel and overlay indicates complete polymerisation.
4
Pour off the overlay. Rinse the gel surface with distilled water and blot dry.
5
Prepare and pour stacking gel (4–5% acrylamide in Tris-HCl pH 6.8). Insert the comb immediately. Allow 20–30 minutes to polymerise.
6
Remove the comb carefully with a straight upward pull. Rinse wells with running buffer. The gel is ready for loading.
Gels can be wrapped in wet paper towel and stored at 4°C for up to one week before use.

Sample preparation for SDS-PAGE

  • Mix sample with 2× or 4× Laemmli sample buffer containing SDS (2%), glycerol (10%), Tris-HCl pH 6.8 (62.5 mmol/L), bromophenol blue tracking dye, and a reducing agent (BME 5% or DTT 0.1 mol/L).
  • Heat at 95–100°C for 5 minutes in a sealed microcentrifuge tube. Denature completely before loading. Do not overheat — this can cause artifactual bands from protein aggregation.
  • Centrifuge briefly (12,000 × g, 1 min) to pellet any insoluble material before loading.
  • Protein amount to load: 5–20 μg per lane for Coomassie staining; 0.1–5 μg per lane for silver staining; 0.01–1 μg for Western blot. Overloading causes streaking and band distortion.
  • Always include a prestained molecular weight marker in at least one lane. Prestained markers are visible during the run so you can monitor migration and stop at the right time.

Running the gel

  • Fill the inner and outer chambers with Tris-glycine-SDS running buffer (25 mmol/L Tris, 192 mmol/L glycine, 0.1% SDS, pH 8.3).
  • Load samples slowly to the bottom of each well using a gel-loading pipette tip. Do not disturb adjacent wells.
  • Run at 80–100 V through the stacking gel, then increase to 120–150 V when the dye front enters the resolving gel. Lower voltage gives better resolution; higher voltage runs faster but generates more heat.
  • Stop the run when the bromophenol blue dye front reaches 0.5–1 cm from the bottom of the gel. Very small proteins or peptides run off the gel if you are not monitoring.
  • Do not open the tank while voltage is applied — electrical safety risk.

Molecular weight estimation

Under denaturing SDS-PAGE conditions, the logarithm of molecular weight (log MW) is linearly proportional to the relative migration distance (Rf) of the protein band.

Molecular weight estimation from SDS-PAGE
R⁦ = distance migrated by band / distance migrated by dye front Plot: log(MW) vs R⁦ for each marker band → linear calibration line Read off log(MW) for unknown band from its R⁦ value MW = 10^(log MW read from calibration line)
Example: unknown protein R⁦ = 0.55. Calibration line: log MW = −1.42 × R⁦ + 5.10 log MW = −1.42 × 0.55 + 5.10 = 4.319 → MW = 10^4.319 = 20.8 kDa
Limitations of SDS-PAGE molecular weight estimation

SDS-PAGE gives only an approximate molecular weight. Proteins with unusual amino acid compositions (very acidic, very basic, or glycoproteins) do not bind SDS uniformly and migrate anomalously. Glycoproteins in particular migrate more slowly than their polypeptide molecular weight predicts because sugar residues contribute to hydrodynamic size but not to SDS binding. Always confirm MW by mass spectrometry for precise values.

Agarose Gel Electrophoresis

Principle

Agarose is a polysaccharide extracted from seaweed that forms a porous hydrogel when melted in buffer and cooled. DNA and RNA molecules, which carry uniform negative charge from their phosphate backbone, migrate through the agarose pores toward the positive electrode. Larger fragments encounter more resistance and migrate more slowly than smaller ones. Separation is therefore by size (base pairs or kilobases).

Unlike SDS-PAGE, agarose electrophoresis does not denature the nucleic acid. Double-stranded DNA migrates differently depending on its conformation: supercoiled circular DNA (Form I) migrates fastest, relaxed circular DNA (Form II / nicked) migrates slowest, and linear DNA (Form III) migrates at an intermediate rate that corresponds to its size. This is important when interpreting plasmid preparations.

Agarose gel preparation

Agarose %Optimal separation rangeTypical use
0.5%2–50 kbLarge genomic DNA fragments, BAC inserts, PFGE
0.8%0.8–10 kbGeneral cloning, restriction digestion check, PCR products >1 kb
1.0%0.2–8 kbMost common; general molecular biology
1.5%0.1–3 kbPCR products 100 bp–3 kb
2.0%0.05–2 kbSmall PCR products, microRNA, oligonucleotides
3.0% (high-resolution)10–500 bpSNP genotyping, fragment analysis, short PCR products
Agarose gel preparation procedure
1
Weigh agarose and dissolve in TAE or TBE buffer (1× working strength) in an Erlenmeyer flask. Use 50–100 mL per standard mini-gel tray.
2
Melt in a microwave on medium power in 30-second bursts, swirling between bursts, until completely dissolved and clear. Wear heat-resistant gloves — superheated agarose erupts violently. Do not cap the flask.
3
Cool to ~60°C (hot but comfortable to hold the flask). Add ethidium bromide to 0.5 μg/mL or use SYBR Safe (see safety note).
4
Pour into the sealed gel tray with the comb in place. Remove any bubbles by pushing them aside. Allow to set at room temperature for 20–30 minutes until opaque and firm.
5
Remove the comb, unseal the tray, and place in the electrophoresis tank. Cover with 1× running buffer (TAE or TBE) to a depth of 2–5 mm above the gel surface.
The gel is ready for loading. Gels without fluorescent stain can be run first and post-stained, avoiding stain exposure during the run.
⛔ Ethidium bromide (EtBr) is a mutagen

EtBr intercalates into DNA and is a potent mutagen and suspected carcinogen. Always wear gloves when handling EtBr solutions or gels. Dispose of EtBr-containing waste through your institution’s hazardous waste stream. Many laboratories have replaced EtBr with safer alternatives: SYBR Safe, GelRed, or GelGreen. These have significantly lower mutagenicity and can be disposed of as regular laboratory waste in most jurisdictions after appropriate testing — verify with your safety officer.

DNA sample preparation

  • Mix DNA with 6× gel loading dye (containing glycerol to sink the sample into the well, and tracking dyes such as bromophenol blue and xylene cyanol to monitor migration). Final concentration in the well should be 1×.
  • Typical DNA amounts: 100–500 ng per lane for EtBr detection; as little as 1–10 ng for SYBR Safe with blue-light imaging.
  • Always include a DNA ladder (molecular weight standard) in at least one lane. Ladders are pre-mixed DNA fragments of known sizes; the most common is the 1 kb ladder (bands at 10,000; 8,000; 6,000; 5,000; 4,000; 3,000; 2,000; 1,500; 1,000; 750; 500; 250 bp).
  • Keep DNA samples on ice until loading to prevent degradation.

Running and visualising the gel

  • Run at 80–120 V (constant voltage). Higher voltage speeds the run but can cause band smearing and melting of low-percentage gels. For sharp bands, run at lower voltage for longer.
  • Current flows from negative (black) to positive (red). DNA migrates toward the positive electrode (red). Orient the wells toward the negative electrode so DNA migrates away from the wells toward the opposite end.
  • Run until the bromophenol blue dye front has migrated 60–80% of the gel length. Stop before the smallest bands of interest run off.
  • Image under UV transillumination (254 nm excites EtBr, emits at 590 nm orange/red) or blue light (460–530 nm for SYBR Safe). Always wear UV-protective goggles — UV light causes retinal damage and corneal burns.

Interpreting band patterns

ObservationInterpretation
Single sharp band at expected sizeClean PCR product or restriction digest; proceed with downstream steps
Multiple bands where one is expected (PCR)Non-specific amplification; optimise annealing temperature or redesign primers
Smear below main band (PCR)Primer dimers or non-specific small products
High-MW smear with no discrete bands (genomic DNA)Intact high-quality genomic DNA; expected appearance
Low-MW smear, no intact bands (RNA)RNA degradation by RNase contamination
Plasmid showing 2–3 bands without digestionDifferent topological forms: supercoiled (fast), nicked circular (slow), and possibly linear
Very faint or absent bandsInsufficient DNA loaded; degradation; EtBr exhausted; UV lamp weak

Safety considerations

Key safety points for both techniques
  • Acrylamide (SDS-PAGE): cumulative neurotoxin in unpolymerised form — gloves at all times; no mouth pipetting
  • EtBr (agarose): mutagen and carcinogen — use alternatives (GelRed, SYBR Safe) where possible; dedicated waste disposal
  • High voltage: both systems run at 80–200 V DC with significant current; never open the tank lid while power is on; verify power is off before adjusting gel or electrodes
  • UV light: always wear UV-protective goggles or face shield when viewing gels under UV; UV from transilluminators will damage unprotected eyes within seconds
  • Methanol and acetic acid: used in Coomassie staining — work in fume hood; flammable

Key takeaways

What to remember from this tutorial

  • SDS-PAGE separates denatured proteins by molecular weight. SDS confers uniform negative charge proportional to mass; migration rate depends solely on size. BME/DTT breaks disulphide bonds for complete denaturation.
  • Choose acrylamide percentage based on the molecular weight range of interest: 10% for general use (20–100 kDa); 15% for small proteins; 6–8% for large proteins. Gradient gels cover the widest range.
  • The discontinuous Laemmli system uses a low-%T stacking gel (pH 6.8) to concentrate samples into sharp bands before they enter the resolving gel (pH 8.8). This is critical for resolution.
  • Estimate MW by plotting log(MW) vs Rf for marker bands, then reading off the unknown’s Rf. Glycoproteins migrate anomalously — confirm exact MW by mass spectrometry.
  • Agarose gel percentage determines the separation range: 0.8–1.0% for general DNA (0.2–10 kb); 2.0–3.0% for small PCR products and fragments <500 bp.
  • DNA migrates toward the positive electrode. Three topological forms of circular DNA (supercoiled, nicked, linear) migrate at different rates — uncut plasmid typically shows 2–3 bands.
  • EtBr is a mutagen — use SYBR Safe or GelRed where possible. Unpolymerised acrylamide is a neurotoxin — gloves always. High voltage is live during electrophoresis — never open the tank lid while power is applied.
