Preparing Buffer Solutions: From Calculation to Bench

What is a buffer?

A buffer is a solution that resists changes in pH when small amounts of acid or base are added. It consists of a weak acid and its conjugate base (or a weak base and its conjugate acid) in equilibrium. Buffers are essential in biochemistry, clinical analysis, and molecular biology because enzyme activity, protein stability, and many chemical reactions are highly pH-dependent.

This tutorial focuses on the practical preparation of buffer solutions — how to calculate the masses of each component, how to make up the solution at the bench, and how to adjust pH accurately. For the theory behind how buffers work, refer to the Buffer Preparation section of the Buffer Calculator.

How this differs from buffer theory

Knowing the Henderson-Hasselbalch equation tells you what ratio of acid to base gives a target pH. This tutorial answers the next practical question: how much of each compound do you actually weigh out, and what do you do when the pH meter disagrees with your calculation? Calculated ratios are a starting point — thermal effects, ionic strength, and reagent purity all shift the final pH slightly, making bench adjustment essential.

Choosing the right buffer system

The most important criterion is that the buffer's pKₐ should be within 1 pH unit of your target pH. Outside this range, the buffer has little capacity to resist pH change. Secondary considerations include compatibility with your assay (some buffers inhibit enzymes or chelate metal ions), toxicity, and cost.

Tris buffers and temperature

Tris is exceptionally temperature-sensitive — its pKₐ decreases by approximately 0.028 pH units per °C. A Tris buffer adjusted to pH 8.0 at 4°C will read approximately pH 7.5 at 37°C. Always adjust the pH of Tris buffers at the temperature at which they will be used.

Henderson-Hasselbalch calculation

The Henderson-Hasselbalch equation gives the ratio of conjugate base to weak acid needed for a target pH:

Figure 1 — Titration curve of a weak acid (acetic acid, pKₐ = 4.76) with strong base (NaOH)
The curve has four distinct regions. Initial steep rise: the pure weak acid at the start has a relatively low pH; adding the first small volumes of base causes a rapid rise. Buffering plateau: as more base is added, the pH rises slowly and resists change — this is the buffering region. At the midpoint of this plateau (the half-equivalence point), exactly half the acid has been neutralised, [A⁻] = [HA], and pH = pKₐ exactly. Equivalence point: all the acid has been neutralised; a sharp pH jump occurs here. Post-equivalence: excess base drives pH toward 12–14. The effective buffer range (shaded, pKₐ ± 1 pH unit) is where the buffer has its greatest capacity. Outside this range, buffering is poor and pH drifts easily.
Henderson-Hasselbalch equation
pH = pKₐ + log([A⁻] / [HA])
Rearranged: [A⁻]/[HA] = 10^(pH − pKₐ) Then: fraction as base = ratio / (1 + ratio) And: fraction as acid = 1 / (1 + ratio)
Worked example — 0.1 mol/L phosphate buffer, pH 7.4, 500 mL
1
Components: NaH₂PO₄ (acid, MW 137.99 g/mol) and Na₂HPO₄ (base, MW 141.96 g/mol). pKₐ = 7.20.
2
Ratio = 10^(7.4 − 7.20) = 10^0.20 = 1.585
3
Fraction as base (Na₂HPO₄) = 1.585 / (1 + 1.585) = 0.613
4
Fraction as acid (NaH₂PO₄) = 1 / (1 + 1.585) = 0.387
5
Total moles in 500 mL at 0.1 mol/L = 0.1 × 0.500 = 0.05 mol
6
Moles Na₂HPO₄ = 0.05 × 0.613 = 0.03065 mol → mass = 0.03065 × 141.96 = 4.351 g
7
Moles NaH₂PO₄ = 0.05 × 0.387 = 0.01935 mol → mass = 0.01935 × 137.99 = 2.669 g
Weigh 4.351 g Na₂HPO₄ and 2.669 g NaH₂PO₄·H₂O. Dissolve and make up to 500 mL. Verify and fine-adjust pH with a calibrated pH meter.

Equipment required

Checklist
  • Analytical balance (4 decimal places)
  • Calibrated volumetric flask (Class A, correct final volume)
  • Calibrated pH meter with temperature probe
  • pH meter calibration buffers (pH 4.00, 7.00, and 10.00)
  • Magnetic stirrer and stir bar
  • Beaker for initial dissolution (1.5× final volume)
  • Concentrated HCl or NaOH (0.1 mol/L or 1 mol/L) for pH adjustment
  • Wash bottle, glass funnel, Pasteur pipette
  • Appropriate storage bottle (glass, amber if light-sensitive)

Method 1 — Dual salt method (most accurate)

In this method, you weigh calculated amounts of both the acid and base components, dissolve them, and then fine-adjust the pH. This gives you the most control over the final buffer concentration.

Dual salt procedure
1
Calculate the mass of each component using Henderson-Hasselbalch (or the Buffer Calculator).
2
Weigh each component separately on the analytical balance. Record exact masses.
3
Dissolve both components in approximately 80% of the final volume of distilled water in a beaker on a magnetic stirrer.
4
Bring to the temperature at which the buffer will be used (especially important for Tris).
5
Measure the pH. Fine-adjust with 0.1 mol/L HCl (to lower) or 0.1 mol/L NaOH (to raise), adding drop by drop while stirring.
6
Transfer quantitatively to a volumetric flask. Make up to final volume with distilled water.
7
Re-check pH after making up to volume — dilution can shift pH slightly.
Fine-adjust again if needed. Do not add significant volumes of acid or base after making up to final volume — this changes the buffer concentration.

Method 2 — Single salt + acid/base titration (faster, less precise)

Dissolve a weighed amount of one buffer component (typically the salt form, e.g. Tris base), adjust the pH to your target by adding concentrated acid, then make up to volume. This is faster but gives slightly less control over final concentration because the volume of acid added is not accounted for in the initial calculation.

  • Suitable for buffers where precision of ±0.02 pH units is acceptable.
  • Preferred for Tris buffers, where the free base is weighed and HCl is added to reach target pH.
  • Not recommended for high-precision analytical work where concentration must be certified.

Adjusting pH on the bench

The calculated ratio is a starting point, not the final answer. Reagent purity, water quality, ionic strength, and temperature all shift the actual pH. Always verify with a calibrated meter.

  • Calibrate your pH meter with two-point calibration using fresh buffers bracketing your target pH before every adjustment session. A drifting electrode gives false readings.
  • Add adjusting acid or base in small increments — the closer you are to the target, the smaller each addition should be. Use 1 mol/L far from target, 0.1 mol/L within 0.5 pH units, and 0.01 mol/L for the final ±0.1 pH units.
  • Allow the pH reading to stabilise after each addition before adding more. Rapid addition without equilibration causes overshoot.
  • Record the volume of acid or base added — this is important if you need to account for ionic strength or if preparing at large scale.
⛔ Do not adjust pH after making up to final volume with HCl or NaOH

Adjusting pH in the volumetric flask after reaching the mark introduces extra sodium or chloride ions and slightly dilutes the buffer. Adjust pH in the beaker before transfer, then make up to final volume. A re-check after making up is acceptable; large corrections at this stage are not.

Temperature effects on pH

The pH of buffer solutions changes with temperature because the pKₐ of the buffer acid changes. This is separate from the temperature compensation function on your pH meter (which corrects for the electrode response, not the buffer chemistry).

BufferΔpH per °CPractical consequence
Phosphate−0.003Negligible; adjust at any temperature
HEPES−0.014Adjust at working temperature for precision work
Tris−0.028Always adjust at working temperature — significant shift
Acetate+0.002Negligible
Borate−0.008Small; adjust at working temperature for exactness

Storage and shelf life

  • Store most aqueous buffers at 2–8°C to minimise microbial growth and chemical degradation. Do not freeze phosphate buffers — dibasic phosphate crystallises.
  • Tris buffers stored at room temperature are susceptible to CO₂ absorption from the air, which gradually lowers the pH. Store tightly sealed and verify pH before critical use.
  • Add sodium azide (0.02%) only if compatible with your assay and permitted by your laboratory's hazardous waste policy — it is an effective antimicrobial preservative but highly toxic.
  • Label every bottle with: buffer name, concentration, pH (at the temperature it was adjusted), preparation date, expiry date, and preparer's initials.
  • Most aqueous buffers remain stable for 1–3 months refrigerated if prepared from pure reagents. Verify pH at the start of each working week.

Key takeaways

What to remember from this tutorial

  • Choose a buffer whose pKₐ is within 1 pH unit of your target. Outside this range, buffering capacity is poor.
  • Use the Henderson-Hasselbalch equation to calculate the ratio of acid to base components, then convert to masses using molar mass and total moles required.
  • Always dissolve components in a beaker at ~80% final volume, not in the volumetric flask. Adjust pH in the beaker before transfer.
  • Calibrate the pH meter with two-point calibration before every use. Adjust pH at the temperature the buffer will be used — this is critical for Tris (−0.028 pH/°C).
  • Add acid or base in decreasing increments as you approach the target pH. Overshoot is difficult to correct without disrupting concentration.
  • Re-check pH after making up to final volume; minor corrections are acceptable.
  • Label with buffer name, concentration, pH at adjustment temperature, date, expiry, and preparer. Store refrigerated and verify pH weekly.
