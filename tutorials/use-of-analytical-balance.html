Use of the Analytical Balance

Types of laboratory balance

Not all laboratory balances offer the same resolution or capacity. Selecting the right balance for the task is the first step in obtaining a meaningful measurement.

Balance typeReadabilityTypical capacityUse case
Top-loading (precision) balance0.01–0.1 g200 g – 5 kgWeighing large amounts of reagents, media, non-critical masses
Analytical balance0.0001 g (0.1 mg)80–300 gPreparing standard solutions, weighing small reagent quantities
Semi-microbalance0.00001 g (0.01 mg)30–100 gHigh-precision weighing of small masses
Microbalance0.000001 g (1 µg)1–5 gUltra-trace weighing, atmospheric particulate sampling
Choose the balance that fits the mass range

Weighing 200 g on a microbalance is impossible — it exceeds capacity. Weighing 0.5 mg on a top-loading balance is meaningless — the resolution is insufficient. Always select a balance whose capacity exceeds your sample and whose readability is at least ten times smaller than the smallest difference you need to detect.

How an analytical balance works

Modern analytical balances use an electromagnetic force restoration mechanism. The pan is connected to a coil suspended in a permanent magnetic field. When a mass is placed on the pan, the coil moves; a sensor detects the displacement and feeds back a compensating electrical current to restore the coil to its null position. The current required is proportional to the mass — this relationship is what the balance reads.

This mechanism is extremely sensitive to vibration, air currents, and changes in the local gravitational field. These sensitivities explain why analytical balances have enclosed draft shields, are mounted on dedicated balance tables, and must be levelled and calibrated in the location where they will be used.

Environmental requirements

The analytical balance is the most environmentally sensitive instrument in routine laboratory use. Its placement and surrounding conditions affect every reading it produces.

Environmental requirements for an analytical balance
  • Vibration-free surface: Use a dedicated balance table with vibration-damping feet, positioned away from centrifuges, pumps, and high-traffic areas. Vibration causes fluctuating readings and makes stabilisation impossible.
  • No air currents: Close the draft shield fully during weighing. Do not position the balance near open windows, air conditioning vents, fume hoods, or doorways. Even normal breathing near an open balance can affect the reading.
  • Level surface: Check the spirit level bubble on the balance daily. Adjust the levelling feet until the bubble is centred. An unlevel balance under-reads systematically.
  • Stable temperature: Analytical balances drift with temperature changes. Allow the balance to warm up for at least 30 minutes after switching on (or after a power cut) before making critical measurements.
  • No static charge: Powdery or plastic samples can acquire static electricity, causing erratic readings. Increase humidity slightly, use an anti-static ioniser, or ground metal containers.
  • Away from magnetic fields: Magnetic stirrers, motors, and speakers near the balance can interfere with the electromagnetic mechanism.

Pre-use checks

Every time you use an analytical balance, perform the following checks before placing any sample on the pan. This takes approximately two minutes and prevents wasted experiment time from undetected instrument faults.

Pre-use checklist
1Inspect the pan and surrounding area for residue from previous use. Clean gently with a soft brush if necessary — never blow, as expelled breath causes moisture and CO₂ contamination.
2Check the spirit level bubble. Adjust levelling feet if the bubble is off-centre.
3Close the draft shield and press the zero/tare button. The display should read 0.0000 g. Allow at least 5–10 seconds for stabilisation.
4Verify the zero is stable — digits should not drift continuously. Persistent drift indicates vibration, an air current, or a warm-up issue.
5Check the calibration status. If the balance has not been calibrated today (or within the required interval), perform a calibration before proceeding.

Calibration

Calibration adjusts the balance's internal reference so that it reads correctly against a known mass. Most modern analytical balances offer internal automatic calibration using a built-in reference weight, activated by a dedicated button (often labelled CAL). Balances without internal calibration must be calibrated manually using certified external weights.

External calibration procedure (manual)

Manual calibration with certified reference weights
1Allow the balance to warm up for at least 30 minutes.
2Zero the empty balance with the draft shield closed.
3Using forceps (never bare hands — skin oils and moisture affect calibration weights), place the certified calibration weight at the centre of the pan.
4Close the draft shield. Wait for the display to stabilise fully.
5Record the displayed value. It must be within the balance's stated accuracy tolerance of the certified weight value (typically ±0.1–0.2 mg for an analytical balance).
6If outside tolerance, follow the manufacturer's calibration adjustment procedure or contact your laboratory's instrument service team.
7Record the calibration check in the balance logbook with date, weight used, result, and your name.
⚠ Never handle calibration weights with bare hands

Certified calibration weights are precision artefacts. Skin oils, moisture, and minute particles transferred from your fingers will alter the weight's mass — potentially by several tenths of a milligram — invalidating the calibration check. Always handle reference weights with clean forceps or gloves. Store weights in their padded case, never loose on the bench.

Standard weighing procedure

Step-by-step weighing procedure
1Zero the balance with the draft shield closed and the pan empty.
2Open the draft shield and place a clean, dry weighing vessel (weighing boat, watch glass, or tared flask) on the centre of the pan. Close the shield.
3Wait for the reading to stabilise, then press Tare. The display should return to 0.0000 g.
4Open the draft shield and carefully add the substance to the weighing vessel using a clean, dry spatula. Close the shield after each addition.
5Wait for the reading to stabilise fully before recording. Record all digits displayed — do not round at this stage.
6Remove the sample, clean the pan with a soft brush, and re-zero before the next weighing.

Using the tare function

The tare function subtracts the current reading from the balance's reference, resetting the display to zero. This allows you to weigh a substance in a container without including the container's mass in the reading. The tare function can be applied multiple times sequentially — useful when adding several substances to the same vessel.

ScenarioTare procedure
Weighing a single substance in a boatPlace empty boat → Tare → Add substance → Read mass
Weighing two substances into the same vesselPlace vessel → Tare → Add substance 1 → Record mass 1 → Tare again → Add substance 2 → Record mass 2
Weighing by difference (see Tutorial 10)Weigh full container → Record → Remove aliquot → Reweigh → Difference = mass transferred
Checking balance zero between samplesRemove sample → Tare → Confirm display returns to 0.0000 before next weighing

Common errors and how to avoid them

ErrorCausePrevention
Drifting or unstable readingAir currents, vibration, warm sample, static charge, balance not warmed upClose draft shield; allow sample and balance to equilibrate; check environment
Reading not returning to zero after sample removedSpilled material on pan; balance not re-zeroed; humidity changeClean pan; re-zero between samples; check and maintain zero before each use
Systematic over- or under-readingBalance unlevel; uncalibrated; temperature driftCheck level; calibrate daily; warm up balance before use
Erratic readings with powdery samplesElectrostatic charge on sample or containerIncrease local humidity; use anti-static devices; use metal containers where possible
Condensation on cold samplesSample from freezer/fridge absorbs moisture from warm air, increasing massAllow sample to equilibrate to room temperature in a sealed container before opening and weighing
Sample loss during transferStatic, poor technique, hygroscopic materialUse weighing by difference (Tutorial 10); minimise exposure time of hygroscopic materials

Care and maintenance

  • Clean the pan after every use with a soft brush. Spilled chemicals must be removed immediately — acidic or alkaline materials corrode the stainless steel pan.
  • If a chemical spills inside the balance chamber, clean immediately with a damp cloth and allow to dry fully before use. Report any corrosive spills to the instrument custodian.
  • Never place a load exceeding the maximum capacity on the pan — overloading damages the electromagnetic mechanism permanently.
  • Keep the balance dust cover fitted when not in use.
  • Record all use, calibration checks, and any anomalies in the balance logbook.
  • Schedule annual servicing with a qualified balance technician for internal cleaning, mechanism check, and formal calibration certificate.
Key Takeaways

What to remember from this tutorial

  • Select a balance whose capacity exceeds your sample and whose readability is at least ten times smaller than the precision you need.
  • Analytical balances require a vibration-free, draft-free, level, thermally stable environment — check all of these before every use session.
  • Allow 30 minutes warm-up time after powering on before making critical measurements.
  • Always handle calibration weights with forceps — never bare hands. Calibrate and record in the logbook before use.
  • The tare function zeroes out the container mass — always tare with the empty container in place, then add the sample.
  • Close the draft shield before every reading and wait for full stabilisation — do not record a still-moving value.
  • Never exceed the balance's maximum capacity — doing so permanently damages the mechanism.
  • Record all use and calibration events in the balance logbook.
