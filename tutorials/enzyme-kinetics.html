Enzyme Kinetics: Michaelis-Menten, Km, Vmax, and Inhibition

Introduction

Enzyme kinetics is the quantitative study of how fast enzymes work and how their rate depends on substrate concentration, inhibitors, pH, and temperature. Understanding kinetics is essential for characterising a newly purified enzyme, designing assays that measure enzyme activity accurately (rather than at a rate limited by substrate concentration), interpreting clinical data, and developing enzyme inhibitors as drugs.

This tutorial builds directly on the Enzyme Activity Assays tutorial, which covers measuring enzyme activity in units and the importance of assay linearity. This tutorial covers what happens when the substrate concentration is deliberately varied — and what the resulting data reveal about the enzyme’s catalytic properties.

The Michaelis-Menten equation

When an enzyme (E) binds a substrate (S) to form an enzyme-substrate complex (ES), which then breaks down to release product (P) and free enzyme, the relationship between the initial reaction rate (v) and the substrate concentration [S] follows a rectangular hyperbola described by the Michaelis-Menten equation.

Michaelis-Menten equation
E + S ⇌ ES → E + P v = Vₘₐₓ [S] / (Kₘ + [S])
v = initial reaction rate (μmol/min or nmol/min) Vₘₐₓ = maximum velocity — the rate when all enzyme active sites are saturated with substrate Kₘ = Michaelis constant — the substrate concentration at which v = Vₘₐₓ/2 [S] = substrate concentration (mol/L or mmol/L) When [S] ≪ Kₘ: v ≈ (Vₘₐₓ/Kₘ)[S] — first-order kinetics (rate depends on [S]) When [S] ≫ Kₘ: v ≈ Vₘₐₓ — zero-order kinetics (rate independent of [S])

The equation predicts that as [S] increases from zero, the initial rate increases steeply (nearly linearly) at low concentrations, then curves and approaches Vₘₐₓ asymptotically at high concentrations. Vₘₐₓ itself is never actually reached — it is a theoretical limit approached asymptotically.

The physical meaning of Km

Kₘ has units of concentration (mol/L or mmol/L). Graphically, it is the substrate concentration at the half-maximum rate — easy to read off a hyperbolic plot as the [S] value corresponding to v = Vₘₐₓ/2.

Kₘ is frequently described as a measure of the enzyme’s affinity for its substrate — but this interpretation requires care. Strictly, Kₘ = (k₋₁ + k₂) / k₁, which includes both the rate of ES complex dissociation back to E + S (k₋₁) and the rate of catalysis (k₂, also called kᴰₐₜ). Only when k₂ ≪ k₋₁ does Kₘ approximate the true dissociation constant Kᴰ. In practice, a low Kₘ means the enzyme reaches half-maximum rate at a low substrate concentration — it works efficiently at physiological [S]. A high Kₘ means the enzyme requires a high [S] to be half-saturated.

Hexokinase (brain)Glucose0.1High affinity; saturated at normal blood glucose; constitutively active
Glucokinase (liver)Glucose10Low affinity; only active at high portal glucose (after meals); acts as glucose sensor
Lactate dehydrogenase (heart)Pyruvate0.05High affinity for pyruvate; favours oxidative metabolism
Carbonic anhydraseCO₂8Works near Kₘ at tissue CO₂ levels; rate modulated by [CO₂]

Experimental design for kinetics

To determine Kₘ and Vₘₐₓ accurately, the initial rate (v₀) must be measured at a range of substrate concentrations that cover from well below Kₘ to well above Kₘ. A common error is using too narrow a range — if all concentrations are above Kₘ, the curve appears linear and Kₘ cannot be determined. If all are below Kₘ, Vₘₐₓ cannot be estimated.

Experimental design principles
  • Use at least 6–8 substrate concentrations spanning 0.2 Kₘ to 10 Kₘ (a 50-fold range)
  • Include concentrations at 0.5 Kₘ, Kₘ, and 2×Kₘ — these constrain the curve most effectively
  • Measure initial rates only — take readings during the first 5–10% of substrate consumption
  • Keep enzyme concentration constant across all substrate concentrations
  • Run at constant pH, temperature, and ionic strength — these all affect Kₘ
  • Run at least duplicate measurements at each [S]; triplicate if Kₘ will be used for a kinetic claim
  • If Kₘ is not known in advance, run a pilot experiment with a wide concentration range (e.g. 0.01–100 mmol/L) to locate the approximate Kₘ before the full study

Lineweaver-Burk plot (double reciprocal plot)

Taking the reciprocal of both sides of the Michaelis-Menten equation gives a linear form, which historically was plotted as 1/v vs 1/[S] — the Lineweaver-Burk or double reciprocal plot.

Lineweaver-Burk linearisation
1/v = (Kₘ/Vₘₐₓ) × (1/[S]) + 1/Vₘₐₓ
Plot: y-axis = 1/v, x-axis = 1/[S] Slope = Kₘ / Vₘₐₓ y-intercept = 1/Vₘₐₓ (extrapolate to y-axis: Vₘₐₓ = 1 / y-intercept) x-intercept = −1/Kₘ (extrapolate to x-axis: Kₘ = −1 / x-intercept)
Worked example — Lineweaver-Burk analysis
1
Experimental data: [S] (mmol/L) = 0.5, 1.0, 2.0, 4.0, 8.0; v (μmol/min) = 2.5, 4.0, 5.7, 7.1, 8.3
2
Calculate reciprocals: 1/[S] = 2.0, 1.0, 0.5, 0.25, 0.125; 1/v = 0.400, 0.250, 0.175, 0.141, 0.120
3
Perform linear regression on 1/v vs 1/[S]: slope = 0.137, y-intercept = 0.103
4
Vₘₐₓ = 1 / 0.103 = 9.71 μmol/min
5
Kₘ = slope × Vₘₐₓ = 0.137 × 9.71 = 1.33 mmol/L
The enzyme has Kₘ = 1.33 mmol/L and Vₘₐₓ = 9.71 μmol/min. Verify by substituting back into the Michaelis-Menten equation and comparing predicted vs measured rates.
Limitation of the Lineweaver-Burk plot

The Lineweaver-Burk plot compresses data points from high [S] (which are most reliable) toward the origin and expands data from low [S] (which have the most measurement error) at the left end of the plot. This means that small errors at low [S] have a disproportionately large effect on the extrapolated intercepts. The Lineweaver-Burk plot is excellent for visualising inhibition types but should not be used as the primary method for calculating Kₘ and Vₘₐₓ — use non-linear regression instead.

Eadie-Hofstee plot

An alternative linear transformation that distributes data points more evenly and is less distorted by experimental error at low [S]. Rearranging the Michaelis-Menten equation gives:

Eadie-Hofstee transformation
v = Vₘₐₓ − Kₘ × (v/[S])
Plot: y-axis = v, x-axis = v/[S] Slope = −Kₘ y-intercept = Vₘₐₓ x-intercept = Vₘₐₓ/Kₘ Both axes contain the dependent variable v, so errors in v affect both axes simultaneously — this is a statistical disadvantage, but the plot is better for detecting deviations from Michaelis-Menten behaviour (cooperativity, substrate inhibition) as it amplifies curvature.

Non-linear regression (the preferred method)

Modern statistical software (GraphPad Prism, R, Python with SciPy) can fit the Michaelis-Menten hyperbola directly to the raw v vs [S] data using non-linear least squares regression. This is the correct and preferred method for determining Kₘ and Vₘₐₓ because:

  • It uses all data points with equal statistical weight
  • It does not introduce the distortions caused by taking reciprocals
  • It provides confidence intervals for Kₘ and Vₘₐₓ
  • It can fit variants of the Michaelis-Menten equation (substrate inhibition, allosteric sigmoidal kinetics, Hill equation) with equal ease

Use the Lineweaver-Burk plot for visualisation and for recognising inhibition patterns. Use non-linear regression for the actual parameter estimates that will be reported.

kcat and catalytic efficiency

Turnover number and catalytic efficiency
kᴰₐₜ = Vₘₐₓ / [E]ₜ Catalytic efficiency = kᴰₐₜ / Kₘ
kᴰₐₜ = turnover number (s⁻¹) — number of substrate molecules converted per enzyme active site per second at saturation [E]ₜ = total enzyme concentration (mol/L) Catalytic efficiency (kᴰₐₜ/Kₘ) has units of L·mol⁻¹·s⁻¹ and measures how efficiently the enzyme converts substrate to product at sub-saturating concentrations. “Kinetically perfect” enzymes have kᴰₐₜ/Kₘ close to the diffusion limit: ~10⁸–10⁹ L·mol⁻¹·s⁻¹. Examples: carbonic anhydrase kᴰₐₜ ≈ 10⁶ s⁻¹; acetylcholinesterase kᴰₐₜ/Kₘ ≈ 1.6×10⁸.

Enzyme inhibition

An inhibitor (I) is a molecule that reduces enzyme activity. Understanding the kinetic pattern of inhibition reveals the mechanism of action and guides the design of more potent inhibitors (important in drug development). The three classic reversible inhibition patterns each produce a characteristic signature on Lineweaver-Burk plots.

Competitive inhibition

The inhibitor competes with the substrate for the same active site. Adding more substrate displaces the inhibitor — Vₘₐₓ is unchanged but the apparent Kₘ increases.

Competitive inhibition
v = Vₘₐₓ [S] / (αKₘ + [S]) α = 1 + [I]/Kᴵ (α > 1 always)
Apparent Kₘ = αKₘ — increases with inhibitor concentration Vₘₐₓ — unchanged Lineweaver-Burk: lines intersect on the y-axis (same 1/Vₘₐₓ; different slopes) Example drugs: statins (competitive inhibitors of HMG-CoA reductase), methotrexate (dihydrofolate reductase)

Non-competitive inhibition

The inhibitor binds to a site other than the active site (an allosteric site), on both the free enzyme and the ES complex equally. Substrate binding is unaffected (Kₘ unchanged) but the enzyme-inhibitor complex cannot complete catalysis — Vₘₐₓ decreases. Adding more substrate does not overcome the inhibition.

Non-competitive inhibition
v = (Vₘₐₓ/α) [S] / (Kₘ + [S]) α = 1 + [I]/Kᴵ
Apparent Vₘₐₓ = Vₘₐₓ/α — decreases with inhibitor concentration Kₘ — unchanged Lineweaver-Burk: lines intersect on the x-axis (same −1/Kₘ; different y-intercepts) Example: cyanide inhibition of cytochrome c oxidase (binds Fe in haem, not substrate site)

Uncompetitive inhibition

The inhibitor binds only to the ES complex, not to the free enzyme. Both apparent Kₘ and apparent Vₘₐₓ decrease by the same factor α’, so the ratio Vₘₐₓ/Kₘ (and thus catalytic efficiency) is unchanged.

Uncompetitive inhibition
v = (Vₘₐₓ/α') [S] / (Kₘ/α' + [S]) α' = 1 + [I]/Kᴵ'
Apparent Vₘₐₓ = Vₘₐₓ/α' — decreases Apparent Kₘ = Kₘ/α' — also decreases (paradoxically — apparent affinity increases) Lineweaver-Burk: parallel lines (same slope; different intercepts) Rare in single-substrate enzymes; more common in two-substrate reactions

Mixed inhibition — the general case

Mixed inhibition occurs when the inhibitor binds to both the free enzyme and the ES complex, but with different affinities (Kᴵ ≠ Kᴵ’). It is actually the most common real-world scenario. Pure non-competitive inhibition (Kᴵ = Kᴵ’) is the special case where the inhibitor has equal affinity for E and ES. On a Lineweaver-Burk plot, mixed inhibition lines intersect to the left of the y-axis but above the x-axis.

Determining the inhibitor constant Ki

Kᴵ is the dissociation constant of the enzyme-inhibitor complex — a measure of inhibitor potency. Lower Kᴵ means tighter binding and more potent inhibition. Kᴵ is determined by measuring Kₘapp at several inhibitor concentrations.

Dixon plot method for Ki
For competitive inhibition: Plot 1/v vs [I] at two or more fixed [S] values. Lines intersect at −Kᴵ on the x-axis. Alternatively, from the slope of Lineweaver-Burk: Slope = Kₘ/Vₘₐₓ × (1 + [I]/Kᴵ) Plot slope vs [I]: x-intercept = −Kᴵ
IC₅₀ vs Kᴵ: IC₅₀ (the inhibitor concentration giving 50% inhibition) depends on [S] and is not a true kinetic constant. Kᴵ is substrate-independent and is the correct parameter for comparing inhibitor potency between studies. The Cheng-Prusoff equation relates them: Kᴵ = IC₅₀ / (1 + [S]/Kₘ) for competitive inhibition.

Practical considerations for kinetic experiments

  • Pre-incubation: Allow enzyme and assay buffer to equilibrate to the assay temperature for at least 5 minutes before adding substrate. Temperature affects both Kₘ and Vₘₐₓ.
  • Substrate stock solutions: Prepare concentrated substrate stocks (100× working concentration) in assay buffer. Adjust pH after dissolving substrates that affect pH (e.g. ATP, NADH). Verify concentration by absorbance where possible (NADH: ε₃₄₀ = 6,220 L·mol⁻¹·cm⁻¹).
  • Initial rate criterion: Measure rates only during the period where less than 10% of substrate is consumed. For a continuous assay, this means taking the slope of the earliest linear portion of the progress curve, ignoring any lag phase.
  • Enzyme stability: Keep the enzyme on ice and add it last. If the enzyme loses activity during the experiment (progress curves become non-linear even at high [S]), the data are invalid. Test stability with a time-course control (same [S], measure rate every 10 minutes).
  • Controls: Run a no-enzyme blank (all components except enzyme) to measure any non-enzymatic substrate consumption or hydrolysis. Subtract from all rate measurements.
Key takeaways

What to remember from this tutorial

  • The Michaelis-Menten equation v = Vₘₐₓ[S]/(Kₘ+[S]) describes how reaction rate depends on substrate concentration. Kₘ is the [S] giving half-maximum rate; Vₘₐₓ is the rate at saturation.
  • Low Kₘ = high apparent affinity (enzyme reaches Vₘₐₓ/2 at low [S]). High Kₘ = low apparent affinity. Physiological [S] relative to Kₘ determines whether the enzyme is operating in first-order or zero-order kinetic regime.
  • Use non-linear regression (not Lineweaver-Burk) for parameter estimation. Lineweaver-Burk distorts data at low [S] and should only be used for visualisation and inhibition type identification.
  • kᴰₐₜ = Vₘₐₓ/[E] ₜ measures turnover rate (s⁻¹). kᴰₐₜ/Kₘ measures catalytic efficiency; diffusion-limited enzymes approach 10⁸–10⁹ L·mol⁻¹·s⁻¹.
  • Competitive inhibition: Kₘᵃᵃᵃ increases, Vₘₐₓ unchanged, Lineweaver-Burk lines intersect on y-axis. Non-competitive: Kₘ unchanged, Vₘₐₓᵃᵃᵃ decreases, lines intersect on x-axis. Uncompetitive: both decrease proportionally, parallel lines.
  • Kᴵ is the true inhibitor potency constant and is independent of [S]. IC₅₀ depends on [S] and should be converted to Kᴵ using the Cheng-Prusoff equation before comparing inhibitors.
