Mineral Analysis by Atomic Absorption Spectroscopy (AAS)

Principle of atomic absorption spectroscopy

Atomic Absorption Spectroscopy (AAS) is an analytical technique for quantifying the concentration of specific metallic elements in solution. It exploits the fact that free atoms of each element absorb light at characteristic, element-specific wavelengths — their resonance lines.

In AAS, the sample is converted into free atoms (atomised) in a high-temperature environment (flame or graphite furnace). A hollow cathode lamp (HCL) specific to the element being measured emits light at exactly the resonance wavelength. As this light passes through the atomised sample, free atoms absorb a fraction of it. The instrument measures the reduction in light intensity (absorbance), which follows the Beer-Lambert law:

Beer-Lambert law applied to AAS
A = log(I₀/I) = ε × c × l
A = absorbance (dimensionless) | I₀ = incident light intensity | I = transmitted intensity ε = molar absorptivity (element and wavelength-specific) c = concentration of free atoms | l = path length through the atom cloud In practice: absorbance is proportional to element concentration within the linear range.

AAS is element-selective — only atoms of the element matching the lamp are detected — which makes it highly specific. It measures one element at a time (unlike ICP-OES or ICP-MS, which are multi-element). Its detection limits, simplicity, and relatively low cost make it the most widely used technique for trace mineral analysis in food, clinical, environmental, and agricultural laboratories.

Instrument components

ComponentFunction
Hollow Cathode Lamp (HCL)Emits narrow-bandwidth light at the element’s resonance wavelength. One lamp per element (or multi-element lamps for some combinations).
Atomiser (flame or furnace)Converts dissolved analyte to free ground-state atoms
MonochromatorSelects the desired wavelength and excludes other emission lines from the HCL or flame
Detector (photomultiplier tube)Measures the intensity of transmitted light
Background correctorRemoves non-specific absorption from molecular species, particles, and matrix components. Deuterium lamp (D2) or Zeeman effect correctors are most common.
Nebuliser + spray chamberConverts liquid sample to a fine aerosol for introduction into the flame (flame AAS only)

Sample preparation

Sample preparation is the most critical step in AAS analysis. The goal is to convert a complex matrix (food, blood, tissue, soil) into a clear, dilute, acidic solution containing the analyte at a concentration within the calibration range, free from substances that would interfere with atomisation or detection.

Wet acid digestion (open vessel)

Wet acid digestion procedure
1
Weigh 0.5–2.0 g of homogenised sample into a 100 mL borosilicate beaker or digestion flask.
2
Add a digestion acid mixture. For most food and biological samples: 5 mL concentrated HNO₃ + 1 mL 30% H₂O₂ (or HNO₃:HClO₄:H₂SO₄ mixtures for specific matrices). Check reference methods for your matrix.
3
Heat gently in a fume hood on a hotplate, starting at low temperature to prevent frothing. Increase gradually. Add further HNO₃ in small amounts if the digest darkens — brown colouration indicates incomplete oxidation.
4
Continue until the digest is colourless or pale yellow and no brown fumes are produced. Cool completely.
5
Transfer quantitatively to a calibrated volumetric flask (10, 25, or 50 mL). Rinse the digestion vessel three times with 1–2% HNO₃ and add rinse water to the flask. Make up to volume with 1–2% HNO₃.
The digest is ready for AAS measurement. Digest samples, blanks, and certified reference materials in the same batch.

Microwave-assisted digestion

Microwave digestion in sealed PTFE vessels under elevated pressure and temperature (typically 200°C, 40 bar) is faster, more complete, and reduces the risk of contamination and analyte loss by volatilisation compared to open-vessel digestion. It is the preferred method for trace element analysis where complete matrix destruction is required (As, Hg, Se). The procedure follows the same principles but uses a validated microwave programme specific to the matrix.

Dilution for biological fluids

Serum, plasma, and urine can often be analysed for major elements (Na, K, Ca, Mg) without full digestion by dilution in an appropriate matrix modifier solution. For example, serum for calcium analysis is typically diluted 1:50 with 0.1–0.2% lanthanum chloride (to suppress ionisation and chemical interferences). Blood for lead requires acid digestion or specific whole-blood protocols with ammonium nitrate matrix modifier in GFAAS.

Calibration and standards

AAS is a relative measurement technique — the absorbance of unknown samples is compared to a calibration curve constructed from standards of known concentration. Calibration must bracket the expected concentration of samples from below (zero/blank) to above.

Calibration standard preparation rules
  • Prepare standards in the same acid matrix as the samples (same HNO₃ concentration, same solvent)
  • Use certified reference stock solutions (1,000 mg/L or 10,000 mg/L) from an accredited supplier — do not prepare primary standards from solid metals unless you have high-purity material and a validated procedure
  • Prepare at least 5 calibration points including a zero blank, ideally bracketing the expected sample range
  • The calibration curve must be linear (R² ≥ 0.999) within the working range
  • Re-run the calibration curve if any standard deviates from the expected value by more than 5% during the run
  • Recalibrate if more than 20 samples have been measured since the last calibration check
Worked example — preparing Ca standards from a 1,000 mg/L stock
1
Target range: 0.5–5 mg/L Ca in 0.1% HNO₃ matrix with 0.1% La as ionisation suppressor.
2
Prepare an intermediate standard: pipette 5.00 mL of 1,000 mg/L Ca stock into a 100 mL flask, add 0.1 mL HNO₃, make up to 100 mL → 50 mg/L intermediate.
3
From the 50 mg/L intermediate, prepare working standards at 0, 0.5, 1.0, 2.0, 3.0, and 5.0 mg/L in 50 mL flasks containing 0.1% La.
Plot absorbance vs concentration. Calculate the equation of the regression line (A = m×c + b). Use this equation to calculate sample concentration from measured absorbance.

Measurement procedure

Flame AAS measurement procedure
1
Instrument warm-up. Switch on the HCL for the element to be measured. Allow 15–30 minutes warm-up for the lamp to stabilise. Set the lamp current per the manufacturer’s specification for the element.
2
Select wavelength and slit width. Consult the instrument’s element table for the optimal analytical wavelength and spectral bandwidth for your element and concentration range.
3
Ignite the flame. Turn on air supply first, then acetylene. Ignite using the instrument’s ignition system. Adjust fuel-to-air ratio: lean (oxidising) flame for most elements; rich (fuel-rich) flame for elements forming refractory oxides (Ca, Al, Cr, Mo, V).
4
Optimise nebuliser. Aspirate distilled water. Adjust the nebuliser flow rate to maximise signal stability and sensitivity.
5
Blank and calibrate. Aspirate the blank (0 mg/L standard). Zero the instrument. Aspirate all calibration standards in ascending order of concentration. Verify the calibration curve is linear (R² ≥ 0.999).
6
Measure samples. Aspirate each sample. Rinse with blank solution between samples (at least 30 seconds). Aspirate a mid-range calibration standard every 10–20 samples as a drift check.
7
Shut down. Aspirate 1–2% HNO₃ for 5 minutes to clean the nebuliser. Turn off acetylene first, then air.
Record all raw absorbance values and calculated concentrations. Any sample reading above the highest calibration standard must be diluted and re-measured.

Interferences in AAS

Spectral interferences

Spectral interferences occur when a wavelength other than the analytical line is measured. In FAAS this includes flame background emission and absorption by molecular species. Background correction (D2 lamp or Zeeman) measures and subtracts non-atomic absorption. Overlapping emission lines from other elements are rare in AAS but must be checked when analysing complex matrices.

Chemical interferences

The most common type of interference in AAS. Chemical interferences occur when the analyte forms compounds in the flame or furnace that are not fully dissociated into free atoms, reducing the measured signal.

InterferenceExampleCorrection
Phosphate suppression of calciumPhosphate forms stable CaHPO₄ in flame; reduces Ca signalAdd lanthanum or strontium as releasing agent (excess La displaces Ca from phosphate complex)
Ionisation interferenceEasily ionised elements (Na, K, Cs) reduce free atom population — affects alkali and alkaline earth metalsAdd ionisation buffer (excess of an easily ionised element like Cs or K) to suppress ionisation in samples and standards equally
Refractory oxide formationAl, Si, Ti form stable oxides in air-acetylene flame — low signalUse hotter nitrous oxide-acetylene flame which dissociates these oxides

Matrix interferences

Differences in viscosity, density, or acid concentration between standards and samples affect nebulisation efficiency and flame characteristics. The matched matrix approach — preparing standards in the same acid matrix as digested samples — minimises this. The method of standard additions overcomes matrix effects that cannot be matched:

Method of standard additions
Add known amounts of analyte to aliquots of the actual sample. Plot absorbance vs added concentration. Extrapolate the line back to the x-axis. The intercept is the negative of the original sample concentration.
Used when the sample matrix cannot be replicated in calibration standards — e.g. complex food digests, seawater, industrial effluents.

Quality control in AAS

  • Blank: A reagent blank (all reagents without sample) processed through the entire digestion and dilution procedure identifies contamination from reagents or vessels. The blank absorbance should be near zero and is subtracted from all sample readings.
  • Calibration check standard: Aspirate a mid-range standard every 10–20 samples. If the check standard reads more than 5% from its expected value, recalibrate before continuing.
  • Certified Reference Material (CRM): Digest and analyse a CRM with every analytical batch. Recovery should be 95–105% of the certified value. Consistent recovery outside this range indicates a systematic error in the sample preparation or measurement procedure.
  • Duplicate analysis: Analyse every 10th sample in duplicate. CV% between duplicates should be <5% for routine work and <2% for high-precision analysis.
  • Spike recovery: Add a known amount of analyte to a sample aliquot and analyse. Recovery of 90–110% confirms absence of significant matrix interference for that sample type.
Contamination control in trace element analysis

Trace element analysis at μg/L levels is vulnerable to contamination from laboratory air, dust, glassware, reagents, and even analyst fingerprints. Use ultrapure water (18 MΩ·cm) and sub-boiling distilled or trace-metal grade acids. Digest in PTFE vessels rather than borosilicate glass (glass leaches Si, Na, and some metals). Wear gloves throughout. Carry all vessels through the same decontamination wash sequence used for sample vessels.

Safety considerations specific to AAS

  • Acetylene cylinder: Store upright, secured, away from heat and oxidising agents. Never allow acetylene pressure to fall below 100 kPa (the solvent in the cylinder may be drawn into the regulator). Have a fire extinguisher accessible. Never use copper fittings (acetylene forms explosive copper acetylide).
  • Nitrous oxide (N₂O): Supports combustion and will sustain a flame in materials that would not burn in air. Keep away from flammable materials near the instrument.
  • Flame: The air-acetylene flame reaches 2,300°C; nitrous oxide-acetylene reaches 2,900°C. Never reach into or above the flame. Tie back long hair and keep flammable materials away from the instrument.
  • Fumes from sample analysis: Atomised biological and food matrices release combustion products. Ensure the laboratory is well-ventilated; instrument exhaust must be connected to external ducting or a local exhaust ventilation system.
  • Acid reagents: See the Safe Handling of Acids, Bases, and Corrosive Reagents tutorial for full procedures.
Key takeaways

What to remember from this tutorial

  • AAS measures free atom absorption of element-specific light from a hollow cathode lamp. Absorbance is proportional to element concentration within the linear range (Beer-Lambert law).
  • Flame AAS (FAAS) is suitable for mg/L concentrations and high-throughput work. Graphite furnace AAS (GFAAS) is 100–1000× more sensitive (μg/L range) and uses only 5–50 μL sample per reading — essential for toxic trace elements in clinical samples.
  • Sample preparation (digestion) is the most critical step. Prepare samples, blanks, and CRMs in the same batch. Use matched acid matrix in standards. Contamination from reagents and glassware is the most common source of error in trace element analysis.
  • Calibration requires at least 5 points including a zero blank, R² ≥ 0.999, and check standard verification every 10–20 samples. Recalibrate if the check standard fails the 5% tolerance.
  • Suppress phosphate interference on calcium by adding lanthanum. Suppress ionisation interference by adding an ionisation buffer. Use the nitrous oxide-acetylene flame for refractory elements (Al, Ca, Cr, Mo).
  • QC every batch: blank, CRM (95–105% recovery), duplicate (CV <5%), and spike recovery (90–110%). Document all QC results.
