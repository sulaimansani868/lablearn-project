Units, Conversions, and the SI System

Why a universal measurement system?

Science is an international, collaborative endeavour. For results to be reproducible and comparable across laboratories, countries, and decades, every measurement must be expressed in units that everyone defines identically. The International System of Units (Système International d'Unités, abbreviated SI) is the globally agreed framework that provides exactly this.

Unit errors are not trivial. In 1999, NASA's Mars Climate Orbiter was destroyed because one engineering team used imperial units (pound-force seconds) while another used SI units (newton-seconds) — a mismatch that sent the spacecraft into the Martian atmosphere at the wrong angle. In a laboratory, unit confusion can invalidate experiments, cause incorrect drug dosing, or produce misleading published data.

⚠ Always record units with every number

A measurement without a unit is meaningless. "The concentration was 5" tells you nothing. "The concentration was 5 mmol/L" is a complete, reproducible measurement. Every number in your laboratory notebook must be accompanied by its unit. This is not optional — it is fundamental scientific practice.

The seven SI base units

All SI units are derived from seven base units, each measuring a distinct physical quantity. These base units are defined by fixed numerical values of fundamental physical constants, making them stable and reproducible anywhere in the universe.

SI prefixes are multipliers that scale a base unit up or down by powers of ten. Mastering these prefixes is essential — laboratory measurements routinely span twelve or more orders of magnitude, from nanometre-scale molecular dimensions to kilogram-scale reagent quantities.

The µ (micro) symbol

The correct symbol for micro is the Greek letter µ (mu), not the letter "u". In handwritten notes and informal lab usage, "u" is widely used as a substitute (e.g. "uL" for µL), but in formal reports and publications, always use the correct µ symbol. Most word processors can insert it via Insert → Symbol, or the keyboard shortcut Alt+0181 (Windows) or Option+M (Mac).

Unit conversions

Unit conversion is the process of expressing the same measurement in a different unit. The safest method is the dimensional analysis (factor-label) method, which treats units as algebraic quantities that cancel when multiplied.

Dimensional analysis — the factor-label method
Desired value = Given value × (conversion factor)

Conversion factor = (desired unit) / (given unit)

Example: Convert 250 mL to litres
250 mL × (1 L / 1000 mL) = 0.250 L

Example: Convert 0.5 mg/mL to µg/µL
0.5 mg/mL × (1000 µg/mg) × (1 mL/1000 µL) = 0.5 µg/µL
Note: mg/mL and µg/µL are numerically equivalent — a useful identity to remember.

Concentration units

Concentration describes the amount of a substance present in a defined volume or mass of solution. Multiple concentration units are used in the laboratory, and selecting or converting between them correctly is critical for preparing accurate reagents.

Micromolarµmol/L or µMMicromoles per litre (= 10⁻⁶ M)Hormones, drugs, trace analytes
Mass per volumeg/L, mg/mL, µg/mLGrams of solute per litre (or sub-units) of solutionProtein concentration, antibiotics, culture media
Percent (w/v)% (w/v)Grams of solute per 100 mL of solutionGels (2% agarose), SDS, sucrose solutions
Percent (v/v)% (v/v)mL of solute per 100 mL of solutionEthanol concentrations, detergent solutions
Parts per millionppmmg of solute per litre (for dilute aqueous solutions)Trace metal analysis, environmental monitoring
Worked example — preparing 100 mL of 50 mM NaCl from solid
1Moles needed: 50 mmol/L × 0.100 L = 5 mmol = 0.005 mol
2Molar mass of NaCl: 23.0 (Na) + 35.5 (Cl) = 58.5 g/mol
3Mass required: 0.005 mol × 58.5 g/mol = 0.2925 g
4Weigh 0.2925 g NaCl, dissolve in ~80 mL DI water, transfer to a 100 mL volumetric flask, make up to the mark with DI water.
Final: 100 mL of 50 mmol/L (50 mM) NaCl solution

Significant figures and units

The number of significant figures in a measurement reflects the precision of the instrument used to make it. When performing calculations, the result cannot be more precise than the least precise measurement that went into it.

RuleExampleSignificant figures
All non-zero digits are significant1.573 g4
Zeros between non-zero digits are significant10.04 mL4
Leading zeros are NOT significant0.0025 g2
Trailing zeros after a decimal point ARE significant2.500 mol/L4
Trailing zeros without a decimal point are ambiguous2500 g (use 2.500 × 10³ g for 4 sf)Ambiguous
Multiplication/division: use fewest sig. figs. of any input2.5 × 1.347 = 3.4 (not 3.3675)2
Addition/subtraction: use fewest decimal places of any input10.25 + 1.3 = 11.6 (not 11.55)1 decimal place
Key Takeaways

What to remember from this tutorial

  • The SI system is the international standard — always use SI units in scientific work and convert explicitly when using non-SI units.
  • The seven SI base units are metre, kilogram, second, ampere, kelvin, mole, and candela.
  • SI prefixes scale by powers of ten: kilo (10³), milli (10⁻³), micro (10⁻⁶), nano (10⁻⁹), pico (10⁻¹²) — memorise these.
  • Use the dimensional analysis (factor-label) method for unit conversions — units must cancel to give the correct final unit.
  • mg/mL and µg/µL are numerically identical — a useful identity in biochemistry.
  • Every measurement must be written with its unit — a number without a unit is scientifically meaningless.
  • Significant figures reflect instrument precision — do not report more significant figures than your least precise measurement.
