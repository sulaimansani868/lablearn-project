Spectrofluorimetry: Principles and Applications in Biochemical Analysis

Fluorescence vs absorbance — why fluorescence is more sensitive

UV-Vis spectrophotometry measures the reduction in light intensity after it passes through a sample — it measures what is not transmitted. Because even a blank solution transmits most of the incident light, very small absorbance differences (corresponding to low analyte concentrations) are difficult to distinguish from background.

Fluorescence spectroscopy measures light emitted by the sample at right angles to the excitation beam. The signal is detected against a dark background — a black background with no signal is the baseline. This geometry means that even a very small number of photons can be detected, making fluorescence measurements typically 100 to 1,000 times more sensitive than absorbance for the same fluorescent compound. Detection limits for fluorescent assays are commonly in the picomolar to nanomolar range rather than the micromolar range of UV-Vis methods.

Jablonski diagram — the electronic basis of fluorescence

When a molecule absorbs a photon of appropriate energy, an electron is promoted from the ground electronic state (S₀) to a higher excited singlet state (S₁ or S₂). This happens within femtoseconds. The excited electron rapidly loses some energy by vibrational relaxation (internal conversion) to the lowest vibrational level of S₁. From S₁, the electron returns to the ground state by one of three pathways:

  • Fluorescence emission: the electron releases the remaining excitation energy as a photon. This photon has lower energy (longer wavelength) than the absorbed photon because some energy was lost to vibrational relaxation.
  • Non-radiative decay: energy is dissipated as heat without photon emission.
  • Intersystem crossing to triplet state (T₁): followed by phosphorescence (very slow emission) or non-radiative decay.

Because vibrational relaxation always precedes emission, the emitted photon always has lower energy than the absorbed photon — the emission spectrum is always at longer wavelengths than the excitation spectrum. This energy difference is the Stokes shift.

Excitation and emission spectra

The excitation spectrum shows the emission intensity at a fixed emission wavelength as the excitation wavelength is scanned. It mirrors the absorption spectrum of the fluorophore. The emission spectrum shows the emission intensity at all wavelengths when excitation is fixed at the peak excitation wavelength.

Key characteristics of excitation and emission spectra:

  • The shapes of the excitation and emission spectra are typically mirror images of each other (Kasha’s rule) because the vibrational energy level spacings in the ground and excited states are similar for many organic fluorophores.
  • The spectra overlap slightly at the isoemissive point — the wavelength where the excitation and emission spectra cross. This region must be excluded when measuring emission to prevent artefacts from scattered excitation light reaching the detector.
  • The peak excitation and peak emission wavelengths are characteristic of each fluorophore and used to identify it. They are also sensitive to the local chemical environment (solvent polarity, pH, viscosity), which is exploited in sensing applications.

Stokes shift

The Stokes shift is the difference (in nm or wavenumbers) between the peak excitation wavelength and the peak emission wavelength. A large Stokes shift is analytically advantageous because the excitation and emission bands are well separated, making it easier to isolate the emission signal from scattered excitation light using optical filters.

For example, quinine sulphate has excitation at 350 nm and emission at 450 nm — a Stokes shift of 100 nm, which is large and easily filtered. Fluorophores with small Stokes shifts (e.g. 10–20 nm) are more difficult to work with because excitation light leaks into the emission detector, increasing background.

Quantum yield

Quantum yield definition
Φ₆ = photons emitted / photons absorbed = k₆ / (k₆ + kₙᵣ)
k₆ = rate constant for fluorescence emission kₙᵣ = sum of all non-radiative decay rate constants Φ₆ ranges from 0 (no fluorescence) to 1.0 (every absorbed photon produces an emitted photon) Examples: fluorescein Φ₆ = 0.92; rhodamine 6G Φ₆ = 0.95; tryptophan Φ₆ ≈ 0.13; NADH Φ₆ ≈ 0.019 Higher quantum yield means more photons emitted per excitation event — a brighter, more detectable signal. Temperature, solvent, pH, and quenching agents all affect Φ₆.

Calibration and the linear range

At low fluorophore concentrations, fluorescence intensity (F) is linearly proportional to concentration [C]:

Linear fluorescence equation (low concentration approximation)
F = 2.303 × Φ₆ × ε × l × I₀ × [C]
F = fluorescence intensity (arbitrary units) Φ₆ = quantum yield | ε = molar absorptivity | l = path length (cm) I₀ = excitation light intensity | [C] = fluorophore concentration This linear relationship holds when absorbance A < 0.05 (approximately). Above A ≈ 0.05, the inner filter effect causes deviation from linearity.

Always construct a calibration curve with at least five concentration points. Verify linearity (R² ≥ 0.999) before measuring unknowns. Include blanks and at least one quality control sample of known concentration with every analytical session.

Inner filter effect — the most common error in fluorescence

The inner filter effect (IFE) is the most frequent source of non-linearity in fluorescence measurements. It occurs when the fluorophore (or another absorbing species in the sample) absorbs a significant fraction of the excitation light before it reaches the centre of the cuvette, or absorbs the emitted fluorescence before it reaches the detector. Either effect reduces the measured fluorescence signal disproportionately at high concentrations, making the calibration curve flatten and eventually decrease.

How to detect and correct the inner filter effect

Detection: measure the absorbance of your samples at both the excitation and emission wavelengths. If Aₐₓᴰ > 0.05 or Aₑₘ > 0.05, the IFE is significant.

Correction: dilute the sample until both absorbances are below 0.05. This is the simplest and most reliable approach. Mathematical correction factors exist but introduce their own error if the absorbance measurement is imprecise.

Practical rule: for fluorescence quantification, always work at concentrations where the sample absorbance is below 0.05 at both excitation and emission wavelengths. This means fluorescence assays are typically valid only in a narrow concentration range — dilute samples that appear dark or deeply coloured before measuring.

Fluorescence quenching

Quenching is any process that reduces fluorescence intensity. Understanding the type of quenching helps distinguish artefacts from genuine biochemical information.

Photobleaching

Photobleaching is the irreversible photochemical destruction of a fluorophore by prolonged or high-intensity illumination. The excited state fluorophore can react with oxygen to form reactive oxygen species that permanently destroy the chromophore structure. Signs: fluorescence intensity decreases progressively during measurement; the decrease is faster with more intense excitation light.

To minimise photobleaching: use the minimum necessary excitation intensity; keep the shutter closed between measurements; use anti-fade reagents for fixed microscopy samples; for solution measurements, do not illuminate the sample for longer than needed to take a stable reading.

Biochemical applications of fluorescence

Protein quantification

The most sensitive fluorescence-based protein assays use dyes that become highly fluorescent only when bound to protein:

  • NanoOrange: excitation 470 nm, emission 570 nm. Detection range 10 ng/mL – 10 μg/mL. Binds to hydrophobic regions of denatured proteins. Requires heating step.
  • SYPRO Orange: similar principle; used both for solution quantification and post-gel staining of SDS-PAGE.
  • Intrinsic protein fluorescence (tryptophan): excitation 280 nm, emission 340 nm. No dye required. Sensitivity limited by tryptophan content (some proteins have none). Used in structural studies — tryptophan emission wavelength and intensity shift with protein conformation changes (unfolding redshifts emission from ~330 to ~355 nm).

DNA and RNA quantification

Fluorescence dyes that intercalate between DNA base pairs or bind in the minor groove show massive fluorescence enhancement (100–1,000 fold) when bound to nucleic acid, enabling extremely sensitive quantification:

  • PicoGreen (dsDNA): detection range 25 pg/mL – 1 μg/mL. Approximately 1,000× more sensitive than UV absorbance at 260 nm. Does not respond to ssDNA or RNA. Standard reference for dsDNA quantification in samples containing multiple nucleic acid species.
  • RiboGreen (RNA): analogous to PicoGreen for RNA quantification.
  • OliGreen (ssDNA): quantifies single-stranded oligonucleotides.
  • SYBR Green I: widely used in real-time PCR (qPCR) for monitoring DNA amplification in real time. Fluorescence increases proportionally to dsDNA amount per cycle.
Fluorescence vs A₂₆₀ for nucleic acid quantification

A₂₆₀ measures all UV-absorbing species including free nucleotides, RNA in a DNA prep, and aromatic amino acids from residual protein. PicoGreen specifically detects intact dsDNA and is preferred when accurate quantification of a pure dsDNA fraction is needed. A₂₆₀ is faster and requires no reagents, making it appropriate for routine quality checks during DNA extraction.

Enzyme-linked fluorescent assays

Many enzyme assays exploit the large difference in fluorescence between the oxidised and reduced forms of NADH/NADPH. NADH and NADPH are highly fluorescent (excitation 340 nm, emission 460 nm); their oxidised forms NAD⁺ and NADP⁺ are essentially non-fluorescent at these wavelengths. This allows kinetic enzyme assays to be performed at 10–100× lower concentrations than the absorbance-based equivalent at 340 nm, extending the measurable range significantly for NADH-linked dehydrogenase assays.

Similarly, resorufin-based substrates (such as Amplex Red, oxidised to resorufin by H₂O₂ in the presence of peroxidase) produce a highly fluorescent product with excitation 571 nm and emission 585 nm, enabling extremely sensitive detection of H₂O₂-producing oxidase enzymes at nanomolar substrate concentrations.

Membrane probes and environmental reporters

Several fluorescence applications exploit the sensitivity of the emission spectrum to molecular environment. Diphenylhexatriene (DPH) and its analogue TMA-DPH partition into lipid bilayers; their fluorescence polarisation (anisotropy) increases in ordered (gel-phase) membranes and decreases in fluid membranes, allowing membrane fluidity to be measured quantitatively. Laurdan reports on membrane phase directly through its generalised polarisation (GP), which shifts from negative values in fluid membranes to positive values in ordered gel phases.

Practical guidelines for fluorescence measurements

Before every fluorescence measurement
  • Warm up the xenon lamp for at least 15 minutes — lamp output is unstable immediately after switching on
  • Run an excitation scan and an emission scan of your fluorophore in the actual sample matrix to confirm peak wavelengths have not shifted
  • Measure the absorbance of your samples at both λₐₓ and λₑₘ. If either exceeds 0.05, dilute before measuring
  • Use a quartz cuvette — glass and plastic absorb strongly below 320 nm and will give false readings for UV-excited fluorophores
  • Ensure all four sides of the cuvette are clean and free of scratches — fluorescence is measured at 90° to the excitation beam, so the emission-face cleanliness matters
  • Run a blank (buffer + dye without analyte) and subtract from all readings to remove background fluorescence from the buffer or dye itself
  • Protect the sample from room light during measurement — ambient light contributes to photobleaching and may reach the detector
Key takeaways

What to remember from this tutorial

  • Fluorescence is measured at 90° to the excitation beam against a dark background, giving 100–1,000× better sensitivity than absorbance for the same fluorescent compound.
  • Emission is always at longer wavelengths than excitation (Stokes shift) because vibrational relaxation dissipates some excitation energy before emission. This wavelength difference allows the excitation and emission to be separated optically.
  • Fluorescence intensity is linear with concentration only at low absorbance (A < 0.05 at both excitation and emission wavelengths). Above this threshold the inner filter effect causes underestimation. Always check sample absorbance and dilute if necessary.
  • Quenching reduces fluorescence without destroying the fluorophore — collisional quenching by O₂, heavy metals, and iodide is common. Static quenching forms a non-fluorescent complex. FRET is exploited to measure molecular distances and interactions.
  • PicoGreen detects dsDNA at 25 pg/mL; RiboGreen for RNA; SYBR Green for real-time PCR. NADH/NADPH intrinsic fluorescence enables enzyme assays at 10–100× lower concentrations than the equivalent absorbance-based assay.
  • Use quartz cuvettes for UV-excited fluorophores. Warm up the xenon lamp 15 minutes. Run excitation and emission scans on every new sample to confirm peak wavelengths.
