Immunoassay Techniques: ELISA Principles and Protocol

What is ELISA?

ELISA (Enzyme-Linked Immunosorbent Assay) is a plate-based technique that uses the specificity of antibody–antigen binding combined with the amplifying power of an enzyme to detect and quantify proteins, hormones, antibodies, and other analytes in complex biological samples. It is one of the most widely used techniques in clinical diagnostics, pharmaceutical quality control, food safety testing, and biomedical research.

The central principle is simple: an antibody specific to the target analyte captures it from the sample; a second enzyme-linked antibody or reagent then produces a measurable colour change proportional to the amount of analyte present. The microplate format allows 96 samples (or up to 384 in high-throughput formats) to be processed simultaneously.

The four main ELISA formats differ in how the antigen is captured and how the signal is generated. Choosing the correct format for your analyte and sample type is the first and most important decision in ELISA development.

Figure 1 — The four ELISA formats
Each well cross-section shows the molecular layers involved. In the competitive format the signal is inversely proportional to analyte concentration — more antigen in the sample displaces the labelled antigen, leaving less HRP on the plate and producing a weaker colour.

The four ELISA formats

Direct ELISA

The antigen is coated directly onto the microplate well. An enzyme-conjugated primary antibody binds directly to the immobilised antigen. Substrate is added to produce colour.

  • Advantages: Fastest format — fewest steps. Minimal cross-reactivity between secondary antibodies. Good for detecting antibodies in serum (antigen-coated plate captures specific antibodies).
  • Disadvantages: Every primary antibody must be individually conjugated to enzyme. Lower sensitivity than indirect formats because there is no secondary amplification step. Antigen must coat efficiently to the plate surface.
  • Typical use: Antibody titre determination; viral antigen detection where a conjugated antibody is already available.

Indirect ELISA

The antigen coats the well. An unconjugated primary antibody binds the antigen. An enzyme-conjugated secondary antibody (directed against the species of the primary antibody) binds the primary antibody and provides the signal.

  • Advantages: Higher sensitivity than direct — multiple secondary antibodies can bind each primary, amplifying the signal. The same secondary antibody works for all primaries raised in the same species. Cost-effective.
  • Disadvantages: Extra incubation step. Potential cross-reactivity if the secondary antibody cross-reacts with other proteins in the sample. Not suitable for crude serum samples where the secondary would detect all antibodies of that species.
  • Typical use: Serology (detecting patient antibodies against a pathogen); anti-drug antibody (ADA) assays; qualitative screening.

Sandwich ELISA (most common for quantification)

A capture antibody is coated onto the well. The sample is added and the target antigen is captured. A second, different detection antibody binds a different epitope on the same antigen. An enzyme-conjugated third reagent (secondary antibody or streptavidin if the detection antibody is biotinylated) provides the signal.

  • Advantages: Highest specificity — requires both antibodies to bind simultaneously, dramatically reducing false positives. Excellent sensitivity. The antigen does not need to be pure (it is captured from crude sample). Wide dynamic range. Industry standard for cytokine, hormone, and biomarker quantification.
  • Disadvantages: Requires two antibodies to the same antigen recognising different (non-overlapping) epitopes. Hook effect at very high antigen concentrations (both antibodies saturated simultaneously, signal paradoxically decreases). More steps = more sources of variation.
  • Typical use: Cytokine quantification (IL-6, TNF-α, IFN-γ); insulin, cortisol, thyroid hormones; HBsAg; troponin; CRP; any quantitative biomarker measurement.

Competitive ELISA

Unlabelled analyte in the sample competes with a known amount of labelled antigen (or labelled antibody) for a limited number of binding sites. The more analyte in the sample, the less labelled antigen binds, so the signal is inversely proportional to analyte concentration.

  • Advantages: Works well for small molecules (haptens) that have only one epitope and cannot support sandwich format. Suitable for analytes that do not coat efficiently. Can detect very small molecules.
  • Disadvantages: Inverse relationship (higher analyte = lower signal) is counterintuitive and requires careful interpretation. Less sensitive than sandwich for large proteins. Kit calibrators are essential.
  • Typical use: Steroid hormones (oestradiol, testosterone, progesterone, cortisol); drugs of abuse; mycotoxins; small pesticides; haptens.

Antibody selection and validation

The quality and specificity of the antibodies used determine the performance of the assay more than any other factor. Key considerations:

  • Monoclonal vs polyclonal antibodies: Monoclonal antibodies (mAbs) recognise a single defined epitope, providing high specificity and lot-to-lot consistency. Polyclonal antibodies recognise multiple epitopes, giving higher sensitivity (more binding events per antigen) but with greater lot-to-lot variation and potential cross-reactivity.
  • Capture and detection antibody pairs (sandwich): Must bind the same antigen at different, non-overlapping epitopes simultaneously. Pre-validated matched pairs are available from major suppliers and are strongly recommended for quantitative assays. Develop your own pair only if no validated pair exists for your target.
  • Validation: Confirm antibody specificity by testing against related proteins or antigens. Confirm linearity by serial dilution of a sample with known high analyte concentration. Verify parallelism — the dilution curve of the sample must be parallel to the standard curve, confirming that the sample matrix does not affect binding.

Equipment and reagents

Checklist
  • 96-well flat-bottom microplate (high-binding polystyrene for protein coating)
  • Plate sealer / adhesive film
  • Multichannel pipette (8-channel, 50–300 μL) and single-channel pipette
  • Reservoir troughs (for multichannel use)
  • Plate washer or wash bottle
  • Microplate reader (spectrophotometer) set to 450 nm (TMB readout)
  • Incubator or warm room at 37°C, and refrigerator at 4°C
  • Capture antibody, detection antibody, enzyme conjugate (HRP-streptavidin or HRP-secondary)
  • Coating buffer (carbonate-bicarbonate, pH 9.6)
  • Blocking buffer (1–5% BSA or 5% non-fat milk in PBS-Tween)
  • Wash buffer (PBS + 0.05% Tween-20, PBST)
  • Analyte standard (recombinant protein of known concentration)
  • TMB (3,3’,5,5’-tetramethylbenzidine) substrate solution
  • Stop solution (2 mol/L H₂SO₄ or 1 mol/L HρO₄)

Sandwich ELISA protocol (step-by-step)

The following protocol describes the most common sandwich ELISA format. Times and concentrations are typical starting points; always optimise for your specific antibody pair and analyte.

Step 1 — Plate coating with capture antibody

Capture antibody coating
1
Dilute the capture antibody to the recommended concentration (typically 1–4 μg/mL) in coating buffer (50 mmol/L carbonate-bicarbonate, pH 9.6).
2
Add 100 μL per well to all wells of the microplate. Cover with a plate sealer.
3
Incubate overnight at 4°C (preferred — slower adsorption gives more uniform coating) or 2 hours at 37°C (faster, but less uniform).
4
Wash 3× with 300 μL/well PBST. Invert and tap on absorbent paper to remove residual wash buffer after each wash.
The capture antibody is now immobilised on the plate surface. Proceed immediately to blocking or store the washed, inverted plate at 4°C for up to 24 hours.

Step 2 — Blocking

Blocking saturates any remaining protein-binding sites on the plate surface that were not occupied by the capture antibody. Without blocking, non-specific proteins from the sample and detection reagents adsorb to the bare plate, producing high and variable background signals.

Blocking procedure
1
Prepare blocking buffer: 1–5% BSA in PBST, or 5% non-fat milk in PBST. The choice depends on the antibodies used — BSA is preferred when the detection antibody is anti-biotin or streptavidin (milk contains biotin which interferes); milk is cheaper and works well for most antibody-based detection.
2
Add 200–300 μL per well of blocking buffer. Cover and incubate at room temperature for 1–2 hours, or at 4°C overnight.
3
Wash 3× with PBST as before. The plate is now ready for sample addition.
Do not let the plate dry after blocking or during any subsequent step. Dry wells cause high and irreproducible background. Keep plates covered and moist throughout the assay.
Blocking reagent selection

No single blocking reagent works optimally for all assays. BSA is the most commonly used and works for the majority of sandwich ELISAs. However, some antibodies are raised in BSA-immunised animals and anti-BSA antibodies in the sample can cause false positives. In those cases, casein or polyvinyl alcohol (PVA) are alternatives. Always test the background signal with different blocking agents when setting up a new assay.

Step 3 — Sample and standard incubation

Sample and standard addition
1
Prepare a standard curve by serial dilution of a recombinant standard protein of known concentration. A 7–8 point curve with a top standard and a zero standard (blank) is standard practice. Prepare in the same diluent as samples to ensure matrix matching.
2
Dilute samples in assay diluent (blocking buffer diluted 1:5 to 1:10 in PBST). Run all samples in duplicate as a minimum; triplicate for critical measurements.
3
Add 100 μL per well of standards and samples. Include a reagent blank well (diluent only, no sample) to measure non-specific background.
4
Cover and incubate at 37°C for 1–2 hours (or 4°C overnight for maximum sensitivity). During this incubation, the target analyte is captured by the immobilised antibody.
5
Wash 5× with PBST. More thorough washing at this step is critical — residual unbound sample proteins increase non-specific signal significantly.

Step 4 — Detection antibody

Detection antibody incubation
1
Dilute the detection antibody to working concentration (typically 0.5–2 μg/mL) in assay diluent. If the detection antibody is biotinylated, streptavidin-HRP will be added in the next step.
2
Add 100 μL per well. Cover and incubate at 37°C for 1 hour.
3
Wash 5× with PBST. Thorough washing again — unbound detection antibody is the most common cause of high background.

Step 5 — Enzyme conjugate (HRP)

HRP conjugate addition
1
If detection antibody is biotinylated: dilute streptavidin-HRP (typically 1:500 to 1:1,000) in assay diluent and add 100 μL/well. Incubate 30 minutes at 37°C.
2
If detection antibody is directly HRP-conjugated: skip to substrate addition after washing in Step 4.
3
Wash 7× with PBST. The additional wash steps at this stage are critical — residual HRP in the wells causes explosive background colour development.
The HRP enzyme is now immobilised in direct proportion to the amount of analyte captured in each well. The more analyte, the more HRP, the more colour will develop in the next step.

Step 6 — Substrate development

TMB (3,3’,5,5’-tetramethylbenzidine) is the most widely used HRP substrate. In its reduced form it is colourless. HRP oxidises TMB in the presence of H₂O₂ to produce a blue intermediate. The stop solution (dilute sulphuric acid) converts the blue intermediate to a stable yellow product measured at 450 nm.

TMB development procedure
1
Allow TMB substrate to equilibrate to room temperature. It must be colourless when added — any blue tinge indicates oxidation and the substrate must be discarded.
2
Add 100 μL per well of TMB substrate. Work row by row as quickly and consistently as possible.
3
Incubate at room temperature in the dark for 10–30 minutes. Monitor colour development — the high-standard wells should turn dark blue. Stop before the highest standard well becomes saturated (very dark blue with no visible concentration gradient between standards).
4
Add 50–100 μL of stop solution (2 mol/L H₂SO₄) in the same order and timing as the TMB addition. Wells turn from blue to yellow immediately. Verify all wells have changed colour — wells that remain blue indicate incomplete stop reaction.
5
Read the plate at 450 nm within 30 minutes of stopping. If a dual wavelength reader is available, subtract the 570 nm reading from the 450 nm reading to correct for optical imperfections in the plate.
The absorbance at 450 nm in each well is now proportional to the amount of analyte captured, completing the assay.

Standard curve construction and calculation

The standard curve relates absorbance (A₄₅₀) to analyte concentration. Because ELISAs typically show a sigmoidal rather than linear relationship over a wide concentration range, a four-parameter logistic (4PL) curve fit is the standard approach for quantitative ELISA data analysis.

Four-parameter logistic (4PL) equation
A = D + (A − D) / [1 + (C/C₀)ᴵ]
A = absorbance (response) C = analyte concentration A = minimum asymptote (background signal at zero analyte) D = maximum asymptote (signal at saturating analyte) C₀ = EC₅₀ (concentration at 50% maximum signal) B = Hill slope (slope factor, typically near 1.0) Practical approach: use ELISA data analysis software (GraphPad Prism, MyAssays, or online 4PL calculators) to fit the curve and back-calculate concentrations from absorbance values. Do not use simple linear regression for ELISA standard curves.
Sample concentration must fall within the standard curve range

Any sample whose absorbance falls above the top standard or below the lowest detectable standard must be flagged. Samples above the range must be diluted and re-assayed. Samples below the lowest detectable standard should be reported as “below the limit of detection” (BLD) or “below the limit of quantification” (BLQ), not as zero. Always multiply calculated concentrations by the dilution factor used.

Key takeaways

What to remember from this tutorial

  • ELISA uses antibody specificity plus enzyme amplification to detect analytes in complex samples. Choose the format based on your analyte: sandwich (large proteins, highest specificity), competitive (haptens and small molecules), indirect (detecting antibodies in serum), direct (fastest, fewest steps).
  • Sandwich ELISA requires two antibodies recognising different, non-overlapping epitopes simultaneously. Pre-validated matched pairs are strongly recommended over developing your own pair.
  • Blocking is essential — it prevents non-specific protein adsorption to bare plate surface. Choose BSA-based blocking when streptavidin-biotin detection is used (milk contains endogenous biotin).
  • Washing is the single most important technical step for reducing background. Increase wash volume and number of washes if background is high. Never allow wells to dry at any point in the assay.
  • Use a 4PL curve fit for standard curve analysis — simple linear regression is not appropriate for sigmoid ELISA curves. All unknowns must fall within the standard curve range; samples above the top standard must be diluted and re-assayed.
  • The hook effect causes false-negative results at very high analyte concentrations in sandwich ELISA. Suspect it when a diluted sample gives a higher calculated result than the undiluted sample.
  • Heterophile antibodies (especially HAMA) are an important source of false-positive clinical ELISA results. Use heterophile blocking reagents in the sample diluent for all clinical immunoassays.
