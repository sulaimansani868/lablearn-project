Laboratory Rules, Ethics, and Conduct

Why laboratory rules matter

Laboratory rules are not bureaucratic inconveniences — they are the codified lessons of decades of accidents, injuries, and ethical failures. A laboratory is a controlled environment where chemicals, biological agents, heat, pressure, electricity, and sharp instruments coexist. Without a common framework of rules and conduct, that environment becomes unpredictable and dangerous.

Rules serve three purposes: they protect you, they protect your colleagues, and they protect the integrity of the science you produce. A result generated in an unsafe or unethical environment is not just dangerous — it is unreliable and scientifically worthless.

Core principle

Every rule in a laboratory exists because someone, somewhere, was harmed — or because data was compromised — when that rule was not followed. Treat every rule as a permanent reminder of that lesson.

General laboratory rules

The following rules apply in virtually every laboratory regardless of discipline. They form the minimum standard of acceptable behaviour in any scientific workspace.

Mandatory rules — no exceptions
  • Wear appropriate PPE at all times in the designated work zone.
  • No food, drink, gum, or application of cosmetics anywhere in the laboratory.
  • Never work alone — always ensure another trained person is present or within call.
  • Keep all containers labelled with contents, concentration, date, and your name.
  • Know the location of all safety equipment before starting any work.
  • Report every accident, spill, injury, or near-miss immediately to your supervisor.
  • Follow waste disposal procedures exactly — never pour unknown substances to drain.
  • Never remove chemicals, samples, or equipment from the laboratory without authorisation.
  • Do not use equipment you have not been trained to operate.
  • Keep walkways, emergency exits, and safety equipment access routes clear at all times.

Rules about personal behaviour

Many laboratory accidents are caused not by faulty equipment but by inattentive or reckless behaviour. The following personal conduct rules address the most common human-factor risks.

BehaviourWhy it is prohibitedWhat to do instead
Running in the laboratoryCauses collisions with colleagues or equipment; increases spill riskWalk, carry glassware with both hands at or below chest height
Using a mobile phone at the benchDistraction; phone surfaces can transfer contaminationSet phone aside; wash hands before touching personal items
Wearing open-toed shoesSevere injury risk from chemical spills or dropped glasswareClosed-toe, closed-heel shoes; no canvas or mesh
Smelling an unknown substanceMany lab vapours are toxic at low concentrationsUse a wafting motion — fan vapour gently toward your nose from a safe distance
Mouth-pipettingIngestion of toxic or infectious materials; universally banned since the 1970sAlways use a pipette aid, bulb, or electronic pipette controller
Working while fatigued or unwellImpaired judgement and reaction time increase accident riskNotify your supervisor; reschedule non-urgent work

Scientific ethics

Scientific ethics govern not just how you behave in the laboratory, but how you handle data, authorship, and intellectual honesty. Ethical failures can end careers, retract published papers, harm patients (if clinical data is involved), and erode public trust in science.

Data integrity

The most fundamental ethical obligation in experimental science is the honest recording and reporting of data. This means recording what you actually observed — not what you expected, hoped for, or what the theory predicts.

⚠ Research misconduct — serious violations

Fabrication (inventing data), Falsification (manipulating data to change outcomes), and Plagiarism (presenting others' work as your own) are collectively known as FFP. These constitute research misconduct and are grounds for dismissal, retraction of publications, and in some countries, criminal prosecution.

Honesty in recording observations

In your laboratory notebook, record observations in real time — not from memory at the end of the day. If an experiment fails, record that it failed and why. If you make an error, cross it out with a single line so the original entry remains readable, then write the correct entry alongside. Never erase, use correction fluid, or tear out pages.

Authorship and credit

Scientific authorship carries responsibility, not just recognition. An author must have made a substantial contribution to the work — simply running a sample or providing funding is not sufficient for authorship. Discuss authorship expectations with your supervisor before a project begins, not after.

Intellectual property and confidentiality

Research conducted in an institution may belong to that institution, not to the individual researcher. Before sharing data, methods, or results — even informally — understand your institution's intellectual property (IP) and confidentiality policies. This is especially important in industry-affiliated research.

Professional conduct

Professionalism in a laboratory means treating colleagues, equipment, and the scientific process with respect. It encompasses communication, attitude, and the recognition that science is a collaborative endeavour.

Professional behaviourWhy it matters
Communicate clearly and promptly with your supervisorPrevents misunderstandings; ensures timely correction of errors
Clean shared equipment after useShared facilities depend on collective responsibility
Respect others' experiments and workspaceCross-contamination and interference with running experiments can destroy weeks of work
Acknowledge mistakes openlyConcealing errors wastes resources and can compromise safety
Follow the chain of communication for concernsRaises issues appropriately without undermining team function
Complete required training before using new equipmentProtects you, your colleagues, and expensive instruments

Respect and inclusion

A laboratory is a diverse professional environment. Harassment, discrimination, or intimidation of any kind — based on gender, ethnicity, religion, disability, or any other characteristic — is prohibited and often illegal. Every person in a laboratory is entitled to work in an environment free from hostility.

Individual responsibilities

Every person working in a laboratory, regardless of seniority, carries a set of non-delegable personal responsibilities.

Your personal responsibilities
  • Complete all required laboratory induction and safety training before beginning work.
  • Read and understand the risk assessment for every experiment you conduct.
  • Read the Safety Data Sheet (SDS) for any new chemical before first use.
  • Maintain your own PPE in good condition and replace damaged items promptly.
  • Keep your section of the laboratory tidy and free of unnecessary items.
  • Store your materials correctly — flammables in designated cabinets, biologicals in appropriate containment.
  • Ensure your laboratory notebook is current and accurate at all times.
  • Stay up to date with your institution's safety updates and policy changes.

Reporting incidents and near-misses

An incident is any unplanned event that causes harm or damage. A near-miss is an event that did not cause harm but could have under slightly different circumstances. Both must be reported.

Near-misses are especially valuable — they reveal hazards before an injury occurs, giving the institution the chance to correct conditions that would otherwise eventually cause real harm. A culture that punishes the reporting of near-misses is a culture that accumulates unreported risk until a serious accident occurs.

How to report

Notify your supervisor verbally immediately after the event. Complete an incident report form within 24 hours. Include: time, location, what happened, who was involved, what actions were taken, and any equipment or substances involved. Do not wait until you are certain something is reportable — report and let your supervisor determine significance.

Consequences of rule violations

Violations of laboratory rules carry consequences that scale with the severity and whether the violation was accidental or deliberate. Understanding the potential consequences reinforces why compliance is not optional.

Violation typePotential consequence
Minor procedural breach (e.g. unlabelled container)Verbal or written warning; mandatory retraining
PPE non-compliance in a hazard zoneImmediate removal from the laboratory; formal warning
Improper chemical disposalDisciplinary action; possible regulatory reporting
Operating unauthorised equipmentSuspension of laboratory access pending investigation
Data fabrication or falsificationDismissal; retraction of publications; potential criminal liability
Deliberate harm to a colleague or their workImmediate dismissal; possible criminal prosecution
Key Takeaways

What to remember from this tutorial

  • Laboratory rules exist because they prevent real harm — they are not optional guidelines.
  • No food, drink, running, mouth-pipetting, or working alone. These apply in every laboratory without exception.
  • Scientific ethics demand honest recording of all observations — including failures and unexpected results.
  • Fabrication, falsification, and plagiarism (FFP) are research misconduct and can result in dismissal and legal consequences.
  • Near-misses must be reported — they are the most valuable opportunity to prevent future injury.
  • Every person in a laboratory is personally responsible for their own safety training, PPE, and compliance.
  • Professionalism includes clean-up of shared equipment, open communication with supervisors, and respect for all colleagues.
