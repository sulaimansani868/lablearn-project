Weighing Techniques: By Difference and Direct Weighing

Two fundamental weighing methods

All analytical weighing in the laboratory uses one of two fundamental approaches: direct weighing (placing the substance directly onto the balance and reading the mass) or weighing by difference (calculating the mass transferred as the difference between two readings of the source container). Each method has specific advantages that make it preferable in certain situations, and knowing which to apply is an essential laboratory skill.

FeatureDirect weighingWeighing by difference
Number of balance readings1 (net mass after tare)2 (before and after transfer)
Sample loss accounted for?No — any material left in the source vessel is not weighedYes — any material remaining is subtracted automatically
Best forNon-critical, stable, non-hygroscopic materialsHygroscopic, volatile, or reactive materials; precise aliquots
Risk of contaminationHigher — sample contacts an intermediate vesselLower — sample delivered directly from original container
PrecisionGoodSlightly higher — eliminates transfer loss as a variable

Direct weighing

Direct weighing is the straightforward approach: place a container on the balance, tare to zero, then add the substance until the desired mass is reached. This is the most intuitive method and is appropriate when the substance is stable, non-hygroscopic, and can be transferred cleanly from a spatula to a weighing vessel.

Direct weighing — step-by-step
1Place a clean, dry weighing boat or watch glass on the balance pan. Close the draft shield and press Tare. Confirm the display reads 0.0000 g.
2Open the draft shield and add the substance in small increments using a clean, dry spatula. Close the shield between additions to allow the reading to stabilise.
3When the reading approaches the target, switch to a smaller spatula or tap the larger one gently to add the final small portion. Overshoot cannot be corrected by removing material — a fresh weighing vessel must be used.
4Close the draft shield. Record the displayed mass once fully stable — record all four decimal places.
5Transfer the substance quantitatively to the destination vessel. Rinse the weighing vessel with solvent if a quantitative transfer is required.
Never remove material from a weighing vessel to correct overshoot

If you add too much substance during direct weighing, you cannot remove the excess back into the stock container — this risks contamination. Instead, record the actual mass weighed (not the target), adjust your subsequent calculations accordingly, or start fresh with a new weighing vessel if the overshoot is too large to be practically accommodated.

Achieving a target mass in direct weighing

When a specific mass is required (e.g. exactly 0.1000 g for a standard solution), use the following approach: add roughly 90% of the target mass in large portions, then switch to a fine spatula or a slightly tapped spatula for the remaining 10%. Accepting a mass within ±0.5% of target (e.g. 0.0995–0.1005 g for a 0.1000 g target) and adjusting the subsequent volume calculation is more efficient than attempting to hit the exact digit — the balance's precision easily accommodates this calculation correction.

Weighing by difference

In weighing by difference, the substance is not transferred to an intermediate vessel. Instead, the entire source container (bottle, weighing tube, or scoop) is weighed before and after dispensing a portion. The difference between the two readings equals the mass of the portion transferred — including any material that may have clung to the spatula and been delivered to the destination.

Weighing by difference — step-by-step
1Zero the balance with nothing on the pan. Place the source container (e.g. a closed bottle or weighing tube containing the substance) on the pan. Close the draft shield.
2Wait for full stabilisation. Record the mass: this is m₁ (initial mass). Do not press Tare.
3Remove the container from the balance. Transfer the desired amount of substance to the destination vessel (flask, beaker). Replace any cap or lid on the source container.
4Return the source container to the balance. Close the draft shield. Wait for full stabilisation. Record the new mass: this is m₂ (final mass).
5Calculate: mass transferred = m₁ − m₂. This is the exact mass of substance now in your destination vessel.
Weighing by difference — calculation
Mass transferred = m₁ − m₂

Example:
m₁ (source container before) = 15.8432 g
m₂ (source container after) = 15.6881 g
Mass transferred = 15.8432 − 15.6881 = 0.1551 g
Record m₁ and m₂ fully in your notebook — not just the final difference. This allows the calculation to be verified and errors to be traced.

Why weighing by difference is more accurate for reactive materials

For hygroscopic substances (those that absorb moisture from the air), every second the material spends exposed on an open weighing boat increases its apparent mass. By transferring directly from a closed container and measuring the mass change of the container, exposure time is minimised and the measured mass reflects only the substance — not absorbed moisture. The same logic applies to volatile materials: mass lost by evaporation during an open transfer is captured by the difference measurement.

Choosing the right method

Substance or situationRecommended methodReason
Stable, non-hygroscopic solid (e.g. NaCl, sucrose)Either method; direct weighing is simplerNo significant mass change on exposure to air
Hygroscopic solid (e.g. anhydrous CaCl₂, NaOH pellets, ATP)Weighing by differenceMass increases rapidly on air exposure; direct weighing includes absorbed moisture
Volatile solid or liquidWeighing by differenceMass lost by evaporation is captured in the difference
Toxic or irritant solid (dust hazard)Weighing by difference into a closed container where possibleMinimises exposure time and generation of airborne particles
Multiple portions from the same sourceWeighing by difference (repeated)Successive differences give the mass of each aliquot without intermediate taring errors
Adding to an existing mixtureDirect weighing with tareThe destination vessel is already on the balance; simpler to add directly

Weighing hygroscopic substances

Hygroscopic substances absorb water vapour from the atmosphere rapidly. Familiar examples include: anhydrous sodium hydroxide (NaOH), phosphorus pentoxide (P₂O₅), calcium chloride (CaCl₂), many amino acids, nucleotides (ATP, ADP), and certain proteins. These materials can gain a measurable fraction of their mass within 30–60 seconds of air exposure.

Protocol for highly hygroscopic materials
  • Store in a desiccator over silica gel or molecular sieves. Allow to equilibrate in the closed desiccator for several hours before use if recently opened.
  • Use weighing by difference — keep the source container closed until the moment of transfer.
  • Work quickly — minimise the time between opening the container and completing the transfer.
  • Keep the balance and weighing area dry — high ambient humidity accelerates absorption.
  • If a weighing boat must be used, pre-weigh it and be prepared to work as rapidly as possible, transferring to solvent immediately after reading.
  • For extremely hygroscopic materials (e.g. P₂O₅, dry AlCl₃), weigh in a dry glove bag or with dry air purging the balance chamber.

Weighing volatile substances

Volatile substances evaporate at room temperature, causing the reading on an open balance to decrease continuously. Direct weighing with an open vessel is therefore unreliable. Strategies include:

  • Closed container method: Weigh by difference using a closed vial — tare the sealed vial, remove a portion by syringe or pipette with the lid replaced, then re-weigh the closed vial. The mass lost equals the mass of liquid removed.
  • Cooled container: For very volatile liquids, cool the receiving vessel in ice to slow evaporation during transfer.
  • Work rapidly: Where brief open exposure is unavoidable, minimise the time between opening and completing the weighing, and record immediately.

Weighing very small masses

When target masses are below approximately 10 mg, the weighing process requires additional care. At these masses, air currents, static electricity, and the mass of a few particles of contamination represent a significant fraction of the total.

ChallengeStrategy
Electrostatic charge on plastic vessels or powdersUse metal weighing vessels; apply an anti-static ioniser; increase local humidity
Sample sticking to spatulaUse a fine stainless steel spatula; tap spatula gently against the vessel edge to dislodge adhering particles
Draft shield disturbance from opening and closingAllow 10–15 seconds after closing before reading; use the balance's draft-stable mode if available
Balance readability insufficientUse a semi-microbalance (0.01 mg readability) for masses below 10 mg
Loss of material during transferAlways use weighing by difference for very small masses; minimise surface area of intermediate vessels

Recording weighing data correctly

All balance readings must be recorded in your laboratory notebook at the time of weighing — not recalculated later from memory. The following data must be captured for every weighing event.

Minimum data to record for each weighing
Date and time of weighing Balance ID / model / location Substance name, CAS number, lot number Weighing vessel description m₁ (initial reading, g) — for by-difference: full container mass m₂ (final reading, g) — for by-difference: container after transfer Net mass = m₁ − m₂ (or tare-subtracted reading for direct weighing) Calibration status of balance (date of last calibration) Operator initials
Key Takeaways

What to remember from this tutorial

  • Direct weighing is simpler and suitable for stable, non-hygroscopic substances where small transfer losses are acceptable.
  • Weighing by difference is more accurate for hygroscopic, volatile, or reactive materials — the mass transferred is captured by the difference m₁ − m₂, regardless of what clings to the spatula.
  • Never remove excess material from a weighing boat — record the actual mass and adjust calculations, or start fresh.
  • For hygroscopic materials, work from a closed container, work quickly, and store in a desiccator before and after use.
  • For volatile liquids, weigh in a closed vial by difference using a syringe for the transfer portion.
  • Below 10 mg, use a semi-microbalance, eliminate static charge, and always use weighing by difference.
  • Record both m₁ and m₂ in your notebook — not just the calculated difference — so the weighing can be verified.
