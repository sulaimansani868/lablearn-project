Reading the Meniscus Correctly

What is a meniscus?

When a liquid is contained in a narrow glass or plastic tube, the surface of the liquid is not flat — it curves where it meets the wall. This curved liquid surface is called the meniscus (plural: menisci), from the Greek word for crescent.

The shape of the meniscus arises from two competing forces: cohesion (liquid molecules attracting each other) and adhesion (liquid molecules being attracted to the container wall). The balance between these forces determines whether the meniscus curves downward (concave) or upward (convex), and it differs between liquids.

Reading the meniscus at the correct position is one of the most fundamental volumetric techniques in the laboratory. An error of even one graduation mark — caused by reading from the wrong part of the meniscus or from the wrong eye level — introduces a systematic error into every measurement made with that technique.

Concave vs convex meniscus

Meniscus typeShapeCauseLiquidsRead from
Concave Curves downward in the centre; edges are higher Adhesion to glass exceeds cohesion between liquid molecules Water, aqueous solutions, most polar solvents, ethanol, acids, bases Bottom of the curve
Convex Curves upward in the centre; edges are lower Cohesion between liquid molecules exceeds adhesion to the wall Mercury, liquid gallium, some non-polar organic liquids in plastic vessels Top of the curve
The most important rule

For water and all aqueous solutions — which account for the vast majority of laboratory volumetric measurements — always read from the bottom of the concave meniscus. The bottom is the lowest point of the curve, not the level where the liquid meets the glass wall.

Parallax error

Parallax error is a systematic reading error that occurs when your eye is not level with the meniscus. If you look at the meniscus from above or below, the graduation mark that appears to align with the meniscus is not actually the one at the same height — your line of sight cuts across the scale at an angle, creating an apparent shift.

Eye positionWhat you seeError introduced
Eye above the meniscus (looking down)The bottom of the meniscus appears to align with a graduation mark that is actually below itYou read a value lower than the true volume
Eye at the same level as the meniscusThe graduation marks on both sides of the tube appear aligned; you see the true bottom of the meniscusNo parallax error — this is the correct position
Eye below the meniscus (looking up)The bottom of the meniscus appears to align with a graduation mark that is actually above itYou read a value higher than the true volume
Eliminating parallax error — the correct technique
  • Position your eye so it is exactly level with the bottom of the meniscus before reading.
  • The front and back graduation marks on the glass should appear as a single line — if you can see two separate lines, your eye is not level.
  • On burettes with a white tile behind them, look for the point where the bottom of the meniscus just touches its own reflection.
  • Keep the instrument vertical — a tilted burette or measuring cylinder introduces a different set of errors.
  • Allow the liquid surface to settle fully before reading — especially after transferring liquid, drainage lines can take several seconds to equilibrate.

Measuring cylinder (graduated cylinder)

The measuring cylinder is the most commonly used volumetric device in routine laboratory work. It provides reasonable accuracy (typically ±1% of its stated volume) and is suitable for approximate volume measurements that do not require the precision of a volumetric flask or burette.

Reading technique

Step-by-step — reading a measuring cylinder
1Place the cylinder on a flat, level surface — do not hold it in your hand while reading, as slight tilting introduces error.
2Crouch or adjust your position so that your eye is exactly level with the bottom of the meniscus.
3Identify the graduation marks immediately above and below the meniscus bottom to determine the scale interval.
4Read the volume at the lowest point of the concave meniscus, estimating to one decimal place beyond the smallest graduation mark.
5Record the reading with the appropriate unit and uncertainty (e.g. 47.5 ± 0.5 mL for a 50 mL cylinder).
Cylinder capacityGraduation intervalReading uncertaintyBest used for volumes
10 mL0.1 mL±0.05–0.1 mL5–10 mL
25 mL0.5 mL±0.25 mL10–25 mL
50 mL1.0 mL±0.5 mL25–50 mL
100 mL1.0 mL±0.5–1.0 mL50–100 mL
250 mL2.0 mL±1.0–2.0 mL100–250 mL
Use the smallest appropriate cylinder

Always use the smallest measuring cylinder that comfortably holds the volume you need to measure. Measuring 8 mL in a 100 mL cylinder introduces far more error than measuring 8 mL in a 10 mL cylinder, because the graduation intervals are much larger relative to the volume being measured.

Burette

The burette is a precision volumetric device used primarily in titrations to deliver precise, variable volumes of a solution. A standard 50 mL burette is graduated in 0.1 mL divisions and can be read to ±0.01–0.02 mL by interpolation.

Key features of burette reading

Burettes are numbered from top to bottom — 0 mL is at the top and 50 mL is at the bottom. This is the opposite of a measuring cylinder and catches many beginners off guard. This means that as you dispense liquid, the reading increases.

Step-by-step — reading a burette
1Ensure the burette is vertical in its clamp — a tilted burette produces inaccurate readings and inconsistent flow.
2Wait at least 30 seconds after dispensing liquid before reading — drainage from the burette walls takes time, and reading too quickly gives a falsely low volume dispensed.
3Position your eye level with the bottom of the meniscus to eliminate parallax.
4Read the bottom of the concave meniscus. A white tile or piece of white card held behind the burette makes the meniscus easier to see. Some burettes have a blue or black stripe on the back to create a shadow at the meniscus bottom.
5Estimate to the nearest 0.01 mL by interpolating between the 0.1 mL graduation marks.
6Record both initial and final readings. Volume dispensed = final reading − initial reading.
⚠ Check the tip for bubbles before every titration

An air bubble trapped in the tip of the burette below the stopcock will be expelled during the titration, appearing as extra volume dispensed. This is one of the most common sources of large titration errors. Before recording the initial reading, open the stopcock fully for a moment to flush any bubble from the tip. Check that the meniscus drops smoothly — a sudden jump indicates a bubble was expelled.

Volumetric pipette

A volumetric (or bulb) pipette is calibrated to deliver one specific, precise volume — most commonly 1, 2, 5, 10, 20, or 25 mL. It has a single calibration mark on the narrow neck above the bulb. When used correctly, it is the most accurate way to transfer a fixed volume.

Reading the fill mark

Filling a volumetric pipette to the mark
1Attach a pipette filler (bulb, Pi-pump, or electronic controller) — never mouth-pipette.
2Draw liquid up slowly until it is a few centimetres above the calibration mark.
3Wipe the outside of the tip with a lint-free tissue — but do not touch the tip opening, which would draw liquid out by capillary action.
4Hold the pipette vertical, at eye level with the calibration mark. Slowly release pressure so the meniscus descends toward the mark.
5Stop releasing when the bottom of the meniscus is exactly tangent to the top of the calibration mark line. The line should bisect the narrowest part of the meniscus as seen from eye level.
6Transfer to the receiving vessel and allow free drainage. A small amount of liquid will remain in the tip — do not blow this out unless the pipette is labelled "TD Blow-out" (indicated by two frosted rings at the top). Standard "To Deliver" (TD) pipettes are calibrated with this residual volume accounted for.

Volumetric flask

The volumetric flask is used to prepare solutions of exact concentration. Its single graduation mark on the narrow neck indicates the precise volume at which the flask is calibrated (usually at 20°C).

Making up to the mark

Dissolve the solute in less than the final volume of solvent in the flask, then add solvent carefully until you are within 1–2 cm of the mark. Switch to a Pasteur pipette or wash bottle for the final addition, dropping solvent in one drop at a time. When the bottom of the meniscus sits exactly on the mark as seen from eye level, the volume is correct. Stopper, invert, and mix thoroughly.

⚠ Never add solute to a flask already at the mark

The solute volume must be dissolved in a smaller volume first, then the total brought to the mark. Adding solute to a flask already containing the full volume of solvent produces a final volume greater than the flask's rated capacity, giving a concentration lower than intended. This is a common and entirely preventable error.

Practical tips for difficult meniscus readings

ProblemSolution
Meniscus not clearly visible in a buretteHold a white card or white ceramic tile behind the burette. Some burettes have a factory-printed blue stripe that creates a shadow exactly at the meniscus bottom.
Coloured solution — meniscus bottom not visibleRead the top of the meniscus consistently for all readings in that titration. As long as you apply the same method to initial and final readings, the volume dispensed is still accurate.
Liquid clings to the inside of a burette after dispensingWait 30 seconds for drainage. If persistent, the burette may be contaminated — clean with detergent and check the stopcock for grease contamination.
Meniscus shifts when stopcock is turnedA poorly lubricated or improperly seated stopcock causes vibration. Clean and re-grease the stopcock; ensure it is turned smoothly rather than jerked.
Reading varies between attemptsYou may be moving your head between attempts, introducing inconsistent parallax. Fix your eye position first, then read — do not find the meniscus first and then move your eye to it.
Key Takeaways

What to remember from this tutorial

  • For water and all aqueous solutions, always read the bottom of the concave meniscus. Mercury and a few non-polar liquids form a convex meniscus — read the top.
  • Parallax error is eliminated by positioning your eye exactly level with the bottom of the meniscus before reading.
  • Burettes are numbered top-to-bottom (0 at the top). Always subtract initial from final reading to get the volume dispensed.
  • Always check for and remove air bubbles from the burette tip before recording the initial reading.
  • Standard volumetric pipettes are "To Deliver" (TD) — do not blow out the residual drop; it is accounted for in the calibration.
  • When making up to the mark in a volumetric flask, add the last portion dropwise and check from eye level to avoid overshooting.
  • For coloured solutions in a burette, read the top of the meniscus consistently for both initial and final readings.
