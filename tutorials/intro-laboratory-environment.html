Introduction to the Laboratory Environment

Laboratory layout and zones

Every laboratory is intentionally organised to separate incompatible activities, minimise contamination, and ensure rapid access to safety equipment. Before you handle any chemical or biological material, you must understand the spatial logic of the room you are working in.

Most teaching and research laboratories are divided into several functional zones. Work zones are the bench areas where active experiments occur. Storage zones hold chemicals, consumables, and equipment when not in use. Clean zones — such as computer stations and write-up benches — must be kept free of reagents or contaminated gloves at all times.

Zone Typical Location Key Rules
Active bench area Central or wall-mounted benches PPE required at all times; no food or drink
Fume cupboard zone Along exterior walls (ventilated) Use for volatile chemicals; sash at working height
Biological safety cabinet Dedicated BSC room or alcove Microbiological work only; HEPA-filtered airflow
Chemical storage Locked cabinets; flammables store Segregated by compatibility; never in fume hood
Clean / write-up area Near entrance or separate alcove No reagents; remove gloves before entering
Waste station Clearly marked area near benches Segregate chemical, biological, sharps, general
Orientation tip
On your first entry to any laboratory, locate the nearest fire exit, eye wash station, emergency shower, fire extinguisher, and first aid kit before you begin any work. This takes less than two minutes and could save your life or someone else's.

Personal protective equipment (PPE)

PPE is your last line of defence after engineering controls (fume hoods, ventilation) and administrative controls (procedures, training). Selecting the correct PPE for a task is as important as wearing it correctly.

Minimum PPE for general laboratory work

Regardless of the specific task, the following are required in most laboratory environments:

Minimum PPE checklist
  • Lab coat — fully fastened, worn at all times in the work zone
  • Safety spectacles or chemical splash goggles (task-dependent)
  • Closed-toe, closed-heel shoes — no sandals, canvas, or mesh footwear
  • Nitrile gloves — check for pinholes before use; change regularly
  • Hair tied back if it reaches shoulder length or longer
  • No loose jewellery, dangling sleeves, or scarves near equipment

Selecting the correct glove

Not all gloves offer equivalent chemical protection. Always consult the Safety Data Sheet (SDS) for the chemicals in use before selecting glove material.

Glove Material Good Protection Against Poor Protection Against
Nitrile (standard) Dilute acids, alkalis, many solvents, biological material Concentrated ketones, some chlorinated solvents
Latex Biological material, aqueous solutions Many organic solvents; latex allergy risk
Neoprene Acids, bases, alcohols, fuels Halogenated solvents at high concentrations
Butyl rubber Peroxides, strong acids, ketones, esters Hydrocarbons, chlorinated compounds
Cut-resistant Sharps, glassware handling Chemical exposure — no chemical resistance
⚠ Double-gloving does not double protection
Wearing two pairs of standard nitrile gloves does not provide double the chemical breakthrough time. If a task requires enhanced protection, use a thicker or chemically appropriate glove, not two thin ones. Double-gloving reduces dexterity and tactile feedback, increasing the risk of accidents.

Understanding hazard symbols

The Globally Harmonised System (GHS) of Classification and Labelling of Chemicals uses standardised pictograms on chemical labels and Safety Data Sheets. You must recognise these symbols before handling any reagent.

GHS Pictogram Name Hazard Category Examples
Flame Flammable gases, liquids, solids, self-reactive substances Ethanol, acetone, methanol
Exclamation mark Irritant, harmful if swallowed/inhaled, narcotic effects Sodium hydroxide (dilute), isopropanol
Skull and crossbones Acute toxicity (fatal or toxic) Methanol, chloroform, cyanide salts
Corrosion Skin corrosion, eye damage, metal corrosion Concentrated H₂SO₄, NaOH, HCl
Health hazard (silhouette) Carcinogen, mutagen, reproductive toxin, sensitiser Formaldehyde, benzene, acrylamide
Environment Aquatic toxicity — do not drain to sink Many organic solvents, heavy metal salts
Oxidiser (flame over circle) May intensify fire; oxidising agents Hydrogen peroxide (>50%), potassium permanganate
Exploding bomb Explosive, self-reactive, organic peroxide Picric acid, dry azides, dibenzoyl peroxide
Gas cylinder Compressed or liquefied gases CO₂, nitrogen, propane cylinders
Safety Data Sheet (SDS) — always read Section 8
Every chemical in a laboratory must have an accessible SDS. Section 8 (Exposure Controls / Personal Protection) specifies the exact PPE required. Section 6 covers accidental release measures. Locate your lab's SDS binder or digital access point on day one.

Common equipment overview

Laboratories house a wide variety of general-purpose instruments. This section introduces equipment found in most beginner-level settings. Detailed tutorials for each instrument are available in the Level 1 series.

Equipment Primary Use Key Caution
Analytical balance Accurate mass measurement (0.1 mg resolution) Level surface; close draft shield; never exceed max capacity
Micropipette Transferring 0.5–1,000 µL volumes Use correct tips; never rotate volume past max
Vortex mixer Rapid mixing of small tubes Cap tubes securely; hold firmly or use touch mode
Water bath Controlled temperature incubation Check water level; use anti-fungal tablets for long-term use
Magnetic stirrer / hot plate Stirring and/or heating solutions Always use hot hand indicator; never heat sealed containers
pH meter Measuring solution acidity or alkalinity Calibrate before use; store electrode in KCl solution
Centrifuge Separating components by density Balance opposing tubes; never open lid during spin
Autoclave Sterilisation by pressurised steam Training required; check door seal; use heat-indicator tape

Safety systems and their locations

Every laboratory is required to provide a minimum set of safety systems. Knowing their exact location is not optional — in an emergency, you will not have time to search.

Safety systems to locate on day one
  1. 1Eye wash station: Should be reachable within 10 seconds from any point in the lab. Flush for a minimum of 15 minutes after chemical eye contact.
  2. 2Emergency shower (drench shower): Used for large-area chemical skin exposure. Remove contaminated clothing while under flow. Flush for at least 15–20 minutes.
  3. 3Fire extinguishers: Identify type — CO₂ for electrical/chemical fires; dry powder for general use. Know the PASS acronym: Pull, Aim, Squeeze, Sweep.
  4. 4Fire blanket: For smothering small fires on clothing or bench surfaces. Never wrap a panicking person — they may fan the flames.
  5. 5First aid kit: For minor cuts and burns. Report all first aid events to a supervisor; complete an incident report form.
  6. 6Spill kits: Chemical and biological spill kits are separate. Do not use a chemical spill kit on a biological spill, and vice versa.
  7. 7Emergency stop / isolation points: Electrical isolators and gas taps. Know where the main gas shut-off is for your bench.

Conduct and safe work practices

Laboratory accidents rarely result from a single catastrophic decision. Most arise from a chain of small lapses in safe working habits — rushing, skipping risk assessments, or assuming familiarity. The following practices eliminate the most common risk factors.

General conduct

  • Never work alone in a laboratory, especially with hazardous chemicals or live electrical equipment.
  • Inform a colleague or supervisor of your intended work before starting.
  • No food, drink, application of cosmetics, or contact lens handling inside the laboratory.
  • Keep work areas uncluttered — a clear bench reduces the chance of knocking over containers.
  • Never mouth-pipette. Always use a pipette aid or micropipette.
  • Walk, do not run. Carry glassware with two hands at or below chest height.

Before starting any experiment

Pre-experiment checklist
  • Complete or review the relevant Risk Assessment for the task
  • Read the SDS for all chemicals to be used
  • Check all glassware for chips, cracks, or star fractures — discard damaged items
  • Confirm fume hood is functioning (sash moves freely; airflow indicator active)
  • Ensure sufficient waste containers are available and correctly labelled
  • Notify a colleague of your planned work and expected finish time
⚠ Never assume a container is safe because it looks familiar
Unlabelled containers must be treated as unknown hazards. Do not smell, taste, or assume the contents based on appearance. If you find an unlabelled container, isolate it and inform your supervisor. This is a legal requirement in most jurisdictions.

Waste disposal basics

Improper laboratory waste disposal is a major source of environmental damage, regulatory fines, and injury to waste-handling personnel. Every laboratory generates several categories of waste that must be segregated from the point of generation — not sorted afterwards.

Waste Category Typical Container Examples Never Mix With
Aqueous chemical waste Labelled carboy or jug Buffer solutions, dilute acids/bases Organic solvents, halogenated waste
Halogenated solvent waste Separate sealed container Chloroform, DCM, chlorobenzene Non-halogenated solvents
Non-halogenated solvent waste Separate sealed container Ethanol, acetone, ethyl acetate Halogenated solvents
Sharps Rigid puncture-proof sharps bin Needles, scalpel blades, broken glass Never in general waste bags
Biological (contaminated) Autoclave bags (yellow/orange) Microbial cultures, contaminated plasticware Chemical waste; sharps
General non-hazardous Standard bin Clean paper, uncontaminated packaging Any chemical or biological material
Labelling waste containers
Every waste container must be labelled with: (1) the hazard type, (2) the date started, (3) a list of constituents, and (4) the responsible person's name. A waste container with no label will be treated as an unknown hazard — significantly increasing disposal cost and risk.

Documentation and labelling

Good laboratory documentation is not a bureaucratic burden — it is the foundation of reproducible science and safe practice. All containers, samples, and solutions prepared in the laboratory must be labelled immediately upon preparation.

Minimum label information for a prepared solution

SODIUM PHOSPHATE BUFFER — 50 mM, pH 7.4 ───────────────────────────────────────── Prepared by : J. Adebayo Date prepared: 14 Apr 2025 Expiry / use by: 14 May 2025 Storage: 4°C | Hazard: Low (irritant) ───────────────────────────────────────── If found unlabelled → do NOT use → inform supervisor

Your laboratory notebook (paper or electronic) should contain, for every experiment: the date, objective, materials and their lot numbers, exact procedure followed, raw observations, and any deviations from protocol. Write in permanent ink if using paper; never erase — cross out errors with a single line.

Electronic lab notebooks (ELN)
Many institutions now require or encourage ELNs. Regardless of format, timestamps are automatically recorded in ELNs and entries should not be deleted — only amended with a note explaining the correction. Check your institution's data retention policy, which is typically 5–10 years for research data.

Emergency procedures

Know your institution's emergency procedures before you begin any laboratory work. The following summarises the most common emergencies in a beginner laboratory context.

⚠ In any life-threatening emergency: call emergency services first
In Nigeria, call 199 (NEMA) or 112 (general emergency). Your institution will also have an internal emergency number — memorise it or keep it visible at your bench. Do not delay calling for help to attempt containment of a serious incident.
Emergency Immediate Action Do Not
Chemical splash to eyes Eye wash station immediately; 15 min flush; seek medical aid Rub eyes; delay flushing to find help first
Chemical skin contact (large area) Emergency shower; remove clothing; flush ≥15 min Leave the shower early; re-apply contaminated clothing
Fire on bench (small) Extinguish with CO₂ if safe; raise alarm if unsure Use water on chemical fires; fight a growing fire
Fire alarm / building evacuation Secure volatile materials; leave immediately via nearest exit Use lift; return for belongings; re-enter without clearance
Chemical spill (minor) Alert others; use appropriate spill kit; dispose correctly Use paper towels for solvent spills; ignore volatile spills
Broken glass / sharps injury Apply pressure; wash wound; seek first aid; report incident Pick up broken glass with bare hands; downplay the injury
Gas leak (smell of gas) Do not operate switches; evacuate; call facilities/emergency Turn lights on or off; use mobile phones near the leak
Key takeaways

What to remember from this tutorial

  • Locate all safety systems — eye wash, emergency shower, fire extinguisher, first aid kit — before starting any work in a new laboratory.
  • PPE is mandatory in the work zone at all times. Select gloves based on the chemical's SDS, not by habit or appearance.
  • GHS hazard pictograms must be understood and observed. Read the SDS for every new chemical before first use, especially Sections 6, 7, and 8.
  • Waste must be segregated at the point of generation: aqueous, halogenated, non-halogenated, sharps, biological, and general waste are never mixed.
  • Label every container immediately upon preparation — including the date, contents, concentration, and your name.
  • Never work alone in a laboratory. Inform a colleague of your planned activity and expected duration.
  • In an emergency, call for help first — your own safety takes priority over saving reagents or equipment.
