Personal Protective Equipment (PPE)

The hierarchy of controls

PPE is the last line of defence in the hierarchy of controls — a framework that ranks protective measures from most to least effective. Before reaching for PPE, higher-order controls should always be applied first.

LevelControl typeExample
1 (most effective)EliminationRemove the hazardous chemical from the process entirely
2SubstitutionReplace with a less hazardous chemical
3Engineering controlsFume hood, biosafety cabinet, ventilated enclosure
4Administrative controlsSafe operating procedures, training, job rotation
5 (least effective alone)PPEGloves, goggles, lab coat, respirator

PPE does not eliminate the hazard — it only reduces exposure. A fume hood protecting you from solvent vapours is more reliable than a respirator, because it acts on the source rather than the receiver. Always use engineering and administrative controls first, with PPE as supplementary protection.

Laboratory coat

The lab coat protects your clothing and skin from chemical splashes, biological spills, and minor heat exposure. Crucially, it is also a containment garment — it prevents contamination from your street clothes entering the laboratory.

Lab coat requirements
  • Must be fully fastened at all times in the work zone — not left open or worn off the shoulders.
  • Material must match the work: cotton or polycotton for general chemistry; flame-resistant fabric (e.g. Nomex) for fire risk environments; fluid-resistant for biological work.
  • Must cover the arms to the wrist. Roll-up sleeves defeat the purpose.
  • Must not be worn outside the laboratory — remove before leaving, especially before using toilets or eating areas.
  • Store contaminated coats in a designated bag, not hung alongside clean coats or personal clothing.
  • Inspect regularly for damage; a coat with chemical burns, holes, or severe staining should be replaced.
⚠ Synthetic fabrics melt

Polyester and nylon lab coats can melt onto skin in the event of fire, causing severe burns far worse than the flame itself would cause. For any work involving open flames, flammable solvents, or heat, use a cotton or inherently flame-resistant coat. Check the label — many cheap lab coats are polyester.

Eye and face protection

Eye injuries are among the most serious laboratory injuries because the consequences can be permanent and irreversible. The eye is not protected by natural barriers like skin — a single drop of concentrated acid can cause permanent vision loss within seconds.

TypeProtection offeredWhen to use
Safety spectaclesFrontal impact and splash; limited side protectionLow-risk general bench work; not a substitute for goggles with chemical hazards
Chemical splash gogglesFull seal around the eye; prevents splash from any angleAny work with liquid chemicals, especially acids, bases, or organic solvents
Face shieldFull face and neck protection; worn over goggles, not instead ofLarge volume transfers, sonication, pressurised systems, or risk of violent reaction
UV-protective gogglesBlocks UV radiationUV transilluminator use (gel imaging), UV sterilisation lamps
Contact lenses in the laboratory

Contact lenses are not a substitute for eye protection and introduce additional risk — chemical vapours can be absorbed by the lens and held against the cornea, worsening exposure. If you wear contact lenses, always wear chemical splash goggles (not just spectacles) and inform your supervisor. Many institutions require you to wear glasses in high-vapour environments.

Gloves

Gloves protect against chemical absorption through the skin, biological contamination, and minor thermal hazards. Selecting the wrong glove material can provide false security — some chemicals permeate nitrile within minutes.

Glove material selection

MaterialProtects well againstPoor resistance toTypical use
Nitrile (standard)Dilute acids/bases, biological material, many solventsConcentrated ketones, some chlorinated solventsGeneral laboratory work
LatexBiological material, aqueous solutionsOrganic solvents; allergy riskBiological work (where no allergy)
NeopreneAcids, bases, alcohols, fuelsHalogenated solvents at high concentrationChemical synthesis, acid handling
Butyl rubberPeroxides, strong acids, ketones, estersHydrocarbons, chlorinated compoundsHigh-hazard chemical work
Cut-resistant (HPPE/Kevlar)Cuts and lacerationsNo chemical resistanceGlassware assembly, sharp instruments
Cryogenic glovesExtreme cold (liquid nitrogen, dry ice)Chemical exposureCryogenic liquid handling

Correct glove use

Glove discipline
  • Check each glove for pinholes before use by inflating it or holding it up to a light source.
  • Change gloves regularly — do not wear the same pair all day.
  • Remove gloves before touching phones, door handles, computer keyboards, or your face.
  • Double-gloving does not double breakthrough time — use a thicker or more appropriate material if enhanced protection is needed.
  • Dispose of used gloves in appropriate waste — never inside-out over your bare hand (use the "beak" technique).
  • Always consult the chemical's SDS Section 8 for the manufacturer's recommended glove type.

Footwear

Footwear is a frequently overlooked element of PPE. Chemical spills fall downward — if your feet are not protected, they receive the concentrated impact of any bench-level spill.

Required: Closed-toe, closed-heel shoes that fully cover the top of the foot. Leather or synthetic leather offers better chemical resistance than fabric.

Prohibited: Sandals, flip-flops, open-toed shoes, stilettos (unstable), canvas shoes (absorb chemicals), mesh trainers (no chemical barrier). Trousers must cover the ankle — shorts and skirts expose significant skin area to spill risk.

Respiratory protection

Respiratory hazards in a laboratory include vapours, aerosols, dusts, and biological particles. Most routine chemical work is conducted in a fume hood — which is an engineering control that eliminates the need for a respirator if used correctly. Respirators are required when fume hood use is not feasible or when working in environments with airborne hazards.

Respirator typeFiltersUse case
Disposable particulate (N95/FFP2)Particulates, aerosols, dustsWeighing dusty powders; low-risk biological aerosols
Half-face with cartridgeChemical vapours, particulates (cartridge-dependent)Work with volatile solvents outside a fume hood
Full-face with cartridgeChemical vapours + eye protectionHigh-concentration solvent work; combined eye + respiratory hazard
Powered air-purifying (PAPR)Biological agents, high-hazard aerosolsBSL-3 work; highly infectious agents
Fit testing is mandatory for reusable respirators

A respirator that does not seal to your face provides little or no protection. Reusable respirators (half-face and full-face) require formal fit testing before first use. Fit testing is conducted by an occupational health or safety officer and must be repeated if your face shape changes (weight change, facial surgery) or if you change respirator model.

Donning and doffing correctly

How you put on (don) and remove (doff) PPE is as important as wearing it. Incorrect doffing — particularly of contaminated gloves — is a common route of self-contamination.

Correct glove removal sequence (beak technique)
1Pinch the outside of one glove at the wrist with your other gloved hand — do not touch your bare skin.
2Peel the glove off inside-out, holding it in your still-gloved hand.
3Slide two fingers from the bare hand inside the wrist of the remaining glove — do not touch the outside surface.
4Peel the second glove off inside-out, enclosing the first glove inside it.
5Dispose of the glove bundle in the appropriate waste container. Wash hands immediately.

Limitations of PPE

Understanding the limitations of PPE prevents the false sense of security that sometimes accompanies it. PPE:

  • Does not eliminate the hazard — it only reduces exposure while the hazard remains present.
  • Has a finite service life — gloves have measured breakthrough times; respirator cartridges have saturation limits.
  • Fails when damaged, worn incorrectly, or used beyond its rated capacity.
  • Can itself become a contamination vector — a contaminated glove touching a clean surface spreads the hazard.
  • Does not substitute for proper risk assessment and engineering controls.
Key Takeaways

What to remember from this tutorial

  • PPE is the last line of defence — engineering controls (fume hoods, ventilation) must be applied first.
  • Lab coat must be fully fastened, cotton or flame-resistant material, and never worn outside the lab.
  • Chemical splash goggles — not spectacles — are required for work with liquid chemicals.
  • Glove material selection must be based on the SDS for each chemical — nitrile is not universally appropriate.
  • Remove gloves before touching phones, keyboards, or your face to prevent spread of contamination.
  • Closed-toe, closed-heel shoes are mandatory — no sandals, canvas, or mesh footwear.
  • Reusable respirators require formal fit testing before first use and at regular intervals.
  • PPE has a finite service life and must be inspected, replaced, and removed correctly to be effective.
