What is HPLC?

High-Performance Liquid Chromatography (HPLC) is a separation technique that uses a pressurised liquid mobile phase to push a sample mixture through a column packed with stationary phase material. Different components in the mixture interact differently with the stationary phase, so they travel through the column at different speeds and emerge — elute — at different times, where a detector records their presence.

HPLC is the workhorse of analytical laboratories in pharmaceutical quality control, food analysis, environmental monitoring, clinical biochemistry, and academic research. It can separate, identify, and quantify almost any molecule that can be dissolved in a liquid.

Separation principle

Chromatographic separation depends on the differential partitioning of analytes between the mobile phase (liquid) and the stationary phase (column packing). An analyte that interacts strongly with the stationary phase spends more time retained on the column and elutes later. One that prefers the mobile phase moves quickly through and elutes early.

The selectivity of the separation — how well peaks are separated — is tuned by adjusting: the stationary phase chemistry, the mobile phase composition, the gradient profile, temperature, and flow rate.

Reading a chromatogram

A chromatogram is a plot of detector signal (y-axis) vs. time (x-axis). Each peak represents a component of the sample. The key information you extract from a chromatogram:

Retention time

The retention time (tR) is the time from injection to the apex of a peak. It is characteristic of a compound under fixed conditions (column, mobile phase, temperature, flow rate). It is used for peak identification, but always confirm identity with a reference standard injected under the same conditions.

Retention factor (k)
k = (tR − t₀) / t₀
t₀ = dead time (time for unretained compound to elute). Optimal k range: 2–10

If k < 1, the analyte elutes too quickly to be reliably separated. If k > 20, run times are impractically long. Adjust mobile phase strength to bring k into the 2–10 range.

Resolution and peak shape

Resolution (Rs) measures how well two adjacent peaks are separated:

Resolution
Rs = 2(tR2 − tR1) / (w1 + w2)
w = peak width at base; Rs ≥ 1.5 = baseline separation; Rs = 1.0 = about 4% overlap

Peak symmetry (tailing factor, T) indicates column condition and sample-stationary phase interaction quality. T = 1.0 is perfect Gaussian peak shape. T > 2.0 indicates tailing, which can result from secondary interactions, column overloading, or degraded column. T < 0.9 (fronting) usually means column overloading.

Column chemistry

Reversed-phase HPLC (RP-HPLC)

Reversed-phase HPLC is the dominant mode — accounting for roughly 70–80% of all HPLC separations. The stationary phase is non-polar (typically C18, C8, or phenyl), and the mobile phase is polar (water + organic solvent). More hydrophobic (non-polar) analytes are retained longer.

Stationary phaseHydrophobicityBest for
C18 (ODS)HighGeneral purpose — most popular; good for moderately to highly hydrophobic molecules
C8ModerateSlightly less retentive; useful when C18 retains analytes too strongly
PhenylModerate, with π–πAromatics, phenolic compounds; different selectivity to C18
C4 / C2LowLarge biomolecules (proteins, peptides)
HILICPolar (reverse of RP)Very polar/hydrophilic compounds that don't retain on C18

Normal-phase HPLC

Normal-phase uses a polar stationary phase (silica, amino, cyano) and a non-polar mobile phase (hexane, dichloromethane). More polar analytes are retained longer. Used mainly for non-polar compounds (lipids, fat-soluble vitamins), isomer separations, and chiral separations.

Ion-exchange HPLC

The stationary phase carries ionic functional groups that attract oppositely charged analyte ions. Used for amino acids, nucleotides, sugars, inorganic ions, and proteins. Mobile phase contains a buffer that competes with analyte ions for binding sites; increasing salt concentration elutes analytes.

Mobile phase selection

For reversed-phase HPLC, the mobile phase is a mixture of water and an organic modifier:

Organic modifierEluting strengthUV cutoffNotes
Methanol (MeOH)Moderate210 nmMost common; inexpensive; slightly high viscosity when mixed with water
Acetonitrile (ACN)Strong190 nmLower UV cutoff than MeOH — better for UV detection below 220 nm
Tetrahydrofuran (THF)Strong212 nmDifferent selectivity; compatible with most columns; can swell some seals
Isopropanol (IPA)Very strong205 nmGood for fatty acids, phospholipids; high viscosity — use at low %

Mobile phase modifiers for ion suppression or retention of ionisable compounds: add 0.1% trifluoroacetic acid (TFA) or 0.1% formic acid for basic analytes; add triethylamine (TEA) or ammonium acetate buffer for acidic analytes. For mass spectrometry (LC-MS), avoid non-volatile buffers like phosphate — use ammonium formate or ammonium acetate instead.

Mobile phase preparation

Always use HPLC-grade solvents. Degas the mobile phase (vacuum filtration through 0.45 µm membrane, or sonication) to prevent bubble formation in the pump and detector. Filter samples through 0.22 µm syringe filter before injection to prevent column contamination.

Isocratic vs gradient elution

In isocratic elution, the mobile phase composition is held constant throughout the run. This is simpler and more reproducible, but may not resolve samples with a wide range of polarity.

In gradient elution, the proportion of organic modifier is increased over time during the run. This progressively elutes more retained (hydrophobic) compounds, compresses late-eluting peaks, and allows complex mixtures to be analysed in a reasonable time.

Example gradient profile for a small molecule mixture
1
0–2 min: 5% ACN / 95% water — equilibration, flush void volume
2
2–12 min: 5% → 95% ACN linear ramp — elute compounds in order of hydrophobicity
3
12–14 min: 95% ACN — wash column of any strongly retained matrix components
4
14–20 min: 95% → 5% ACN re-equilibration — restore initial conditions before next injection
Always allow adequate re-equilibration time (typically 10 column volumes) before the next injection.

Method development workflow

A systematic approach reduces development time significantly. Follow this order:

Systematic HPLC method development
1
Define the goal: Separation of known analytes? Unknown profiling? Quantitative assay? Trace analysis? This drives every other choice.
2
Characterise the analytes: Log P (hydrophobicity), pKa, molecular weight, UV response, stability. Use literature values or calculate with ChemDraw.
3
Select column type: Start with C18 unless analytes are very polar (HILIC) or the mechanism is clearly ion-exchange.
4
Scouting gradient: Run a broad gradient (5–95% ACN over 20 min) to map where all peaks elute. This reveals retention window and rough selectivity.
5
Optimise selectivity: Adjust organic modifier type, pH, or column phase to improve resolution of critical pairs.
6
Compress and finalise gradient: Narrow the gradient range to the elution window of your analytes; adjust slope for adequate resolution with acceptable run time.
7
Validate: Demonstrate linearity, precision, accuracy, LOD/LOQ, and robustness per ICH Q2(R1) if for pharmaceutical use.
Document all method parameters precisely — column lot, mobile phase composition to 3 significant figures, flow rate, temperature, and injection volume.

Key takeaways

What to remember from this tutorial

  • HPLC separates compounds based on differential partitioning between mobile phase and stationary phase.
  • Start method development with a C18 column and a scouting gradient (5–95% ACN); then optimise from there.
  • Retention factor k = (tR − t₀) / t₀. Optimal k range is 2–10.
  • Resolution Rs ≥ 1.5 is required for baseline separation in quantitative work.
  • Reversed-phase (C18, C8) is the most common mode — non-polar stationary phase, polar mobile phase.
  • Use ACN rather than methanol for better UV transparency below 220 nm; avoid non-volatile buffers for LC-MS.
  • Always filter samples through 0.22 µm before injection; use HPLC-grade solvents and degas the mobile phase.
  • Document every method parameter exactly — small changes in column lot or mobile phase pH can dramatically affect retention.
