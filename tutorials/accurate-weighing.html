Accurate Weighing with a Laboratory Balance

Introduction

Every quantitative laboratory result ultimately traces back to a mass measurement. An incorrectly weighed reagent produces an incorrectly prepared solution, which produces incorrect results in every subsequent analysis. Despite being the most fundamental operation in the laboratory, weighing is also among the most frequently performed incorrectly — usually because of simple procedural oversights rather than equipment failure.

This tutorial covers the correct technique for using both analytical and top-loading balances, the sources of weighing error, and how to avoid them.

Types of balance

TypeReadabilityTypical capacityUse
Analytical balance0.1 mg (4 decimal places)Up to 200 gAccurate weighing of small masses; primary standards; micro-quantities
Semi-micro balance0.01 mg (5 decimal places)Up to 50 gVery precise weighing; trace analysis
Top-loading (precision) balance0.01–0.1 g (2–3 decimal places)Up to 6 kgLarger masses where high precision is not critical; bulk reagent preparation

Use the most appropriate balance for your purpose — an analytical balance for solution preparation and primary standards, a top-loading balance for larger quantities where the extra decimal places are not needed. Using a top-loading balance where an analytical balance is required introduces systematic error.

Before you weigh

Levelling the balance

All balances have a spirit level bubble (usually visible through a small window on the front or rear). The bubble must be centred within the circle before any weighing is done. Most balances have adjustable feet — turn them until the bubble is centred. A balance that is not level will give systematically incorrect readings because gravity acts at an angle to the weighing mechanism.

Warm-up time

Analytical balances must warm up before use. Most manufacturers specify 30 minutes; some instruments require up to 2 hours after being powered on from cold. Check your balance's manual. Weighing on a cold balance gives unstable readings because the electronic components have not reached thermal equilibrium.

Calibration

Calibrate the balance at the start of each working day using certified reference weights. Most modern balances have an internal calibration function (press the CAL button); some require external weights. Record the calibration check in the balance logbook — mass, expected value, observed value, and pass/fail. If the balance fails calibration, tag it out of service and notify the laboratory supervisor.

Calibration vs verification

Internal calibration adjusts the balance's internal reference. External verification checks whether the balance reads correctly using independently certified weights. Both should be done regularly — internal calibration daily, external verification as specified by your quality system (typically monthly or quarterly).

Weighing procedure

Taring the container

Correct taring procedure
1
Ensure the balance pan is clean and the display reads zero with nothing on it. If not, press the Zero or Tare button with the pan empty.
2
Close the draught shield (analytical balance). Place the empty weighing container (boat, flask, or paper) centrally on the pan.
3
Wait for the display to stabilise, then press Tare. The display should return to 0.0000 g. The balance has subtracted the container weight and will now display only the mass of material you add.
4
Do not move or lean on the bench while weighing — vibration affects the reading.

Adding the sample

  • Add solid material slowly using a clean, dry spatula — never a wet or contaminated one
  • Add material to the centre of the weighing boat — asymmetric loading can affect accuracy on some balances
  • Close the draught shield before taking the reading
  • Allow the display to stabilise fully before recording — the reading should not be fluctuating
  • If you overshoot the target mass, remove a small amount and re-stabilise — do not add more to compensate first

Recording the mass

Always record the exact mass displayed, not the target mass you were aiming for. In accurate work, you use the actual mass to calculate the actual concentration. For primary standard preparation specifically: weigh out approximately the target amount, record what you actually weighed, and calculate the exact concentration from that mass. This is correct analytical practice.

Weighing hygroscopic and volatile materials

Hygroscopic materials absorb moisture from the air. Examples include NaOH, CaCl₂, P₂O₅, many primary standards, and most dried reagents. They must be weighed quickly after removal from the desiccator, and the container should be capped between additions. The longer a hygroscopic material is exposed to air, the more its mass increases — giving a falsely high reading and a less concentrated solution than intended.

Volatile materials (ethanol, acetone, some organic solvents) evaporate during weighing, giving a mass that decreases over time. Weigh these in capped or tared containers and work quickly.

Sources of weighing error

Error sourceEffectPrevention
Balance not levelSystematic error in all readingsCheck spirit level before every session; re-level if needed
Balance not warmed upUnstable, drifting readingsAllow 30–120 min warm-up after power-on
Draughts and vibrationFluctuating display; incorrect final readingClose draught shield; do not bump the bench
Electrostatic charge on containerErratic readings, especially with plastic boatsUse anti-static spray or earthed metal weighing boats; ground yourself
Temperature difference between sample and balanceConvection currents affect readingAllow hot or cold samples to equilibrate to room temperature
Moisture absorption (hygroscopic)Mass increases over time — solution too diluteWeigh quickly from desiccator; cap container between additions
Contaminated panTare includes residue; true mass of sample is less than displayedClean pan with dry cloth or brush before every session
Magnetic materials near balanceAttraction or repulsion affects readingKeep magnets, motors, stirrers away from the balance area

Balance care and maintenance

  • Clean the pan after every session — use a dry, lint-free cloth or a soft brush. Never use wet cloths that could allow moisture into the mechanism
  • Clean spills immediately — corrosive vapours from acids or bases left on the pan or in the housing cause permanent damage
  • Log every use — record the date, user, materials weighed, and calibration check result
  • Never overload the balance — check the maximum capacity and stay well below it
  • Keep the area around the balance clean and free of air currents — do not place the balance near doors, fume hoods, or air conditioning outlets
  • Report faults immediately — a drifting, noisy, or inconsistent reading is a sign of a fault, not normal variation
Key takeaways

What to remember from this tutorial

  • Level the balance and check the spirit level before every weighing session.
  • Allow at least 30 minutes warm-up after switching on an analytical balance from cold.
  • Calibrate daily using internal or external weights; record in the logbook.
  • Tare the empty container first; then add sample slowly with a clean, dry spatula.
  • Always record the exact mass displayed — never the nominal target.
  • Weigh hygroscopic materials quickly from the desiccator and cap the container between additions.
  • Draughts, vibration, temperature gradients, and electrostatic charge are all sources of weighing error.
  • Clean the pan after every session and log every use.
