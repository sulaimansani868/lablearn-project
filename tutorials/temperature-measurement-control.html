Temperature Measurement and Control

Why temperature matters in the laboratory

Temperature is one of the most important variables in experimental science. It governs reaction rates (via the Arrhenius equation), enzyme activity, protein stability, solubility, viscosity, vapour pressure, and volumetric expansion of liquids and glassware. A temperature error of just a few degrees can invalidate a kinetic experiment, denature an enzyme, or cause a reagent to precipitate out of solution.

Temperature also governs storage conditions. The stability of biological samples, reagents, standards, and cell cultures depends critically on maintaining specified temperature ranges. A malfunctioning incubator or freezer that goes undetected for a weekend can destroy months of irreplaceable samples.

Temperature scales

Three temperature scales are relevant in laboratory work. Understanding their relationships prevents conversion errors.

ScaleSymbolZero pointUsed forConversion
Celsius°CFreezing point of water (at 1 atm)All routine laboratory work°C = K − 273.15
KelvinKAbsolute zero (−273.15°C)Thermodynamic calculations, gas laws, spectroscopyK = °C + 273.15
Fahrenheit°FDefined historicallyNot used in scientific work; occasionally in clinical contexts°F = (°C × 9/5) + 32
Always use Kelvin for thermodynamic equations

Equations derived from thermodynamic principles — including the Arrhenius equation, the van't Hoff equation, the ideal gas law (PV = nRT), and Gibbs free energy (ΔG = ΔH − TΔS) — require temperature in Kelvin. Substituting Celsius values into these equations produces incorrect results. When using these equations, convert °C to K first: K = °C + 273.15.

Types of thermometer

Different thermometer types offer different ranges, response times, and accuracy levels. Selecting the appropriate type for the task avoids both safety hazards and measurement errors.

TypeRangeAccuracyBest useLimitations

Digital probe (RTD / Pt100)−200 to +650°C±0.1°CHigh-precision temperature logging, water bathsMore expensive; slower response than thermocouple
Infrared (non-contact)−50 to +1000°C±1–2°CSurface temperature of hot plates, external equipmentMeasures surface only — not internal liquid temperature; emissivity-dependent

⚠ Mercury thermometers are banned in most laboratories

Mercury thermometers are prohibited in most institutions due to the severe health and environmental hazard posed by mercury spills. If a mercury thermometer breaks, the resulting spill requires specialist hazardous waste contractors to clean up. Replace all mercury thermometers with alcohol-filled or digital alternatives. If a mercury thermometer break occurs, evacuate the immediate area, do not attempt DIY clean-up, and notify your safety officer immediately.

Thermometer calibration and verification

A thermometer that reads incorrectly introduces a systematic error into every temperature-dependent measurement made with it. Calibration should be verified before any critical temperature-sensitive experiment and at regular scheduled intervals.

Two-point verification method

Verifying a thermometer using ice-water and boiling water
1Ice point (0°C): Fill a beaker with crushed ice and add a small amount of cold distilled water to create a wet ice slush. Insert the thermometer and stir gently. After 2–3 minutes, a calibrated thermometer should read 0.0°C ± 0.1°C.
2Steam point (100°C at sea level): Bring distilled water to a full rolling boil. Suspend the thermometer in the steam (not in the liquid) and wait for stabilisation. At sea level (1 atm), the reading should be 100.0°C. Correct for altitude if applicable: approximately −0.034°C per 100 m elevation.
3Record the actual readings against the expected values. The difference is the correction factor to apply to all measurements with this thermometer. A correction of more than ±1°C warrants replacement or recalibration.

Heating devices

Multiple heating devices are available in the laboratory, each with different precision, uniformity, maximum temperature, and safety profile. Selecting the appropriate device for the temperature required and the vessel type used is critical.

DeviceTemperature rangeUniformityBest forCautions

Oil bathAmbient to ~250°C±1–2°CReactions requiring temperatures above 100°C; reflux setupsFire hazard — oil can ignite; never heat above the flash point; clamp all vessels securely
Sand bathAmbient to ~300°C±5–15°C (poor)Distillations, glassblowing support — where uniformity is not criticalPoor temperature uniformity; hot sand can cause burns; not suitable for precise temperature work
Heating mantleAmbient to ~450°C±2–5°CHeating round-bottom flasks; distillation; refluxMust match flask size; do not use without a controller; do not place on a lab jack without a secure support
Hot plate / stirrerAmbient to ~500°C (surface)±5–10°C (surface)Heating flat-bottomed vessels; simultaneous stirringSurface temperature ≠ solution temperature; always use an immersion thermometer; use hot-surface indicator disc
Dry block heaterAmbient to ~130°C±0.5°CHeating tubes at exact temperatures; enzyme reactions; PCR setupInsert tubes fully; match block to tube size; use a lid for volatile samples
Muffle furnace200–1200°C±5–10°CAshing, ignition, sinteringRequires full PPE; specialist training required; never open door suddenly at high temperature
⚠ Hot plate surface temperature is NOT the solution temperature

The set-point dial on a hot plate controls the surface temperature of the plate — not the temperature of the liquid inside the vessel. A hot plate set to 80°C may produce a solution temperature of anywhere from 40°C to 75°C depending on vessel size, stirring rate, and ambient conditions. Always use an immersion thermometer or digital probe directly in the solution to verify actual temperature.

Temperature monitoring and logging

Storage units (refrigerators, freezers, incubators) must have their temperatures monitored and recorded continuously, or at minimum twice daily. A temperature excursion that goes undetected over a weekend can silently ruin all stored samples.

Temperature monitoring requirements
  • Every storage unit should have a calibrated thermometer or electronic data logger inside, not just the built-in display (which measures air temperature at the sensor location, not the actual sample temperature).
  • Electronic data loggers record continuously and can send alarms via email or SMS when temperature goes out of range — strongly recommended for any storage unit holding irreplaceable samples.
  • Manual temperature logs should record: date, time, observed temperature, acceptable range, and the recorder's initials. Any out-of-range reading must be investigated and documented.
  • Incubators used for cell culture or microbiology should also log CO₂ percentage and humidity where applicable.
  • Temperature log records must be retained as part of the laboratory's GLP documentation — typically for at least 2 years for research and longer for regulated studies.

Key Takeaways

What to remember from this tutorial

  • Temperature affects reaction rates, enzyme activity, solubility, and sample stability — it must be measured and controlled, not assumed.
  • Use Kelvin (not Celsius) in all thermodynamic equations. Convert using: K = °C + 273.15.
  • Mercury thermometers are banned in most laboratories — use alcohol-filled or digital thermometers instead.
  • Verify thermometers using ice-point (0°C) and steam-point (100°C) checks before critical measurements.
  • The hot plate dial controls surface temperature — always measure solution temperature with an immersion probe.
  • Dry ice (−78.5°C) and liquid nitrogen (−196°C) cause severe cryogenic burns on contact — always use appropriate insulated gloves and face protection.
  • Storage unit temperatures must be monitored continuously (or twice daily at minimum) and logged — electronic data loggers with alarm functions are strongly recommended.
