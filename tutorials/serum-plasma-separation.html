Serum and Plasma Separation: Technique and Best Practices

Serum vs plasma — what is the difference?

Whole blood consists of cellular components (red blood cells, white blood cells, platelets) suspended in plasma. When blood is collected and processed, two types of cell-free liquid can be obtained depending on how clotting is handled.

PropertySerumPlasma
Clotting allowed?Yes — clot forms and is removedNo — anticoagulant prevents clotting
Contains fibrinogen?No (consumed in clot)Yes
Contains clotting factors?NoYes
Tube typeRed top (plain) or SST (gold top)EDTA (purple), citrate (blue), heparin (green)
Common usesClinical chemistry, serology, most biochemical assaysCoagulation tests, haematology, some enzyme assays
Volume yield~55% of whole blood volume~60% of whole blood volume
Figure 1 — Blood tube layers after centrifugation
Left to right: whole blood before centrifugation (uniform dark red); plain red-top after clotting and centrifugation (serum above, fibrin clot and red cells below); SST gold-top with gel separator (gel barrier physically separates serum from clot, preventing cellular contamination); EDTA purple-top anticoagulant tube (plasma above, thin buffy coat of white blood cells and platelets in the middle, packed red cells at the bottom). The teal arrows indicate the harvest point for serum or plasma. Plasma yields approximately 5% more volume than serum because no proteins are consumed in clot formation.

Order of draw

When collecting multiple tubes, follow the correct order of draw to prevent additive carryover: Blood cultures → Citrate (blue) → Serum (red/gold) → Heparin (green) → EDTA (purple) → Fluoride (grey). This is standardised by CLSI protocol.

Sample collection — critical pre-analytical factors

  • Patient preparation: For most biochemical tests, blood should be collected after fasting (8–12 hours) unless the test does not require it.
  • Tourniquet time: Apply for no longer than 1 minute. Prolonged tourniquet causes haemoconcentration and falsely elevated protein, calcium, and enzyme results.
  • Venepuncture technique: Clean the site with 70% alcohol and allow to dry before puncture. Contamination with alcohol causes haemolysis.
  • Tube filling: Fill tubes to the marked line. Underfilling dilutes the anticoagulant in relation to blood volume — critical for citrate tubes (must be exactly 9:1 blood:citrate).
  • Mixing: Gently invert anticoagulant tubes 5–10 times immediately after collection. Do not shake — this causes haemolysis.

Serum preparation procedure

Standard serum separation protocol
1
Collect blood into a plain red-top or SST (gold-top) tube using correct venepuncture technique.
2
Allow the tube to clot at room temperature for 20–30 minutes in an upright position. Do not refrigerate before clotting — cold inhibits the clotting cascade.
3
Centrifuge at 1500–2000 × g for 10 minutes (or as per your laboratory SOP). The clot will retract to the bottom; serum rises above.
4
For SST tubes: the gel creates a physical barrier between serum and clot. For plain tubes: carefully aspirate the serum without disturbing the clot using a Pasteur pipette.
5
Transfer serum to clearly labelled aliquot tubes. Process within 2 hours of collection for most analytes.
Serum should be pale yellow and clear. Pink or red colour indicates haemolysis.

Plasma preparation procedure

Standard plasma separation protocol
1
Collect blood into the appropriate anticoagulant tube (EDTA, heparin, or citrate depending on the test required).
2
Invert the tube 8–10 times gently immediately after collection to mix with the anticoagulant. Do not shake.
3
Centrifuge at 1500–2000 × g for 10 minutes at room temperature. This sediments all cellular components.
4
Do not allow tubes to stand after centrifugation — cellular metabolism continues and analyte levels change.
5
Carefully aspirate the plasma (top layer) avoiding the buffy coat (thin pale layer between plasma and red cells) and transfer to labelled aliquot tubes.
Plasma should be clear to slightly cloudy. Avoid disturbing the cell pellet.

Centrifugation conditions

RCF vs RPM

Always specify centrifugation as Relative Centrifugal Force (RCF or × g), not RPM. RPM depends on the rotor radius and varies between centrifuges. RCF is universal. Formula: RCF = 1.118 × 10⁻⁵ × r × N², where r = rotor radius (cm) and N = speed (RPM).

Sample typeRecommended RCFTimeTemperature
Serum (general biochemistry)1500–2000 × g10 minRoom temperature
Plasma (general)1500–2000 × g10 minRoom temperature
Citrate plasma (coagulation)1500–2000 × g15 minRoom temperature
Platelet-poor plasma2000–2500 × g15 minRoom temperature
Urine400 × g5 minRoom temperature

Aliquoting and labelling

  • Divide separated serum or plasma into aliquots of appropriate volume for your laboratory's assay workload. Repeated freeze-thaw cycles degrade many analytes.
  • Use cryovials (screw-cap microtubes) for storage — not open Eppendorf tubes which can open during freezing and cause evaporation.
  • Label every aliquot with: patient ID, date and time of collection, sample type (serum/plasma), and anticoagulant if applicable.
  • Never rely on verbal identification — label each tube as you work.

Storage conditions

Storage conditionStabilityNotes
Room temperature (18–25°C)2–4 hours for most analytesOnly for immediate analysis
Refrigerated (2–8°C)Up to 5–7 days for most analytesGlucose degrades even refrigerated (use fluoride tube)
Frozen (−20°C)Weeks to monthsAvoid for enzymes — many lose activity; avoid for cryoproteins
Deep frozen (−70 to −80°C)Months to yearsBiobanking; preserve most analytes including proteins and DNA

Haemolysis — causes and prevention

Haemolysis (rupture of red blood cells) releases intracellular contents into the sample, causing falsely elevated potassium, LDH, AST, and other analytes. It is the most common cause of unsuitable samples in clinical laboratories.

  • Causes: Traumatic venepuncture, small-gauge needle, excessive syringe suction, mixing too vigorously, prolonged tourniquet, alcohol not dried before puncture, delayed centrifugation, freezing samples in non-cryovials.
  • Visual detection: Haemolysed serum or plasma appears pink to red. Modern analysers measure absorbance at 414 nm to quantify haemolysis.
  • Action: Report the degree of haemolysis and note its impact on specific analytes. Reject and recollect if the degree of interference exceeds your laboratory's acceptance criteria.

Key takeaways

What to remember from this tutorial

  • Serum is obtained after clotting; plasma is obtained with anticoagulant. Serum lacks fibrinogen and clotting factors.
  • Choose the correct tube type based on the test requested — wrong tube means wrong results.
  • Allow serum to clot 20–30 minutes at room temperature before centrifugation. Never refrigerate before clotting.
  • Centrifuge at 1500–2000 × g for 10 minutes. Use RCF (× g), not RPM.
  • Invert anticoagulant tubes 8–10 times gently — never shake.
  • Haemolysis is the most common cause of unsuitable samples; visual inspection before analysis is mandatory.
  • Aliquot samples before freezing to avoid repeated freeze-thaw cycles.
