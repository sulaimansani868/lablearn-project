Glassware

Glass is the dominant material for laboratory containers because it is chemically inert to most substances, transparent, able to withstand moderate heat, and easily cleaned and sterilised. Understanding which piece of glassware to use for which task is foundational laboratory knowledge.

ItemPrimary useGraduated?Key notes
BeakerGeneral-purpose vessel for mixing, heating, dissolvingApproximate onlyNot for accurate volume measurement; wide mouth for easy mixing
Erlenmeyer (conical) flaskMixing, reactions, swirling solutionsApproximate onlyNarrow neck reduces evaporation and splash; good for titrations
Round-bottom flaskHeating, distillation, reflux reactionsNoDistributes heat evenly; requires a clamp or cork ring to stand
Volumetric flaskPreparing solutions of exact concentrationSingle calibration markAccurate to ±0.01–0.06 mL; never heat; neck designed for exact volume only
Measuring cylinder (graduated cylinder)Measuring approximate volumesYes — graduatedBetter than a beaker; less accurate than a volumetric flask or pipette
BuretteDelivering precise variable volumes in titrationsYes — 0–50 mL, 0.1 mL divisionsRead from the bottom of the meniscus; check stopcock for leaks before use
Pipette (volumetric)Delivering one fixed precise volumeSingle markMost accurate volumetric transfer device; requires a pipette aid
Test tubeSmall-scale reactions and observationsNoCan be heated directly in a flame; do not point opening at anyone
Watch glassCovering beakers; evaporation; weighing small amountsNoNot for heating liquids; used as a non-absorbent weighing surface
Petri dishCulturing microorganisms; evaporation; sample displayNoGlass or polystyrene; polystyrene not for organic solvents
⚠ Inspect glassware before every use

Chips, cracks, and star fractures are stress concentrators — they cause glassware to shatter unpredictably, especially when heated or under vacuum. Hold each piece of glassware up to light and inspect before use. Any damaged item must be discarded in the designated broken glass container — never in a regular waste bin.

Measuring and volumetric equipment

Accurate volume measurement is central to quantitative laboratory work. The choice of measuring device must match the required accuracy and the volume range needed.

Mixing and heating equipment

Many laboratory procedures require controlled mixing or heating. Using the appropriate device prevents accidents and ensures consistent results.

EquipmentFunctionKey caution
Magnetic stirrer / hot plateStirs and/or heats solutions using a rotating magnet and heating elementUse a hot-surface indicator; never heat sealed containers; check stir bar is spinning
Vortex mixerRapid high-speed mixing of small tubes by eccentric rotationCap tubes before vortexing; do not vortex fragile samples (cells, proteins)
Orbital shakerGentle, continuous circular mixing of flasks or platesClamp all vessels securely; do not exceed speed rating for vessel weight
Water bathControlled temperature incubation; precise thermal equilibrationCheck water level daily; use anti-bacterial/anti-fungal additive for extended use
Heating block (dry block)Dry heating of tubes at precise temperature without waterMatch block insert to tube diameter; use with a lid for volatile samples
Bunsen burnerOpen flame for sterilisation, heating, and ignitionNever leave unattended; tie back hair; keep flammable solvents away from flame
Muffle furnaceHigh-temperature heating (up to 1200°C) for ashing and sinteringRequires specialist training; use heat-resistant crucibles only

Choosing the right equipment for the task

A common beginner error is reaching for the nearest available vessel rather than the most appropriate one. Apply the following logic when selecting equipment.

Equipment selection decision process
1What is the volume? — Select a container that holds 1.5–2× the volume you need to work with; do not fill any vessel beyond two-thirds capacity when heating.
2How accurate must the volume be? — If ±1 mL is acceptable, use a measuring cylinder. If ±0.1 mL is needed, use a graduated pipette. If exact concentration is critical, use a volumetric flask and volumetric pipette.
3Will the vessel be heated? — Use borosilicate glass (Pyrex/Duran), not soda glass, which can crack under thermal stress. Never heat a volumetric flask or measuring cylinder.
4What chemicals are involved? — Some chemicals attack glass (e.g. hydrofluoric acid, concentrated alkalis). Use PTFE or polyethylene containers for these.
5Does sterility matter? — Use pre-sterilised disposable plasticware or autoclaved glassware for microbiological or cell culture work.
Key Takeaways

What to remember from this tutorial

  • Beakers and Erlenmeyer flasks are for mixing and reactions — not accurate volume measurement.
  • Volumetric flasks, volumetric pipettes, and burettes are for accurate volume work — never heat volumetric glassware.
  • Always inspect glassware for chips and cracks before use — damaged glassware must be discarded, not used.
  • Select the measuring device that matches your required accuracy: measuring cylinder → graduated pipette → volumetric pipette/flask in increasing accuracy.
  • Never put a used spatula back into a stock reagent bottle — always use a clean spatula for each reagent.
  • Equipment selection should consider: volume, required accuracy, heating, chemical compatibility, and sterility requirements.
  • Consumables (tips, tubes, filter paper) must match the task — check ratings before use with non-standard conditions (cryogenics, high centrifugal force).
