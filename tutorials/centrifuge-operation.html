Centrifuge Operation: Safety, Balancing, and Protocol

Principle and types of centrifuge

A centrifuge separates components of a mixture by applying centrifugal force — particles denser than the surrounding medium migrate outward and sediment at the bottom of the tube, while less dense components remain above. The rate of sedimentation depends on particle size, density, the density of the medium, and the centrifugal force applied.

Laboratory centrifuges range from small benchtop microcentrifuges to large floor-standing ultracentrifuges. Understanding which type is appropriate for your application is the first step in safe and effective centrifugation.

TypeMax RCFTypical useNotes
Microcentrifuge16,000 × gPelleting cells, DNA, precipitates from 0.5–2 mL tubesMost common bench centrifuge; fixed-angle rotor; no temperature control in basic models
Benchtop centrifuge5,000–10,000 × gSerum/plasma separation, urine, 15 mL and 50 mL tubesAccepts a wide range of tube sizes; often has swing-out and fixed-angle rotors
Refrigerated centrifugeUp to 30,000 × gTemperature-sensitive samples — coagulation, cells, proteinsMaintains set temperature during run; essential for labile analytes
High-speed centrifuge30,000–100,000 × gSub-cellular fractionation, virus pelleting, mitochondria isolationRequires specially designed sealed rotors
UltracentrifugeUp to 700,000 × gLipoprotein separation, ribosomes, macromoleculesRuns under vacuum; requires extensive training and certification
Haematocrit centrifuge10,000–12,000 × gPacked cell volume (PCV) determination from capillary tubesSpecialised fixed-angle head for capillary tubes; short run time (5 min)

RCF vs RPM — understanding the difference

This is the most important conceptual point in centrifuge operation. Always specify centrifugation conditions as RCF (Relative Centrifugal Force, expressed as × g), not as RPM (revolutions per minute). RPM depends entirely on the rotor radius — the same RPM on two different rotors produces completely different centrifugal forces.

Converting between RCF and RPM
RCF (× g) = 1.118 × 10⁻⁵ × r × N²
r = radius of rotor (cm) from centre of rotor to bottom of tube N = speed in RPM Example: r = 8.6 cm, N = 13,000 RPM RCF = 1.118 × 10⁻⁵ × 8.6 × 13,000² = 16,260 × g

Most modern centrifuges allow you to enter the target RCF directly and will calculate the required RPM for the installed rotor automatically. If your centrifuge only accepts RPM, calculate the required RPM from the target RCF using a nomogram (provided in the centrifuge manual) or the rearranged formula above.

Why protocols specify × g, not RPM

A protocol specifying “centrifuge at 3,000 RPM” is ambiguous — it may produce 800×g in one centrifuge and 1,800×g in another. A protocol specifying “1,500×g for 10 minutes” produces the same result in every centrifuge regardless of rotor model. When writing or following protocols, always use ×g.

Rotor types

The rotor is the rotating assembly that holds the sample tubes. Different rotor designs produce different sedimentation paths, which affects both separation efficiency and the pelleting pattern.

Rotor typeTube orientationSedimentation pathBest used for
Fixed-angle25–45° to vertical axisShort — particle hits tube wall, slides to bottomPelleting cells, precipitates, nuclei; fastest pelleting time
Swing-out (swinging bucket)Swings to horizontal during spinLong — particle travels full tube lengthDensity gradient separations, serum/plasma from SST tubes, cell washes
VerticalCompletely verticalVery short — particle travels tube radius onlyUltracentrifugation; isopycnic banding; lipoprotein separation
Near-vertical7–10° from verticalShortCsCl and sucrose gradient ultracentrifugation
Figure 1 — Three centrifuge rotor types: tube orientation and pellet position
At speed, centrifugal force (RCF) acts horizontally outward from the spin axis. The rotor design determines how the tube is held and therefore where the pellet forms. Fixed-angle rotors give the shortest sedimentation path; swing-out rotors give the longest. Vertical rotors (not shown here) hold tubes parallel to the axis — particles travel only the tube radius before pelleting.
⛔ Never use a rotor beyond its rated maximum speed

Every rotor has a maximum RPM rating stamped on its body. Exceeding this causes metal fatigue and can result in rotor failure — a catastrophic mechanical event that destroys the centrifuge and can cause serious injury. Always check the rotor’s rated maximum before setting speed, especially when using an unfamiliar rotor.

Before you start — safety checks

Centrifuge accidents are rare but serious when they occur. Most result from imbalanced loads, rotor damage, or tube failure at high speed. A systematic pre-run check takes less than two minutes and eliminates most risks.

Pre-run safety checklist
  • Check that the correct rotor is installed and seated properly on the drive shaft
  • Inspect the rotor for cracks, corrosion, pitting, or discolouration — remove from service if any defects are found
  • Verify that the rotor O-ring or gasket is present, clean, and undamaged (for sealed rotors)
  • Check that all tube positions are either occupied by balanced pairs or by correctly placed balance tubes — never leave rotor positions randomly empty
  • Confirm that tube caps or lids are securely fastened
  • Check the centrifuge bowl for debris, dried liquid, or broken tube fragments from a previous run
  • Verify that the centrifuge lid closes and latches correctly — do not run with an improperly latched lid
  • Confirm the set RCF and time match the protocol requirements before pressing start

Balancing samples correctly

Balancing is the most critical safety and performance step in centrifuge operation. An unbalanced rotor vibrates excessively, damages the drive shaft bearings, and at high speed can cause rotor failure.

The rule: Tubes placed opposite each other in the rotor must have the same mass to within the tolerance stated in your centrifuge manual — typically ±0.5 g for a microcentrifuge and ±1–2 g for a benchtop centrifuge. Mass, not volume, is what matters for balancing — tubes of the same volume but different content density have different masses.

Balancing rules by tube count
1
One sample tube: Place one balance tube containing the same volume of water (or buffer of similar density) directly opposite in the rotor.
2
Even number of tubes of equal mass: Space them symmetrically around the rotor — opposite pairs for 2 tubes; at 120° intervals for 3 tubes; at 90° for 4 tubes.
3
Odd number of sample tubes: Add balance tubes to make up to the nearest even or symmetric arrangement. A rotor holding 6 positions can run 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, or 6 tubes if balanced symmetrically.
4
Different tube sizes in the same run: Use adapters to seat smaller tubes in larger rotor positions. Ensure every position with an adapter is balanced against an equivalent adapter + balance tube.
5
Weigh tubes when density differs: For samples containing heavy solutions (CsCl, sucrose, perchloric acid), weigh each tube on a balance and match masses within tolerance rather than relying on equal volumes.
After loading, manually rotate the rotor slowly by hand before closing the lid. It should rotate smoothly with no wobble. Resistance or wobble indicates imbalance — recheck your loading.
Balancing a swing-out rotor

In a swing-out rotor, each bucket must be balanced individually — not just the total rotor. Each bucket swings independently, so a heavy bucket on one side and a light bucket on the other will cause severe vibration even if the total mass on both sides appears equal. Weigh each loaded bucket and balance to within tolerance.

Loading and closing the rotor

  • Load tubes so that the sample pellet will form at the bottom of the tube (for fixed-angle rotors, pellet forms at the outer lower wall; for swing-out, at the true bottom).
  • Do not fill tubes more than three-quarters full when using snap-cap tubes at high speed — liquid expansion under centrifugal force can force caps open and cause leakage.
  • For screw-cap tubes, tighten caps firmly but do not overtighten (this can strip the cap or distort the tube, causing leakage).
  • Wipe the outside of tubes dry before loading — liquid on the outside contaminates the rotor and makes mass estimation less accurate.
  • Close the rotor lid or bucket caps before closing the centrifuge lid. Engage any locking mechanism on the rotor.
  • Close the centrifuge lid gently. Confirm it latches. Most centrifuges will not start with an open or improperly latched lid.

Setting speed, time, and temperature

Setting centrifuge parameters
1
RCF (speed): Enter the target ×g from your protocol. If the centrifuge only accepts RPM, calculate the required RPM for your installed rotor using the formula above or the centrifuge’s built-in conversion function.
2
Time: Set the run time as specified in the protocol. Note whether the protocol specifies time from start of spin or from reaching the set speed — these differ by the acceleration period (typically 30–90 seconds).
3
Temperature: For refrigerated centrifuges, pre-cool the rotor and bowl to the target temperature (typically 4°C for biological samples) for at least 20 minutes before starting the run. Confirm the display shows the bowl temperature, not just the set point.
4
Acceleration and deceleration: For samples where pelleting is irreversible (cells, DNA), use maximum acceleration and deceleration. For density gradient separations where the band must not be disturbed, set a low deceleration rate (“soft stop” or “decel 1”) to prevent the gradient from mixing when the rotor slows.
5
Confirm all parameters before pressing Start. Check ×g, time, temperature, and deceleration setting.
Press Start. The centrifuge should begin spinning smoothly. Any vibration, unusual noise, or error code within the first 30 seconds — press Stop immediately, wait for the rotor to come to a complete halt, then investigate before restarting.

During and after the run

During the run: Never leave the centrifuge unattended in the first 2 minutes of a run. This is when imbalance, tube failure, and rotor problems are most likely to become apparent. Listen and watch — a normally running centrifuge produces a smooth, even noise that reaches maximum pitch as it attains full speed, then remains constant. Vibration, intermittent noise, or a grinding sound are all signs to stop immediately.

⛔ Never open the centrifuge lid while the rotor is spinning

Even at low speed, a rotating rotor can cause severe injury. Wait until the rotor has come to a complete stop and the centrifuge signals that it is safe to open (the lid interlock releases). Never override the lid interlock mechanism under any circumstances.

After the run: Open the lid gently. For gradient separations, do not jolt or vibrate the centrifuge or rotor — the separated bands disturb easily. Remove tubes carefully, withdraw liquid from the top down if harvesting a supernatant, and inspect the rotor bowl for any spilled liquid or broken tubes before closing.

  • Wipe the rotor bowl immediately if any liquid has leaked — dried protein and salt deposits corrode aluminium rotors.
  • Remove tubes promptly after the run — samples left in a stationary centrifuge at room temperature may warm up, and pellets may resuspend if left too long.
  • Log the run in the centrifuge logbook: date, time, rotor used, RCF, duration, operator name, and any observations.

Biohazardous and infectious samples

Blood, serum, urine, sputum, and any sample of unknown infectious status must be treated as potentially biohazardous. Centrifugation creates aerosols — fine droplets that become airborne during high-speed spinning, especially if a tube leaks or breaks inside the rotor.

Biosafety requirements for centrifugation
  • Centrifuge biohazardous samples in a certified biosafety cabinet where possible, or in a centrifuge equipped with a sealed aerosol-tight rotor or biosafety buckets with lids
  • Wear appropriate PPE — gloves, laboratory coat, and eye protection as a minimum; face shield for high-risk samples
  • If a tube breaks during a run, do not open the centrifuge for at least 30 minutes (or 10 minutes for a sealed rotor) to allow aerosol to settle
  • Decontaminate the rotor, bowl, and all contaminated surfaces with an appropriate disinfectant (70% ethanol or 1% hypochlorite) before removing broken tubes
  • Report tube breakage incidents involving biohazardous material to your supervisor and record in the incident log
  • Use sealed tubes with screw caps — snap-cap tubes are more likely to open under high centrifugal force

Common centrifugation applications

Different laboratory workflows require different centrifugation conditions. The table in the Settings section covers the most common. Below are specific notes on three frequently mishandled applications.

Serum and plasma separation

Allow serum tubes (SST, plain red top) to clot completely at room temperature for 20–30 minutes before centrifugation. Centrifuging before clot formation is complete traps fibrin strands in the serum, causing turbidity and potential analytical interference. Plasma tubes (EDTA, heparin) should be centrifuged promptly without a clotting wait.

Urine sediment

Use 400–500 × g, not higher. Excessive centrifugal force destroys fragile casts and cells, making microscopic examination unreliable. Standardise both RCF and the volume of urine used (typically 10 mL in a conical tube) to allow semi-quantitative comparison between samples.

Density gradient centrifugation

Sucrose and Ficoll density gradient separations depend on carefully layering the sample above the gradient and using a slow deceleration setting. Any vibration or fast deceleration mixes the gradient and destroys band separation. Always use a swing-out rotor for density gradients — fixed-angle rotors are not suitable.

Rotor care and inspection

Rotors are precision-machined from aluminium alloy or carbon fibre composite. They are subjected to enormous stress during every spin. Proper care significantly extends rotor life and maintains safety.

  • Clean after every use: Wipe the rotor with a damp cloth and mild detergent. Rinse with distilled water and dry thoroughly. Never use steel wool, abrasive cleaners, chlorine bleach above 0.5%, or strong alkalis on aluminium rotors — these cause pitting corrosion that weakens the metal.
  • Dry before storing: Moisture trapped in rotor cavities corrodes aluminium. Invert the rotor and allow to air-dry, or dry with a soft cloth. Store inverted with cavities facing down.
  • Inspect before every use: Look for pitting, cracks, discolouration (white powder on aluminium indicates corrosion), or distorted tube positions. A cracked or significantly corroded rotor must be removed from service immediately and must not be used.
  • Rotor log and maximum number of runs: Many high-speed rotors have a stated maximum number of runs or service interval (often 500–1,000 runs for ultracentrifuge rotors). Maintain a rotor log and retire rotors at the stated limit even if they appear undamaged.
  • Chemical compatibility: Check the rotor material against the chemicals in your samples. Strong acids, strong bases, and some organic solvents are incompatible with aluminium rotors. Use chemically resistant (polypropylene or carbon fibre) rotors for these applications.
⛔ Remove a damaged rotor from service immediately

A rotor with any cracks, significant corrosion pitting, or deformed tube cavities must never be used. Rotor failure at high speed is a catastrophic event — the rotor fragments fly outward with enormous kinetic energy, destroying the centrifuge and potentially penetrating the centrifuge housing. Label the rotor “DO NOT USE — DAMAGED” and report it immediately to your supervisor.

Key takeaways

What to remember from this tutorial

  • Always specify centrifugation conditions as RCF (× g), not RPM. The same RPM produces different RCF values in different rotors. Use RCF = 1.118 × 10⁻⁵ × r × N² to convert.
  • Balance is the most critical safety requirement. Tubes opposite each other must have equal mass (not just equal volume) within the manufacturer’s stated tolerance. Weigh tubes when sample density differs.
  • Complete a pre-run checklist before every spin: rotor condition, O-ring, balance, cap security, bowl cleanliness, lid latch.
  • Never open the centrifuge lid while the rotor is spinning. Never exceed the rotor’s rated maximum RPM.
  • Stay near the centrifuge for the first 2 minutes of every run. Vibration, unusual noise, or any burning smell — stop immediately.
  • For biohazardous samples, use sealed aerosol-tight rotors or biosafety buckets, appropriate PPE, and follow your institution’s biosafety protocol for tube breakage incidents.
  • Clean, dry, and inspect the rotor after every use. A rotor with cracks, pitting, or corrosion must be removed from service immediately and never used again.
