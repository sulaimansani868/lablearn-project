Reagent-Grade Water: Preparation, Quality, and Use

Why water quality matters

Water is the solvent for virtually every reagent, standard, and sample preparation step in an analytical laboratory. Impurities in the water used for preparation directly contaminate these solutions, producing systematic errors that are difficult to trace. A laboratory may invest in precision balances, calibrated pipettes, and certified reference materials — all undermined by the unseen contamination introduced through poor-quality water.

This tutorial is directly connected to several others in this library. AAS mineral analysis requires ultrapure water free from trace metals. GOD-PAP glucose assays are affected by oxidising contaminants. UV-Vis spectrophotometry is affected by organic contamination that absorbs in the UV range. Buffer preparation is affected by ionic impurities that alter ionic strength and pH. Understanding water quality is therefore foundational to all other analytical work.

Water quality grades

Laboratory water quality is defined by international standards, principally ISO 3696 and ASTM D1193. Both classify water into grades based on resistivity, maximum ion and organic contaminant levels.

Type 1 / Grade 1 (Ultrapure)Grade 1≥ 18.2HPLC mobile phases, AAS, ICP-MS, cell culture media, molecular biology (PCR, sequencing), critical electrochemistry
Type 2 / Grade 2Grade 2≥ 1.0General laboratory reagents, buffers, culture media, routine spectrophotometry, most clinical chemistry assays
Type 3 / Grade 3 (Distilled or RO)Grade 3≥ 0.05Glassware rinsing, autoclave water, buffer preparation where high purity is not critical
Tap waterNot graded< 0.01Never for analytical work; only for cooling, cleaning non-contact surfaces
18.2 MΩ·cm — the theoretical maximum

18.2 MΩ·cm (or 18.2 megohm-centimetres) is the maximum resistivity of pure water at 25°C, set by the autoionisation of water itself (K𝑤 = 10&sup-;¹&sup4;). It represents the theoretical limit of ionic purity. Any ionic impurity reduces resistivity. Resistivity is therefore the primary in-line quality indicator used by ultrapure water systems.

Municipal tap water contains numerous dissolved impurities that interfere with analytical measurements in different ways:

Water purification methods

Distillation

Water is heated to boiling; the steam is collected and condensed. Dissolved salts, most bacteria, and non-volatile organics remain in the boiling flask and are not carried over in the steam. Distillation effectively removes ionic contaminants and particulates but does not remove volatile organic compounds (which co-distil with steam) or dissolved CO₂.

Single distillation produces water approximately equivalent to Type 3. Double or triple distillation (in borosilicate glass or quartz stills) can approach Type 2. For trace metal work, use a quartz or PTFE still — borosilicate glass leaches Si, Na, and B into the distillate.

Ion exchange (deionisation)

Water is passed through columns containing cation exchange resin (H-form, removes Ca²♠, Mg²♠, Na♠, K♠ etc.) and anion exchange resin (OH-form, removes Cl&sup-;, SO₄²&sup-;, NO₃&sup-; etc.). The exchanged ions are replaced by H♠ and OH&sup-; respectively, which combine to form water. A mixed-bed column (both resins mixed) produces water of highest ionic purity.

Deionisation does not remove uncharged organics, bacteria, particles, or dissolved gases. Exhausted resins can release organics and bacteria into the water — resistivity monitoring is essential to know when the resin needs regeneration or replacement.

Reverse osmosis (RO)

Water is forced under pressure through a semi-permeable membrane with pores too small for hydrated ions, large organic molecules, and bacteria to pass through. RO alone produces water suitable for Grade 3 applications and removes 90–99% of ions, most organic molecules above 200 Da, and bacteria. It is not sufficient for Grade 1 applications alone but is the standard pre-treatment step before ultrapure polishing systems.

Ultrapure water systems (Type 1 / Grade 1)

Modern laboratory ultrapure water systems combine several technologies in series: pre-treatment (RO), ion exchange polishing, activated carbon for organic removal, UV irradiation (185 nm for organic oxidation; 254 nm for bacteria), ultrafiltration (0.2 µm or smaller membrane for endotoxin and bacteria removal), and continuous resistivity monitoring.

The resistivity at the point of use is displayed continuously. Water should be used immediately — ultrapure water absorbs CO₂ from the air within seconds of exposure, which lowers resistivity and raises ionic content. Dispense directly into the vessel being used; do not allow it to stand in open containers.

Resistivity, conductivity, and TOC as quality indicators

Resistivity and conductivity relationship
Resistivity (ρ, MΩ·cm) = 1 / Conductivity (σ, µS/cm) Pure water (25°C): ρ = 18.2 MΩ·cm ≡ σ = 0.055 µS/cm Tap water: ρ < 0.01 MΩ·cm ≡ σ > 100 µS/cm
TOC (Total Organic Carbon, µg/L or ppb): measures dissolved organic content. Grade 1 water typically requires TOC < 5 ppb. High TOC despite good resistivity indicates organic contamination that ions exchangers do not remove.

Storage and handling of purified water

⛔ Ultrapure water degrades rapidly on standing

Grade 1 (ultrapure) water absorbs CO₂ from the air within seconds, raising conductivity. It leaches trace ions from plastic or glass storage vessels within minutes. It supports microbial growth within hours. Dispense ultrapure water directly from the tap at the point of use. Never store it in open containers or use water that was dispensed earlier in the day for critical applications.

  • Grade 3 and distilled water can be stored in clean, sealed polyethylene or glass containers in the dark at room temperature for up to one week for non-critical applications.
  • Use PTFE or high-density polyethylene (HDPE) containers for storing water intended for trace metal analysis — borosilicate glass leaches Si, Na, and B; regular polyethylene leaches plasticisers and trace metals.
  • Never return unused water to the stock container — this risks contaminating the entire supply.
  • Label every container of purified water with the grade, source (still/deioniser/system name), date of preparation, and initials of the person who prepared it.

Key takeaways

What to remember from this tutorial

  • Water quality is graded by resistivity: Grade 1 (≥18.2 MΩ·cm, ultrapure) for critical work such as AAS, HPLC, and cell culture; Grade 2 (≥1 MΩ·cm) for general laboratory reagents and routine assays; Grade 3 for rinsing and autoclave use only.
  • Tap water contains ionic impurities, trace metals, organics, bacteria, and dissolved gases — none of which are acceptable in analytical reagents. Never use tap water to prepare analytical-grade standards, buffers, or working reagents.
  • Distillation removes ions and bacteria but not volatile organics. Ion exchange removes ions but not organics or bacteria. Reverse osmosis is an effective pre-treatment. Ultrapure systems combine all technologies and provide continuous resistivity monitoring.
  • Grade 1 ultrapure water degrades within seconds of exposure to air (CO₂ absorption). Dispense directly from the system tap at the point of use. Never store ultrapure water in open or glass containers for use in trace analysis.
  • Monitor water quality regularly: resistivity for ionic purity, TOC for organic contamination, and microbial counts for biological purity. A system that reads 18.2 MΩ·cm but has high TOC is not suitable for UV spectrophotometry or HPLC.
  • Match the grade to the application. Using Grade 1 water for autoclave fill is wasteful; using Grade 3 for AAS introduces serious errors. Know what your application requires and use the appropriate grade.
