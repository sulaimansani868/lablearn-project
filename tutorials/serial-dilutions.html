Serial Dilutions: Technique & Calculation

What is a serial dilution?

A serial dilution is a stepwise sequence of dilutions in which a fixed volume is transferred from one tube to the next, with each tube containing the same volume of diluent. Each step reduces the concentration by a constant factor called the dilution factor.

If you start with a 1 M solution and perform a series of 1:10 dilutions, the concentrations progress: 0.1 M → 0.01 M → 0.001 M → and so on. The power of this technique is that it lets you cover many orders of magnitude of concentration using a small number of simple, reproducible steps.

Why use serial dilutions?

Direct single-step dilutions to very low concentrations are impractical and introduce significant pipetting error. For example, to prepare a 1 × 10⁻⁶ M solution from a 1 M stock, you would need to pipette 1 µL into 1 L — impossible with standard equipment.

Serial dilution solves this by breaking that extreme dilution into manageable steps, each within the accurate range of your pipettes. It is fundamental to:

  • Preparing calibration standards for spectrophotometric assays
  • Counting microbial cells (colony-forming units per mL)
  • Antibody titration and immunological assays (ELISA, Western blot)
  • Drug dose-response studies in pharmacology
  • Environmental sample analysis

Dilution factor

The dilution factor (DF) for a single step is the ratio of the total volume in the receiving tube to the volume transferred into it:

Dilution factor — single step
DF = V_total / V_transferred
e.g. transferring 1 mL into 9 mL diluent → DF = (9+1)/1 = 10

For a series of n identical steps, the cumulative dilution factor is:

Cumulative dilution factor
DF_total = DF^n
e.g. three 1:10 steps → DF_total = 10³ = 1000× dilution

The final concentration after the series is:

Final concentration
C_final = C_stock / DF_total

The C₁V₁ = C₂V₂ equation

Each dilution step is a simple application of the dilution equation. The amount of solute (moles or mass) stays the same before and after mixing:

Dilution equation
C₁ × V₁ = C₂ × V₂
C₁ = initial conc. · V₁ = initial vol. · C₂ = final conc. · V₂ = final vol.

You can rearrange this to find any one unknown. The online dilution calculator handles all four cases.

Step-by-step procedure

A clean, methodical technique is essential. Here is the standard approach for a 1:10 serial dilution using 1.5 mL tubes:

General procedure — 1:10 serial dilution
1
Label all tubes clearly: stock, 10⁻¹, 10⁻², 10⁻³, etc. before you begin.
2
Add 900 µL of diluent (e.g. distilled water or buffer) to each tube except the stock.
3
Using a calibrated micropipette, transfer 100 µL from the stock into tube 10⁻¹.
4
Vortex or mix tube 10⁻¹ thoroughly for 5–10 seconds.
5
Change the pipette tip. Transfer 100 µL from tube 10⁻¹ into tube 10⁻².
6
Repeat: mix each tube before sampling, always change tips between transfers.
Critical: change tips at every step — carryover is the most common source of serial dilution error.
⛔ Do not skip mixing

Incomplete mixing before the next transfer causes the transferred aliquot to not represent the true average concentration of the tube. Always vortex or pipette-mix (10 up-and-down strokes) before taking the next sample.

Worked example — 2-fold dilution series

2-fold serial dilutions are the standard in immunology for antibody titration. Starting from a serum sample diluted 1:2, each subsequent step halves the concentration:

2-fold dilution — antibody titration setup
1
Add 100 µL of buffer to wells 2 through 8 of a 96-well plate row.
2
Add 200 µL of the undiluted serum sample to well 1.
3
Transfer 100 µL from well 1 to well 2. Mix by pipetting 5 times.
4
Transfer 100 µL from well 2 to well 3. Continue along the row.
5
Discard 100 µL from the last well to maintain equal volumes.
Dilutions: 1:2 → 1:4 → 1:8 → 1:16 → 1:32 → 1:64 → 1:128

Common errors and how to avoid them

ErrorConsequencePrevention
Not changing pipette tipsCarryover — concentrations are too highAlways use a fresh tip per transfer
Incomplete mixingSamples not representative — variable resultsVortex 5–10 s, or pipette-mix × 10
Pipetting from wrong tubeErrors cascade through the seriesLabel clearly; work left-to-right
Pipetting inaccuracy at small volumesSystematic error in all dilutionsCalibrate pipettes; use the 100–900 µL scheme not 10–90
Using the wrong diluentProtein denaturation, salt effectsUse the same buffer as your assay system

Applications by field

Where serial dilutions are used
  • Microbiology: counting colonies (CFU/mL) from environmental or food samples
  • Biochemistry: preparing protein or enzyme calibration standards for Bradford, BCA, or Lowry assays
  • Clinical immunology: antibody titration, ELISA standard curves, viral neutralisation assays
  • Pharmacology: dose-response curves (IC₅₀, EC₅₀ determination)
  • Environmental chemistry: trace element calibration standards for AAS or ICP-MS

Key takeaways

What to remember from this tutorial

  • Serial dilutions apply the same dilution factor at each step, creating a geometric progression of concentrations.
  • Dilution factor = total volume ÷ volume transferred. For 100 µL into 900 µL, DF = 10.
  • Cumulative dilution factor after n steps = DF^n.
  • Always change pipette tips between every transfer — tip carryover is the most common source of error.
  • Mix thoroughly before each transfer; incomplete mixing invalidates the dilution.
  • Use C₁V₁ = C₂V₂ to verify or design any individual dilution step.
