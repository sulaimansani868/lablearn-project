Introduction to Good Laboratory Practice (GLP)

What is Good Laboratory Practice?

Good Laboratory Practice (GLP) is a formal quality system for the conduct of non-clinical laboratory studies. It was introduced in the late 1970s following a series of scandals involving fraudulent safety studies submitted to regulatory agencies — most notoriously the Industrial Bio-Test Laboratories scandal in the United States, in which thousands of safety studies were found to be fabricated or invalid.

In a formal regulatory sense, GLP refers to the OECD GLP Principles — a set of internationally recognised guidelines that govern how studies must be planned, conducted, recorded, and reported when the data will be submitted to government regulators to assess the safety of chemicals, pharmaceuticals, or medical devices.

In everyday laboratory use, "GLP" has a broader informal meaning: a set of practices that ensure laboratory work is reliable, reproducible, and traceable. This tutorial addresses GLP in that broader sense, which is relevant to every student and researcher regardless of whether they work in a regulated environment.

Why GLP matters for students

Even if your undergraduate project will never be submitted to a regulator, GLP habits matter because they protect your data, enable others to reproduce your work, and build the professional discipline that will define the quality of your science throughout your career. Sloppy habits formed early are hard to break.

The ten GLP principles (OECD)

The OECD GLP Principles are organised around ten areas. Understanding these helps you appreciate the scope of what GLP is trying to achieve — traceability, reproducibility, and integrity throughout the entire lifecycle of a study.

#Principle areaWhat it covers
1Test facility organisation and personnelDefined roles; qualifications; training records; study director responsibility
2Quality assurance programmeIndependent inspection of studies to verify GLP compliance
3FacilitiesAdequate space, separation of work areas, appropriate storage
4Apparatus, materials, and reagentsCalibration records, expiry dates, suitability for intended use
5Test systemsCharacterisation and care of biological or chemical test systems
6Test and reference itemsIdentity, purity, stability, storage, and chain of custody of all substances tested
7Standard Operating Procedures (SOPs)Written, approved, accessible procedures for all routine activities
8Performance of the studyStudy plan; conduct as per plan; deviations documented
9Reporting of study resultsFinal report requirements; signed by study director; all data referenced
10Storage and retention of recordsArchives; access control; retention periods; raw data preservation

Laboratory notebooks

The laboratory notebook is the primary document of experimental science. It is the permanent, contemporaneous record of everything you planned, did, observed, and concluded. It is not a place for writing up a tidy version of events after the fact.

Paper notebooks — rules of record

Notebook discipline — mandatory practices
  • Write in permanent ink — never pencil, and never use correction fluid (Tipp-Ex).
  • Date every page, every entry, and every signature.
  • Record in real time — not at the end of the day from memory.
  • Never leave blank spaces that could be filled in later — draw a line through empty space.
  • Never tear out pages — if a page is spoiled, draw a line through it and explain why.
  • Correct errors with a single line through the incorrect text, initial and date alongside. The original must remain readable.
  • All raw data (instrument printouts, gel images) must be taped in with date and description, or cross-referenced if stored separately.
  • Include the lot numbers and expiry dates of all reagents used.
  • Record the identity and model of all instruments used with their calibration status.

What a notebook entry should contain

Minimum content per experiment
1Date and title — clearly identifying the experiment and its objective.
2Purpose / hypothesis — why you are doing this experiment and what you expect to find.
3Materials — all chemicals (with CAS number, supplier, lot number, purity, expiry), equipment (with model and calibration date), and samples (with unique ID).
4Method — reference to the SOP or protocol used; record any deviations from the written procedure.
5Raw observations — exactly what you saw, measured, or recorded, including unexpected results and failures.
6Calculations — show all working with units; do not record only the final answer.
7Conclusion / next steps — brief interpretation and what follows from the result.

Standard Operating Procedures (SOPs)

A Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) is a written, step-by-step instruction for a routine task. SOPs ensure that the same procedure is carried out the same way every time, by every person — eliminating variation caused by individual interpretation.

In a regulated GLP environment, SOPs are formally approved documents with version control, signatures, and review dates. In a teaching or research laboratory, SOPs may be less formal — but the principle is the same: a written procedure that anyone trained could follow and reproduce.

A good SOP includes
  • Title, SOP number, version number, effective date, and author
  • Scope — what the procedure covers and what it does not
  • Materials and equipment required
  • Safety precautions and required PPE
  • Step-by-step procedure in numbered format
  • Acceptance criteria or expected results where applicable
  • Troubleshooting guidance for common problems
  • References to related SOPs or methods

Reagent and sample management

GLP requires that the identity, purity, and stability of all materials used in an experiment can be demonstrated. This begins with correct receipt, storage, and labelling of reagents when they arrive in the laboratory.

Receiving new reagents

When a new chemical arrives: record the receipt date, check the label matches the order, inspect for damage or leakage, note the expiry date, and assign a unique lot number if one is not already printed on the container. Enter the details in your reagent register before storing.

Prepared solutions

Every solution you prepare must be labelled immediately with: the chemical name and concentration, the preparation date, the expiry date (based on known stability), the preparer's name or initials, and any storage conditions. A solution with no label cannot be used — it must be treated as an unknown and disposed of appropriately.

Equipment records

In a GLP environment, every piece of equipment used to generate data must have a record demonstrating it was fit for purpose at the time of use. This includes calibration records, preventive maintenance logs, and qualification records.

Record typeWhat it demonstratesTypical frequency
Calibration certificateInstrument reads accurately against a traceable standardAnnually for most analytical instruments
Daily performance checkInstrument is within acceptable range on the day of useBefore every use session
Maintenance logServicing, filter changes, cleaningPer manufacturer schedule
Temperature log (incubators, fridges)Storage conditions were maintained continuouslyDaily or continuous electronic monitoring
Balance calibration weights checkBalance reads correctly with certified weightsDaily before use

Traceability and chain of custody

Traceability means that any data point in a final report can be traced back through a complete, unbroken chain of documentation to the original raw measurement. Chain of custody refers specifically to the documented transfer of samples from one person or location to another, demonstrating that a sample has not been altered, contaminated, or confused with another sample at any point.

In practice, traceability requires that you record: the unique identifier of every sample, who handled it, when, under what conditions, and what was done to it. This is especially critical in forensic, clinical, and regulatory laboratory settings, but is good practice in any scientific work.

GLP vs GMP vs GCP

These three acronyms are often confused. They apply to different phases of product development and have different regulatory frameworks.

AcronymFull nameApplies toRegulated by
GLPGood Laboratory PracticeNon-clinical safety studies (chemicals, drugs, devices)OECD; national regulatory agencies
GMPGood Manufacturing PracticeManufacture of drugs, food, medical devices for human useWHO; FDA; EMA; NAFDAC (Nigeria)
GCPGood Clinical PracticeClinical trials involving human subjectsICH E6; national ethics committees
Key Takeaways

What to remember from this tutorial

  • GLP is a quality system ensuring laboratory studies are reliable, reproducible, and traceable — relevant to all laboratory workers, not just those in regulated industries.
  • The laboratory notebook is a permanent, contemporaneous record — written in ink, dated, never erased, with errors corrected by single-line strikethrough only.
  • Every notebook entry must include: date, objective, all materials with lot numbers, the exact procedure and deviations, raw observations, calculations, and conclusions.
  • Standard Operating Procedures (SOPs) eliminate procedure-to-procedure variation and must be followed as written — deviations must be documented.
  • Every prepared solution must be labelled immediately with name, concentration, date, expiry, and preparer.
  • Equipment records (calibration, maintenance, daily checks) demonstrate that instruments were fit for purpose when data was generated.
  • GLP, GMP, and GCP are separate standards applying to different phases of product development — GLP covers non-clinical laboratory studies.
