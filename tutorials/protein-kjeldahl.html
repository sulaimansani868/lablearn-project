Protein Estimation by the Kjeldahl Method

Principle and overview

The Kjeldahl method, developed by Johan Kjeldahl in 1883, is the internationally recognised reference method for determining the total nitrogen content of organic materials. Because protein contains a relatively constant proportion of nitrogen (~16% by mass), measuring total nitrogen and multiplying by a conversion factor gives the crude protein content.

The method proceeds in three sequential phases: digestion (converting all organic nitrogen to ammonium sulphate using concentrated sulfuric acid at high temperature), distillation (releasing ammonia as a gas by adding alkali, then trapping it in a boric acid receiver), and titration (measuring the absorbed ammonia by acid-base titration).

Overall reaction sequence
Digestion: Organic N + H₂SO₄ (conc, Δ) → (NH₄)₂SO₄ Distillation: (NH₄)₂SO₄ + 2NaOH → Na₂SO₄ + 2NH₃↑ + 2H₂O Absorption: NH₃ + H₃BO₃ → NH₄⁺ + H₂BO₃⁻ Titration: H₂BO₃⁻ + H⁺ (HCl) → H₃BO₃ + Cl⁻

What the Kjeldahl method measures

The Kjeldahl method measures total organic nitrogen — not protein nitrogen specifically. The result is called crude protein because non-protein nitrogenous compounds (urea, nucleic acids, amino sugars, alkaloids) are also detected and included in the result. This is generally acceptable for food analysis, but researchers must be aware that adulteration with nitrogen-rich non-protein compounds (notably the melamine scandal in 2008) will give falsely elevated crude protein figures.

The method does not detect nitrate or nitrite nitrogen — oxidised forms of nitrogen are not converted to ammonium under standard digestion conditions.

Equipment and reagents

Equipment
  • Kjeldahl digestion flasks (250 mL or 500 mL, long-necked borosilicate glass)
  • Digestion block or fume hood with Kjeldahl digestion rack
  • Kjeldahl distillation apparatus (steam distillation unit)
  • 500 mL conical flasks for receiver
  • Burette (50 mL, Class A) and stand
  • Analytical balance (4 d.p.)
  • Graduated pipettes and bulbs
Reagents
  • Concentrated sulfuric acid (H₂SO₄, 98%, analytical grade)
  • Kjeldahl catalyst tablets or mixture: K₂SO₄ (raises boiling point) + CuSO₄ (catalyst) + selenium or mercury (accelerator)
  • 40–50% NaOH solution (concentrated, for distillation)
  • 4% boric acid receiver solution containing mixed indicator (methyl red + bromocresol green)
  • 0.1 mol/L HCl (standardised) for titration
  • Distilled water

Safety requirements

⛔ Critical safety warnings

Concentrated sulfuric acid (98%) is one of the most corrosive laboratory chemicals. It causes deep, slow-healing burns on contact with skin, is strongly exothermic when mixed with water, and produces toxic sulfur dioxide fumes during digestion. Full PPE is mandatory for the entire digestion phase: acid-resistant gloves, lab coat, face shield, and closed-toe shoes. All digestion work must be performed in a fume hood. Concentrated NaOH causes severe burns and reacts exothermically with water. Add slowly during distillation setup. Never add water to concentrated NaOH — always add NaOH solution to water.

  • Work in a dedicated fume hood for all digestion and distillation steps.
  • Have a neutralising agent (sodium bicarbonate solution for acid spills; dilute acetic acid for alkali spills) and an eyewash station immediately accessible.
  • Dispose of digested waste according to your laboratory’s hazardous waste policy — the digested solution contains sulfuric acid, heavy metal catalyst, and ammonium salts.
  • Mercury-containing catalysts are now largely replaced by selenium or copper catalysts in modern laboratories due to environmental regulations. Verify your catalyst type before disposal.

Phase 1 — Digestion

Digestion procedure
1
Weigh the sample accurately into a dry Kjeldahl flask. Typical amounts: 0.5–2.0 g for food samples with ~10–20% protein; 0.1–0.5 g for high-protein samples (>50%). Record the exact mass.
2
Add catalyst. Place one or two Kjeldahl catalyst tablets (or the equivalent catalyst mixture) into the flask.
3
Add concentrated H₂SO₄. In the fume hood, carefully add 15–25 mL of concentrated sulfuric acid. Swirl gently to wet all the sample. Always add acid to sample, never sample to acid.
4
Heat cautiously. Place the flask on the digestion block at low heat initially to prevent frothing and spattering. Gradually increase temperature until the mixture boils gently. At high temperature the digest should be colourless or pale yellow — a dark brown colour indicates incomplete digestion.
5
Digest until clear. Continue heating for 30–60 minutes after the solution becomes colourless and clear (typically 2–4 hours total for food samples). This ensures complete conversion of all organic nitrogen to ammonium sulphate.
6
Cool completely to room temperature before proceeding. Hot concentrated sulfuric acid is extremely dangerous to transfer.
A clear, pale yellow or colourless digest indicates complete digestion. A persistent dark colour means nitrogen-containing material remains — continue digestion.

Phase 2 — Distillation

Distillation procedure
1
Prepare the receiver flask. Pipette 25–50 mL of 4% boric acid indicator solution into a 500 mL conical flask. The mixed indicator (methyl red + bromocresol green) gives a purple/grey colour in acid, changing to green when ammonium borate forms.
2
Transfer the cooled digest to the distillation apparatus. Rinse the Kjeldahl flask three times with small amounts of distilled water and add all rinse water to the distillation flask.
3
Add concentrated NaOH. Slowly add 40–50% NaOH (typically 50–80 mL) down the side of the flask to make the digest strongly alkaline. An excess of alkali is essential to ensure complete conversion of ammonium to free ammonia. The solution should turn dark brown and the flask becomes very hot.
4
Connect to distillation apparatus. Ensure the delivery tube tip is submerged below the surface of the boric acid in the receiver flask. This traps all released ammonia.
5
Distil for 3–5 minutes or until at least 150–250 mL of distillate has collected. Verify distillation is complete by testing a drop of distillate on pH paper — it should be neutral (pH 7) when all ammonia has transferred.
6
Lower the receiver flask away from the delivery tube before turning off the heat to prevent back-siphoning.
The receiver solution turns green/blue-green as ammonium borate accumulates. This colour change confirms that ammonia was successfully trapped and is ready for titration.

Phase 3 — Titration

Titration procedure
1
Fill the burette with standardised 0.1 mol/L HCl. Record the initial burette reading.
2
Titrate the green receiver solution with HCl. As acid is added, the indicator changes from green/blue-green toward grey, then to a definite pink/purple at the endpoint.
3
Approach the endpoint with half-drops. The endpoint is a faint, persistent pink/purple colour lasting 30 seconds.
4
Record the final burette reading. Calculate the titre volume: Vₜ (mL) = final − initial.
5
Run a blank in parallel — the same procedure without sample. The blank titre (Vᵇ) accounts for nitrogen impurities in reagents. All titres must be corrected: V (corrected) = Vₜ − Vᵇ.
Perform at least three titrations. Titres should agree within 0.1 mL. Use the mean of concordant results.

Calculation of crude protein content

Kjeldahl calculation — step by step
Step 1: Moles of HCl used = C(HCl) × Vₜ(L) Step 2: Moles of NH₃ = moles of HCl (1:1 reaction) Step 3: Moles of N = moles of NH₃ Step 4: Mass of N (g) = moles of N × 14.01 Step 5: % N = (mass of N / sample mass) × 100 Step 6: % Crude Protein = % N × F
F = nitrogen-to-protein conversion factor (see table below) C(HCl) = concentration of HCl used for titration (mol/L) Vₜ = corrected titre volume (litres) = (Vₜ − Vᵇ) / 1000
Worked example
1
Sample mass = 1.0532 g wheat flour. C(HCl) = 0.1000 mol/L. Titre = 18.45 mL. Blank titre = 0.20 mL. Corrected titre = 18.25 mL = 0.01825 L.
2
Moles HCl = 0.1000 × 0.01825 = 0.001825 mol = moles N
3
Mass of N = 0.001825 × 14.01 = 0.02557 g
4
% N = (0.02557 / 1.0532) × 100 = 2.428%
5
% Crude Protein = 2.428 × 5.70 (wheat factor) = 13.84%
The wheat flour sample contains 13.84% crude protein.

Blank correction and standards

Always run a reagent blank (same procedure, no sample) and subtract its titre from all sample titres. The blank corrects for nitrogen impurities in the catalyst, acid, and water. A blank titre exceeding 0.5 mL indicates reagent contamination — replace the reagents.

Run a certified reference material (e.g. EDTA, which has a known nitrogen content of 9.589%) with every batch as a positive control to verify the entire procedure from digestion through titration. Recovery should be 98–102%.

Nitrogen-to-protein conversion factors

Food/sample typeConversion factor (F)
General proteins (default)6.25
Wheat and most cereals5.70
Rice5.95
Milk and dairy products6.38
Meat and fish6.25
Soy and legumes6.25
Groundnuts (peanuts)5.46
Gelatin5.55

The factor 6.25 (the most widely used default) is derived from the assumption that protein contains 16% nitrogen by mass: 100/16 = 6.25. Food-specific factors exist because the actual nitrogen content of different proteins varies slightly. Using 6.25 for milk (which has a true factor of 6.38) slightly underestimates dairy protein content.

ErrorEffectPrevention
Incomplete digestion (dark colour remains)Falsely low nitrogen — underestimate proteinContinue heating; ensure catalyst and sufficient acid
Loss of ammonia during distillationFalsely low resultSubmerge delivery tube; check all connections for leaks
Back-siphoning at end of distillationLoss of distillate; contaminationLower receiver before switching off heat
No blank correctionFalsely high result (reagent nitrogen included)Always run a blank and subtract
Unstandardised HClSystematic error in all resultsStandardise HCl against primary standard (Na₂CO₃) before use
Using wrong conversion factorSystematic over- or under-estimationUse the food-specific factor from reference tables

Key takeaways

What to remember from this tutorial

  • The Kjeldahl method measures total organic nitrogen and converts to crude protein using a food-specific factor. It does not distinguish protein nitrogen from non-protein nitrogen.
  • Three phases: Digestion (H₂SO₄ + catalyst + heat → ammonium sulphate) → Distillation (NaOH releases NH₃, trapped in boric acid) → Titration (standardised HCl measures trapped NH₃).
  • Full PPE and fume hood are mandatory for all digestion and distillation work. Concentrated H₂SO₄ and 50% NaOH are both extremely corrosive.
  • Always run a reagent blank and subtract its titre. Run a certified reference material with every batch.
  • % Crude Protein = % N × F. Use F = 6.25 for general proteins; use food-specific factors (e.g. 5.70 for wheat, 6.38 for dairy) where applicable.
  • Standardise your HCl against a primary standard before use. Unstandardised titrant gives systematic error in all results.
