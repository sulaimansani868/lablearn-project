Micropipette Technique: Selection, Calibration, and Use

Why pipetting technique matters

The micropipette is the most frequently used instrument in a biochemistry or clinical laboratory, and pipetting error is one of the most common sources of inaccuracy in quantitative analysis. A systematic error of just 2% in every pipetting step compounds across multiple transfers — a protocol with five pipetting steps could deliver a final result that differs from the true value by 10% or more, purely due to technique.

This tutorial covers every variable that affects pipetting accuracy: choosing the correct pipette and tip, setting the volume, pre-wetting, aspiration angle and depth, dispensing technique, and the difference between forward and reverse pipetting. It also covers how to perform a gravimetric calibration check and how to maintain a micropipette in working condition.

Micropipette types and volume ranges

Air displacement micropipettes are the standard type used in most laboratories. They aspirate liquid by creating a partial vacuum above an air column, which pushes liquid up into the tip. The volume is set by adjusting the piston stroke length.

Positive displacement vs air displacement

For viscous liquids (glycerol, blood, serum), volatile solvents (ethanol, chloroform, acetone), or radioactive samples, positive displacement pipettes are preferable. In these, the piston contacts the liquid directly with no air gap, eliminating errors caused by vapour pressure, viscosity, and aerosol formation. The tips (called capillaries or pistons) are disposable and liquid-specific.

Selecting the right pipette

The most important rule of pipette selection: always work in the upper 65–100% of the pipette’s volume range. Pipettes are calibrated and most accurate near their maximum volume. Using a P1000 to transfer 50 μL introduces far greater error than using a P200 for the same volume.

Selecting the right pipette for the volume
✔
Transfer 800 μL → use P1000 (volume is 80% of range — excellent accuracy)
✔
Transfer 150 μL → use P200 (volume is 75% of range — good accuracy)
✔
Transfer 8 μL → use P10 or P20 (volume is 80% or 40% of range)
✘
Transfer 50 μL with a P1000 → volume is only 5% of range — highly inaccurate, avoid
✘
Transfer 5 μL with a P200 → volume is only 2.5% of range — extremely inaccurate, never do this
When in doubt: choose the smallest pipette whose maximum volume exceeds your target volume.

Choosing the correct tip

Always use tips specifically designed for your pipette brand and model. Universal tips often have a loose fit that creates air leaks, causing under-aspiration. A correctly seated tip creates an airtight seal when pressed firmly onto the shaft.

  • Standard tips: For aqueous solutions. Available in filtered and non-filtered versions.
  • Filtered tips (with aerosol barrier): Mandatory when pipetting nucleic acids, pathogens, radioactive material, or any sample that must not contaminate the pipette barrel. The filter prevents aerosol and liquid from reaching the piston.
  • Low-retention tips: Coated with a hydrophobic material that minimises liquid adhesion. Important for viscous samples, very small volumes (under 10 μL), and expensive reagents.
  • Wide-bore tips: Larger opening prevents shearing of cells, genomic DNA, or other fragile samples during aspiration.
⛔ Never use a tip beyond its volume rating

Using a P20 tip on a P1000 pipette and aspirating 500 μL causes the tip to partially collapse inward under suction, delivering far less than the set volume and potentially damaging the pipette seal. Always match tip range to pipette range.

Setting the volume

On most micropipettes, volume is set by rotating a thumb-wheel or volume adjustment knob. The display shows the set volume in three digits.

  • Always set the volume by approaching from below — turn upward to your target rather than downward to it. This removes backlash from the adjustment mechanism and ensures the piston is in the correct position.
  • Never set a volume above the pipette’s maximum or below its minimum. This overextends or underextends the piston, damaging the internal spring and O-ring seals.
  • Read the volume display carefully — on a P200, the display reads in μL (e.g. “200” = 200 μL). On a P1000, the bottom digit represents 10 μL (e.g. “100” = 1000 μL, “050” = 500 μL).

Pre-wetting the tip

The most frequently overlooked step in pipetting, and one of the biggest sources of systematic under-delivery, is failing to pre-wet the tip.

When a dry tip aspirates liquid for the first time, some of the liquid evaporates into the air column above it, and the air itself absorbs some vapour. This causes the liquid volume in the tip to be slightly less than the set volume. Pre-wetting eliminates this effect by saturating the air column with vapour before the real aspiration.

Pre-wetting procedure
1
Attach a fresh tip and depress the plunger to the first stop.
2
Immerse the tip into the liquid and aspirate the full set volume.
3
Dispense the liquid back into the source container.
4
Repeat this aspiration and dispense once or twice more.
The tip is now pre-wetted. Proceed with the actual aspiration for transfer. This is especially critical for volumes below 50 μL, volatile solvents, and viscous liquids.

Forward pipetting technique

Forward pipetting (also called standard pipetting) is used for aqueous solutions of normal viscosity. It is the default method for most laboratory work.

Forward pipetting — step by step
1
Attach the tip by pressing firmly onto the pipette shaft. Use the tip box, not your fingers — finger-pressing introduces contamination and rarely achieves an airtight seal.
2
Pre-wet the tip as described above, especially for volumes under 100 μL.
3
Depress the plunger to the first stop (the soft stop before resistance increases). Do not push to the second stop yet.
4
Immerse the tip into the liquid at the correct depth (see the Angle and Depth section below). Keep the pipette vertical or tilted slightly — never more than 20° from vertical.
5
Release the plunger slowly and smoothly to aspirate the liquid. A rapid release creates turbulence and draws in less liquid than set. Pause 1 second before withdrawing the tip.
6
Withdraw the tip from the liquid by sliding it along the wall of the container to remove any droplet clinging to the outside.
7
Position the tip against the wall of the receiving vessel at a slight angle. Touch the tip to the wall — this helps complete delivery of the last drop.
8
Depress to the first stop to deliver the liquid. Wait 1 second. Then depress to the second stop (blow-out) to expel the last drop from the tip.
9
Remove the tip while still depressing to second stop. Slide tip up the wall before releasing the plunger to avoid re-aspiration of liquid.
Eject the tip using the ejector button — never remove tips by hand to avoid contamination and sharps injury risk.

Reverse pipetting technique

Reverse pipetting is used for viscous liquids (glycerol, serum, blood, detergent solutions), foaming liquids (SDS solutions, Triton X-100), and very small volumes (under 10 μL). It consistently delivers more accurate volumes with difficult samples than forward pipetting.

The key difference: in reverse pipetting, you aspirate more than the set volume (by pressing to the second stop first), then dispense only to the first stop, leaving excess liquid in the tip.

Reverse pipetting — step by step
1
Attach tip. Set the desired volume.
2
Depress the plunger to the second stop (full blow-out position) before immersing the tip.
3
Immerse the tip and release the plunger slowly. This aspirates more than the set volume.
4
Withdraw the tip from the liquid.
5
Position tip against the wall of the receiving vessel and depress only to the first stop. The set volume is delivered.
6
Do not press to the second stop — this would expel the excess and over-deliver.
7
Discard the tip with the residual liquid still inside.
The excess liquid in the tip acts as a reservoir that prevents air from being included in the delivered volume — which is why this method works better for viscous and foaming samples.
Figure 1 — Micropipette angle and immersion depth: correct technique vs common errors
✓ Correct (0–20°): pipette held nearly vertical, tip immersed 2–5 mm below the liquid surface. Hydrostatic pressure is negligible and no liquid contacts the outside of the tip.   ✗ Over-tilted (>45°): hydrostatic pressure adds liquid beyond the set volume and the tilted angle may allow liquid to flow toward the barrel.   ✗ Too shallow: the tip is not fully submerged; air is aspirated with the liquid, causing under-delivery.   ✗ Too deep: liquid wicks up the outside of the tip by capillary action and is carried into the receiving vessel as extra volume, causing over-delivery. Correct immersion depth: 2–3 mm for P2–P20; 3–6 mm for P100–P200; 6–10 mm for P1000.

Angle, immersion depth, and aspiration speed

Three physical factors — the angle you hold the pipette, how deeply you immerse the tip, and how fast you release the plunger — directly affect the accuracy of every aspiration.

Calibration check by gravimetry

ISO 8655 defines the standard method for verifying micropipette accuracy: weigh distilled water delivered at the set volume and convert mass to volume using the known density of water at the measurement temperature.

Gravimetric calibration formula
Volume (μL) = Mass delivered (mg) / Density of water at T (g/mL)
Density of water: 0.9982 g/mL at 20°C | 0.9970 g/mL at 25°C | 0.9957 g/mL at 30°C Use a 4-decimal-place analytical balance. 1 mg = 1 μL water (approximately at 20°C).
Gravimetric check procedure — P200 at 200 μL
1
Allow the pipette, water, and balance to equilibrate to room temperature (20–25°C) for 30 minutes. Record the temperature.
2
Tare a clean, capped microcentrifuge tube (1.5 mL) or weighing vessel on the analytical balance.
3
Pre-wet the tip with distilled water 3 times. Aspirate 200 μL and dispense into the tared vessel. Record the mass.
4
Repeat 10 times without changing the tip (using the same pre-wetted tip throughout). Record all 10 masses.
5
Convert each mass to volume. Calculate the mean and coefficient of variation (CV%).
Acceptable accuracy for a P200 at 200 μL: mean within ±0.8% of set volume. CV% (imprecision) below 0.3%. If outside these limits, send the pipette for professional servicing.

Maintenance and care

  • Ejector mechanism: Press the tip ejector button rather than pulling the tip off by hand. Pulling puts lateral stress on the shaft and misaligns the O-ring seal over time.
  • Exterior cleaning: Wipe the barrel and shaft with 70% ethanol on a lint-free wipe weekly, or immediately after contact with biohazardous material. Do not spray ethanol directly onto the pipette — this may force liquid into the piston mechanism.
  • Decontamination: Most modern pipettes can be autoclaved in whole or in part. Check the manufacturer’s guidance — some components must be removed first. The barrel must be clean and dry before autoclaving.
  • Servicing: Send pipettes for professional calibration and service at least annually, or more frequently in high-throughput laboratories. The service technician will replace O-rings, springs, and seals, and issue a calibration certificate.
  • Storage: Store pipettes upright in a pipette stand — never on their side or inverted. Storing horizontally allows liquid contamination in the tip to migrate into the barrel.
  • Never: Do not set the volume beyond the maximum or below the minimum. Do not use a P1000 tip on a P200 pipette. Do not aspirate corrosive solvents without the appropriate chemical-resistant pipette model.
Ergonomics and repetitive strain

Repeated pipetting with a thumb-actuated pipette is a significant cause of repetitive strain injury (RSI) in laboratory workers. Use a pipette with a comfortable plunger force, take regular breaks, alternate hands where possible, and consider electronic pipettes for high-volume repetitive work. RSI from pipetting is a recognised occupational health issue and should be reported to a supervisor if it occurs.

Key takeaways

What to remember from this tutorial

  • Always work in the upper 65–100% of the pipette’s volume range. Using a large pipette for a small volume is one of the most common and easily avoided sources of error.
  • Pre-wet every tip before the first aspiration, especially for volumes below 100 μL, volatile solvents, and viscous liquids. This saturates the air column and prevents systematic under-delivery.
  • Use forward pipetting for normal aqueous solutions. Use reverse pipetting for viscous, foaming, or very small-volume samples.
  • Hold the pipette vertical or within 20° of vertical. Immerse the tip only 2–10 mm depending on pipette size. Release the plunger slowly and smoothly.
  • Touch the tip to the wall of the receiving vessel during dispense. Press to the first stop, pause, then press to the second stop for blow-out.
  • Always use filtered tips when working with nucleic acids, pathogens, or expensive reagents. Match tip volume range to pipette range.
  • Perform gravimetric calibration checks regularly. Send for professional servicing at least once per year.
