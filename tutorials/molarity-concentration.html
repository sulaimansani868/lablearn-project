Molarity, Molality & Normality

Introduction

When you prepare a reagent solution in the laboratory, you need a precise way to describe how much solute is dissolved in how much solution or solvent. Chemists use several different concentration expressions depending on what property matters most for the application at hand.

The three most important are molarity, molality, and normality. They are frequently confused by students because they look similar, but each serves a distinct purpose. This tutorial explains each one clearly with worked examples, and tells you exactly when to reach for each in your work.

Molarity (M)

Molarity is by far the most commonly used concentration unit in analytical chemistry, biochemistry, and general laboratory work. It expresses the number of moles of solute per litre of solution.

Definition
M = n / V(L)
Where n = moles of solute, V = total volume of solution in litres

Molarity is a solution-based measurement — the volume refers to the final volume of the entire solution, not just the solvent added.

Calculating molarity in practice

To find moles from mass, divide the mass by the molar mass (molecular weight) of the compound:

Full formula
M = (mass in grams / molar mass) / V(L)
Molar mass (g/mol) is the sum of atomic masses of all atoms in the molecule
Worked example — preparing 500 mL of 0.1 M NaCl
1
Molar mass of NaCl = 23.00 (Na) + 35.45 (Cl) = 58.44 g/mol
2
Moles needed = 0.1 mol/L × 0.500 L = 0.05 mol
3
Mass = 0.05 mol × 58.44 g/mol = 2.922 g
4
Weigh 2.922 g NaCl, dissolve in ~400 mL distilled water, then make up to 500 mL in a volumetric flask.
Result: 0.1 mol/L NaCl solution
⚠ Common mistake

Never add 500 mL of water to the solute — the final volume must be 500 mL. Adding a fixed volume of solvent changes the total volume unpredictably due to solvation effects. Always make up to the final volume in a calibrated volumetric flask.

Molality (m)

Molality expresses the number of moles of solute per kilogram of solvent — not solution. This is a subtle but critical difference.

Definition
m = n / mass of solvent (kg)
Note the lowercase m — and that this is per kg of SOLVENT, not solution
Worked example — molality of glucose solution
1
36 g of glucose (MW = 180.16 g/mol) dissolved in 200 g of water.
2
Moles of glucose = 36 / 180.16 = 0.1999 mol
3
Mass of solvent = 200 g = 0.200 kg
4
Molality = 0.1999 / 0.200 = ≈ 1.0 mol/kg
Result: 1.0 m glucose solution (1.0 molal)

Molarity vs molality — the key difference

Molarity changes with temperature because liquid volumes expand and contract. If you prepare a 1 M solution at 20°C, its concentration at 50°C is slightly lower because the volume of liquid has increased.

Molality is temperature-independent because it is based on mass, which does not change with temperature. This makes it essential for calculations involving boiling point elevation, freezing point depression, and osmotic pressure.

Normality (N)

Normality expresses the number of equivalents of solute per litre of solution. It was historically the dominant unit in titrimetry and is still commonly used in acid-base and redox analysis.

Definition
N = (equivalents of solute) / V(L)
Or equivalently: N = M × n-factor

Understanding equivalents and the n-factor

The n-factor (equivalence factor) depends on the reaction type:

  • Acids/bases: number of H⁺ or OH⁻ ions donated or accepted per molecule
  • Redox reactions: number of electrons transferred per molecule
  • Precipitation: number of charged equivalents per formula unit
Worked example — normality of H₂SO₄
1
H₂SO₄ is a diprotic acid — it donates 2 H⁺ ions per molecule. Therefore the n-factor = 2.
2
A 1 M H₂SO₄ solution has a normality of: N = 1 × 2 = 2 N
3
For HCl (monoprotic, n-factor = 1): 1 M HCl = 1 N
Key: 1 N of any acid always neutralises 1 N of any base — regardless of the specific compound.
Note on normality

Normality is reaction-specific. The same H₂SO₄ solution has N = 2 in an acid-base reaction (donates 2 H⁺) but could have a different N in a redox context. Always specify the reaction type when stating a normality value.

When to use each unit

As a practical rule, reach for each unit in these situations:

Practical guide
  • Molarity (M) — use for almost everything: reagent preparation, standard solutions, dilutions, enzyme assays, cell culture media. Default choice.
  • Molality (m) — use whenever temperature varies significantly, or when calculating boiling/freezing point changes, osmolality, or working with non-aqueous solvents.
  • Normality (N) — use for acid-base and redox titrations where equivalence simplifies calculations. Common in alkalinity, acidity, hardness, and COD/BOD measurements.

Key takeaways

What to remember from this tutorial

  • Molarity (M) = moles of solute per litre of solution — the most widely used unit.
  • Molality (m) = moles of solute per kg of solvent — temperature-independent.
  • Normality (N) = equivalents per litre — reaction-specific, used in titrations.
  • M and N are both solution-based and change with temperature; m does not.
  • When making up a molar solution, always dilute to the final volume, never add a fixed volume of solvent.
  • N = M × n-factor; for monoprotic acids, N = M.
