Laboratory Safety: Chemical Hazard Classes

Why chemical safety matters

Laboratory accidents are almost always preventable. Most incidents result from insufficient hazard knowledge, improper PPE, or poor storage practices — not freak events. Understanding chemical hazard classification is the foundation of safe laboratory work: you cannot protect yourself from a hazard you do not understand.

This tutorial introduces the Globally Harmonised System (GHS), teaches you to read a Safety Data Sheet (SDS), and covers the most important chemical hazard classes you will encounter in a university or industrial laboratory.

⛔ Before you touch anything

Always read the SDS for every chemical before using it for the first time. Know the location of the nearest eyewash station, emergency shower, fire extinguisher, and first-aid kit before starting work.

The GHS system

The Globally Harmonised System of Classification and Labelling of Chemicals (GHS) is an internationally agreed framework for communicating chemical hazards. Adopted by the United Nations in 2003, it replaces the many different national systems that existed previously, ensuring that chemical labels and safety data sheets carry consistent, internationally understood information.

GHS classifies hazards into two broad categories:

  • Physical hazards — flammable, explosive, oxidising, pressurised, self-reactive, etc.
  • Health and environmental hazards — acute toxicity, carcinogenicity, reproductive toxicity, aquatic hazard, etc.

GHS pictograms

GHS uses nine standardised diamond-shaped pictograms — a black symbol on a white background inside a red diamond border. You will find one or more of these on every chemical label and at the top of Section 2 of the SDS.

Two signal words are used on labels: DANGER (more severe hazard categories) and WARNING (less severe). Always take DANGER-labelled chemicals more seriously and be conservative in your risk assessment.

Reading a Safety Data Sheet (SDS)

Every chemical produced commercially must have a Safety Data Sheet. SDSs are standardised into 16 sections under GHS. The most important sections for day-to-day lab work are:

SectionTitleKey information
1IdentificationProduct name, supplier, emergency contact
2Hazard identificationGHS classification, pictograms, signal word, hazard statements (H codes), precautionary statements (P codes)
4First-aid measuresWhat to do if inhaled, ingested, or skin/eye contact occurs
5Fire-fighting measuresExtinguishing agents, special fire hazards
6Accidental releaseSpill containment and cleanup procedures
7Handling and storageSafe handling, incompatible materials, storage conditions
8Exposure controls / PPEOEL/TWA values, required protective equipment
11Toxicological informationLD50, LC50, carcinogenicity, reproductive effects
📋 H and P codes

Hazard statements (H codes) describe the nature and degree of the hazard — e.g. H301 = "Toxic if swallowed". Precautionary statements (P codes) describe measures to minimise or prevent adverse effects — e.g. P260 = "Do not breathe dust/fumes/vapours/spray". Learn the H codes of your most frequently used chemicals.

Key hazard classes explained

Flammable chemicals

A flammable liquid has a flash point below 60°C. The flash point is the lowest temperature at which vapours above the liquid can ignite. Flammable chemicals must be kept away from all ignition sources, including open flames, hot plates without an explosion-proof motor, and static electricity from synthetic clothing.

Common flammable chemicals in the lab: ethanol, methanol, acetone, diethyl ether, hexane, chloroform. Note that diethyl ether and THF can form explosive peroxides on prolonged storage — always check the peroxide test date before use.

Corrosive chemicals

Corrosive substances cause irreversible damage to living tissue (skin, eyes) or corrode metal. Strong acids (HCl, H₂SO₄, HNO₃) and strong bases (NaOH, KOH) are the most common. Acid burns are painful but often self-limiting; alkali burns are more insidious because they penetrate deeply and pain may be delayed.

For any acid or base spill on skin, the immediate response is copious water flushing for at least 15 minutes. Do not try to neutralise a skin burn with another chemical — this can cause an exothermic reaction and worsen injury.

Toxic chemicals

Toxicity is classified by route of exposure (oral, dermal, inhalation) and severity. GHS acute toxicity categories 1–3 (labelled DANGER, skull & crossbones) are the most severe. Common laboratory toxins include:

  • Heavy metals: mercury, lead, cadmium, chromium(VI) — cumulative toxins, require careful disposal
  • Cyanide compounds: extremely toxic — dedicated cyanide waste containers, never acidify cyanide solutions
  • Acrylamide: neurotoxin and probable carcinogen — always handle powder in a fume hood with gloves and mask
  • Formaldehyde: irritant, sensitiser, probable carcinogen — use in fume hood, monitor air levels

Oxidising agents

Oxidising chemicals can intensify fires by providing oxygen, causing flammable materials to burn much more vigorously. They must be stored separately from flammables. Common oxidisers: potassium permanganate (KMnO₄), hydrogen peroxide (H₂O₂) at high concentrations, concentrated nitric acid, perchloric acid.

⛔ Perchloric acid warning

Concentrated perchloric acid at elevated temperatures can form shock-sensitive perchlorate salts in fume hood ducts. It must only be used in a dedicated, water-washdown perchloric acid fume hood. Never use a standard fume hood for perchloric acid digestions.

Environmental hazards

Chemicals with the GHS09 aquatic toxicity pictogram (dead tree/fish) are acutely or chronically hazardous to aquatic ecosystems. These must never be poured down the drain. Examples include: organotin compounds, certain biocides, heavy metal salts, polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons (PAHs), and many chlorinated solvents.

Personal protective equipment (PPE)

PPE is your last line of defence — not a substitute for engineering controls and safe practices. Section 8 of the SDS specifies the minimum PPE required.

Glove compatibility

Nitrile gloves are adequate for most aqueous solutions, but not for all organic solvents. Acetone and methylene chloride penetrate nitrile rapidly. Butyl rubber gloves are required for concentrated acids and many organic solvents. Always check the glove manufacturer's chemical resistance chart.

Chemical storage rules

Improper chemical storage is a leading cause of laboratory accidents — not because chemicals explode on the shelf, but because incompatible chemicals stored together can react violently if a container fails. Key rules:

  • Separate acids from bases — store in separate cabinets or with secondary containment
  • Separate flammables from oxidisers — store flammables in approved flammable storage cabinets
  • Separate flammables from corrosives — acids vapours corrode metal containers of solvents
  • Never store chemicals in alphabetical order — nitric acid and nitriles must not be adjacent
  • Store volatile chemicals in a ventilated cabinet or fridge designed for flammable storage (no sparking motor)
  • Label everything with the chemical name, concentration, date prepared, and your initials

Spill response

Know what to do before a spill happens. Spill response depends on the chemical, quantity, and location:

General spill response procedure
1
Assess: Is it a small, manageable spill, or a large release? Is anyone injured? Are there vapours?
2
Evacuate if needed: Large spills, toxic vapours, or fires — evacuate the room or building, alert others, call emergency services.
3
Put on PPE: Nitrile gloves, goggles, lab coat. Respirator if vapours are present.
4
Contain: Use absorbent material (vermiculite, spill kit pads, dry sand — not paper towel) to absorb. Work from the edges inward.
5
Neutralise if appropriate: Acid spills — neutralise with sodium bicarbonate. Alkali spills — neutralise with citric acid or dilute acetic acid. Only if safe to do so.
6
Dispose: Place contaminated material in a sealed chemical waste bag. Label it. Report the incident in the lab safety log.
Key takeaways

What to remember from this tutorial

  • GHS provides globally standardised hazard classification using 9 pictograms and two signal words: DANGER and WARNING.
  • Always read the SDS (especially sections 2, 4, 7, and 8) before handling an unfamiliar chemical.
  • H codes describe hazards; P codes describe precautions. Know the H codes for your common reagents.
  • Never store chemicals alphabetically — always by chemical compatibility class.
  • Glove material matters: nitrile is not suitable for all solvents. Check the manufacturer's compatibility chart.
  • For skin contact with acids or alkalis: flush with water for at least 15 minutes, then seek medical attention.
  • Environmental toxins (GHS09) must never go down the drain — segregate for hazardous waste disposal.
