Blood Glucose Determination: Glucometer and Glucose Oxidase Methods

Introduction and clinical significance

Blood glucose measurement is one of the most frequently performed tests in clinical and biochemistry laboratories. Glucose is the primary energy substrate for all cells, and its concentration in blood is tightly regulated by hormones — principally insulin (which lowers glucose) and glucagon (which raises it). Sustained derangement of glucose homeostasis is the hallmark of diabetes mellitus, a condition with significant global prevalence and serious long-term complications.

Two methods are covered in this tutorial because they serve different purposes and are used in different settings. The point-of-care glucometer gives a rapid result at the bedside or in the ward within 5 seconds and is used for monitoring and screening. The glucose oxidase (GOD-PAP) colorimetric assay is the gold-standard laboratory method used for diagnosis, population studies, and research, offering higher precision and the ability to process large numbers of samples simultaneously.

Diagnosis requires confirmation

A single elevated fasting glucose ≥ 7.0 mmol/L is sufficient for diagnosis only if the patient has symptoms (polyuria, polydipsia, unexplained weight loss). In the absence of symptoms, a second confirmatory test on a different day is required before diagnosing diabetes mellitus (WHO/IDF guidelines).

Glucose in blood without a glycolysis inhibitor falls approximately 7 mg/dL (0.4 mmol/L) per hour at room temperature due to continued red cell metabolism. Serum separated within 30 minutes is acceptable, but fluoride/oxalate (grey top) tubes are mandatory for any sample that will not be analysed within 30 minutes of collection.

Method 1 — Point-of-Care Glucometer Testing

Principle

Modern glucometers use an electrochemical biosensor strip. The test strip contains a dry enzyme layer — either glucose oxidase or glucose dehydrogenase (GDH) — coated on an electrode. When blood is applied, glucose reacts with the enzyme, generating electrons. The meter measures the resulting electrical current (amperometry), which is proportional to the glucose concentration in the sample.

Electrochemical reaction in glucometer strip
Glucose + O₂ → Gluconolactone + H₂O₂ (Glucose oxidase) H₂O₂ → 2H⁺ + O₂ + 2e⁻ (at platinum electrode) Current generated ∝ glucose concentration

Glucometer procedure

Step-by-step glucometer procedure
1
Prepare the meter. Check the strip code on the meter matches the code on the test strip vial. Confirm strips are within expiry date. Check the meter passed its last QC test.
2
Prepare the patient’s finger. Ask the patient to wash hands with warm water and soap and dry thoroughly. Cold, dirty, or moist fingers give inaccurate results. Do not use alcohol swabs immediately before the prick — allow the site to dry completely if used.
3
Insert a fresh strip into the meter. The meter turns on automatically on most devices.
4
Perform the finger prick. Use a single-use lancet. Prick the side of the fingertip (not the pad — less nerve density, less painful). Wipe away the first drop of blood (it may be diluted with tissue fluid). Allow a second drop to form naturally — do not squeeze hard, as this dilutes the sample with interstitial fluid.
5
Apply blood to the strip. Touch the drop to the sample window of the strip (do not smear). The meter automatically draws the sample by capillary action. Most modern meters require 0.5–1.0 μL.
6
Read the result. The meter displays glucose in mmol/L or mg/dL (depending on settings) within 5–10 seconds. Record the result with time and date.
7
Dispose of the lancet immediately into a sharps container. Eject the strip safely.
Document the result, time since last meal, and any relevant clinical notes. Results outside the meter’s linear range (typically <1.1 or >33.3 mmol/L) display as LO or HI and require laboratory confirmation.

Quality control for glucometers

Glucometers are medical devices subject to analytical quality requirements. External quality control must be performed at the start of every shift, after opening a new vial of strips, if the meter is dropped or damaged, and whenever a result seems inconsistent with the patient’s clinical state.

  • Apply the manufacturer’s supplied control solutions (low and high levels) exactly as if they were patient samples.
  • Results must fall within the stated acceptable range printed on the control solution vial. If either level fails, do not use the meter for patient testing until the fault is identified and corrected.
  • Common QC failure causes: expired strips, expired control solution, wrong code on meter, strips stored outside temperature range, or a damaged meter.
  • Document all QC results — pass or fail — in the QC logbook with date, time, strip lot number, and operator name.

Method 2 — Glucose Oxidase Colorimetric Method (GOD-PAP)

Principle and reaction chemistry

The GOD-PAP (Glucose Oxidase — Phenol Aminophenazone) method is an enzymatic colorimetric assay. Glucose is oxidised by glucose oxidase, generating hydrogen peroxide. The hydrogen peroxide then reacts with 4-aminoantipyrine (4-AAP) and a phenol derivative in the presence of peroxidase (POD) to form a coloured quinoneimine dye. The absorbance of this dye is measured at 500–510 nm and is directly proportional to the glucose concentration.

GOD-PAP reaction sequence
Step 1: Glucose + O₂ + H₂O → Gluconic acid + H₂O₂ (catalysed by Glucose Oxidase) Step 2: 2 H₂O₂ + 4-AAP + Phenol → Quinoneimine dye + 4 H₂O (catalysed by Peroxidase) Absorbance measured at 500–510 nm ∝ [Glucose]
The deeper the pink/red colour, the higher the glucose concentration.

GOD-PAP laboratory procedure

Reagents required
  • GOD-PAP working reagent (commercial kit or prepared from components)
  • Glucose standard solution (e.g. 5.55 mmol/L or 100 mg/dL — check kit)
  • Distilled water (reagent blank)
  • Patient serum or plasma (fluoride tube preferred)
  • Test tubes or microplate wells
  • UV-Vis spectrophotometer set to 500–510 nm
  • Waterbath at 37°C
  • Calibrated micropipettes
GOD-PAP assay procedure — tube method
1
Label three tubes: Blank (B), Standard (S), and Test (T). For multiple samples, label one Test tube per patient sample.
2
Pipette as follows into each tube:
Blank: 1,000 μL working reagent + 10 μL distilled water
Standard: 1,000 μL working reagent + 10 μL glucose standard
Test: 1,000 μL working reagent + 10 μL patient serum/plasma
3
Mix well by gentle vortexing or inversion. Avoid foaming.
4
Incubate at 37°C for exactly 10 minutes (or at room temperature for 20 minutes if a waterbath is unavailable — check your kit’s protocol). Colour develops during this period.
5
Read absorbance at 500–510 nm within 60 minutes of incubation. Zero the spectrophotometer with the blank. Record the absorbance of the standard (Aₛ) and each test sample (Aₜ).
Calculate glucose concentration using the formula below. Colour is stable for approximately 60 minutes — read promptly.

Calculation

Glucose concentration calculation
[Glucose] = (Aₜ / Aₛ) × [Standard]
Aₜ = absorbance of test sample Aₛ = absorbance of standard [Standard] = known concentration of glucose standard (e.g. 5.55 mmol/L) Example: Aₜ = 0.312, Aₛ = 0.356, [Standard] = 5.55 mmol/L [Glucose] = (0.312 / 0.356) × 5.55 = 4.86 mmol/L

If a calibration curve is available (multiple standards of known concentration), plot absorbance vs concentration and read off the patient value from the curve. This is more accurate than single-point calculation and accounts for any non-linearity at high concentrations.

Linearity limit

Most GOD-PAP kits are linear up to approximately 22–33 mmol/L (400–600 mg/dL). If the test sample absorbance exceeds that of the highest standard, dilute the sample with saline (1:2 or 1:5), repeat the assay, and multiply the result by the dilution factor.

Interpreting and reporting results

  • Always report units: State whether the result is in mmol/L or mg/dL. To convert: mg/dL × 0.0555 = mmol/L; mmol/L × 18.02 = mg/dL.
  • Note fasting status: A random glucose of 8.0 mmol/L is not diagnostic of diabetes; a fasting glucose of 8.0 mmol/L is. Always record whether the sample was fasting, and for how long.
  • Report sample type: Whether the result is from capillary blood (glucometer), venous plasma, or whole blood affects interpretation.
  • Critical values: Most laboratories define critical glucose values requiring immediate clinical notification — commonly glucose < 2.5 mmol/L or > 25 mmol/L. Know your laboratory’s critical value policy.
Key takeaways

What to remember from this tutorial

  • Use fluoride/oxalate (grey top) tubes for glucose if the sample will not be analysed within 30 minutes. Glucose falls ~7 mg/dL per hour at room temperature without inhibitor.
  • Glucometers use electrochemical detection and give results in seconds. They are suitable for monitoring but not for diagnosis. CV is 3–8%; haematocrit is a significant interference.
  • Perform glucometer QC at every shift start, after opening a new strip vial, and after any meter incident. Document all QC results.
  • The GOD-PAP method uses glucose oxidase and peroxidase to produce a pink dye measured at 500–510 nm. Calculate: [Glucose] = (Aₘ / Aₛ) × [Standard].
  • Haemolysis and ascorbic acid cause falsely low results in both methods. Maltose causes falsely high glucometer results only with GDH-PQQ strips — clinically important in patients on peritoneal dialysis.
  • Fasting venous plasma in a fluoride tube is the gold-standard sample for glucose diagnosis. Always document fasting status and sample type with the result.
