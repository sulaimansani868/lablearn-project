Safe Handling of Acids, Bases, and Corrosive Reagents

How this tutorial differs from chemical hazard classification

The Laboratory Safety: Chemical Hazard Classes tutorial covers how to read GHS pictograms, interpret Safety Data Sheets, and understand the classification of chemical hazards. This tutorial is its practical companion — it describes the actual physical procedures for handling corrosive chemicals at the bench: how to dilute them, how to dispense them safely, what PPE to wear for each specific reagent, and what to do when something goes wrong.

PPE selection for corrosive chemicals

Standard laboratory PPE — gloves, lab coat, and safety spectacles — is the minimum for dilute corrosive solutions. For concentrated acids and bases, this minimum is not adequate.

TaskGlovesEye/face protectionAdditional
Handling dilute solutions (<1 mol/L)Nitrile (minimum)Safety spectaclesLab coat
Preparing 1–6 mol/L solutions from concentrateThick nitrile or neopreneChemical splash goggles (not spectacles)Lab coat; work in fume hood
Handling concentrated H₂SO₄, HNO₃, HCl, NaOHThick neoprene or butyl rubber; double-gloveFace shield over splash gogglesAcid-resistant apron; fume hood; closed shoes
Large-volume transfers (>1 L)As aboveFace shieldAcid-resistant apron; bottle carrier; second person present
Glove selection matters — nitrile is not sufficient for all acids

Standard nitrile gloves (0.1–0.2 mm thick) offer only limited protection against concentrated acids. For concentrated H₂SO₄, concentrated HNO₃, or prolonged contact with any concentrated acid, use thick neoprene or butyl rubber gloves. Check the glove manufacturer’s chemical resistance chart for your specific reagent and concentration — breakthrough times vary significantly between glove materials.

Dilution technique — always add acid to water

This rule is absolute and non-negotiable: always add concentrated acid or base to water, never water to acid or base.

When concentrated sulfuric acid is mixed with water, an enormous amount of heat is released instantaneously (∆H ≈ −880 kJ/mol). If water is added to acid, the small amount of water sits on top of the dense acid and the localised heat causes it to boil explosively, spattering hot concentrated acid in all directions. Adding acid slowly to a large volume of water dilutes each drop immediately so the heat is dissipated gradually.

Correct dilution procedure for concentrated H₂SO₄
1
Put on full PPE: thick gloves, face shield over goggles, acid-resistant apron. Work in the fume hood.
2
Place a large borosilicate glass beaker containing the required volume of distilled water on a magnetic stirrer. Start the stirrer.
3
Measure the required volume of concentrated H₂SO₄ in a resistant measuring cylinder. Do not use graduated pipettes for concentrated acid — use a measuring cylinder or a calibrated glass acid-dosing bottle.
4
Add the acid slowly and carefully to the water, pouring down the inside wall of the beaker, with stirring. Add in small portions if preparing a large volume — allow the heat to dissipate between additions.
5
Allow the solution to cool to room temperature before transferring to a storage bottle. The solution will be hot — do not cap or stopper until cooled.
The same rule applies to NaOH, KOH, and other concentrated bases. Always dissolve alkali pellets in water, not the reverse.

Dispensing from large containers

  • Never carry large bottles of concentrated acid or base by the neck. Always use both hands and a bottle carrier (safety tote). Carry at waist height, close to the body — never above shoulder height.
  • Use an acid-resistant pump or a bottle-top dispenser to transfer from Winchester-size (2.5 L) stock bottles rather than pouring directly. This eliminates the risk of spillage from tipping heavy bottles.
  • Never use your mouth to pipette. Use a bulb filler, pipette pump, or motorised pipette controller. This applies to all liquids, but is especially important for corrosive chemicals.
  • When opening a bottle of concentrated HCl or ammonia, tilt the cap slightly and allow pressure to equalise before removing fully. Cold concentrate poured into a warm room can produce a momentary pressure surge.

Fume control

Any open work with concentrated HCl, concentrated ammonia, concentrated HNO₃, glacial acetic acid, or concentrated HF must be performed inside a fume hood with the sash at the working height marked on the hood. The fume hood is not optional for these reagents — even brief exposure to their vapours at working concentrations can cause significant respiratory irritation and long-term damage.

  • Before using the fume hood, verify the airflow indicator shows adequate extraction. If the indicator shows low or no airflow, do not use the hood — report it for service immediately.
  • Keep your head outside the sash plane even when working inside the hood. The sash provides splash protection; it does not protect your head if it is inside the hood enclosure.
  • Never store large quantities of corrosive chemicals inside a fume hood — this blocks airflow and reduces protection. The hood is a work surface, not a storage area.

Never store acids and bases on the same shelf. A bottle failure resulting in acid-alkali contact produces a violent exothermic reaction. Store in dedicated, labelled, ventilated cabinets with appropriate secondary containment (acid-resistant trays to catch spills).

HF + calcium gluconate (antidote, not reagent)Forms calcium fluoride — the correct first-aid response to HF burns

First aid for chemical exposures

⛔ Immediate actions — time is critical

Skin contact: Remove contaminated clothing immediately. Flush affected area with large amounts of flowing water for a minimum of 15–20 minutes. Do not attempt to neutralise the chemical on the skin — this generates heat and wastes time. Seek medical attention for any significant acid or base burn.

Eye contact: Irrigate immediately with eyewash station for at least 15 minutes, holding eyelids open. Remove contact lenses if easily removable — do not delay irrigation to remove them. Seek emergency medical attention after irrigation.

Inhalation of fumes: Move to fresh air immediately. If symptoms persist (coughing, chest tightness, burning sensation), seek medical attention. Do not return to the contaminated area.

HF (hydrofluoric acid) skin contact: Unique emergency — fluoride ion penetrates deeply and causes systemic toxicity and potentially fatal hypocalcaemia. Apply calcium gluconate gel to the affected area immediately and summon emergency medical services.

Spill response

Acid or base spill procedure
1
Alert others in the area and evacuate if the spill is large or involves fuming acids. Do not attempt to clean a large spill alone.
2
Put on PPE before approaching the spill: thick gloves, face shield, acid-resistant apron.
3
Neutralise: Cover acid spills with sodium bicarbonate (baking soda) or dry sand to absorb and neutralise. Cover base spills with citric acid powder or dry sand. Do not add water directly to fuming acid spills — this generates steam and fumes.
4
Absorb and collect the neutralised material using a dustpan or spill kit sorbent pads. Place in a labelled waste bag.
5
Clean the area with water and a mop. Rinse twice. Dispose of all materials as chemical waste.
6
Report the incident in the laboratory accident logbook. For large spills, significant personal exposure, or any medical treatment, complete a formal incident report form.

Waste disposal

Corrosive waste must not be poured down the sink without treatment, and must never be mixed with incompatible waste streams. Requirements vary by institution and jurisdiction, but general principles apply:

  • Dilute acids and bases can generally be neutralised (to pH 6–8) and then disposed of down the drain with large amounts of water — check your local regulations first.
  • Concentrated acids, nitric acid waste, and chromic acid waste require collection in labelled, sealed corrosive waste containers for disposal by a licensed hazardous waste contractor.
  • Never mix acid and base waste in the same container — neutralisation is exothermic and can cause the container to burst.
  • Label every waste container with: chemical content, approximate concentration, date first opened, and responsible person.
Key takeaways

What to remember from this tutorial

  • Always add acid to water, never water to acid. This applies to all concentrated acids and to concentrated NaOH. The rule exists because dilution is exothermic — acid added to water dilutes immediately; water added to acid causes explosive boiling.
  • Standard nitrile gloves are not adequate for concentrated acids. Use thick neoprene or butyl rubber gloves and a face shield (not just goggles) for all work with concentrated corrosives.
  • All work with fuming or volatile corrosives (concentrated HCl, HNO₃, ammonia, glacial acetic acid) must be performed inside a functioning fume hood with the sash at the correct height.
  • Never store acids and bases on the same shelf. Segregate oxidising acids (HNO₃, HClO₄) from all organic material and flammables.
  • For skin or eye contact: flush with water for 15–20 minutes immediately — do not try to neutralise the chemical on skin. For HF exposure, apply calcium gluconate gel and call emergency services.
  • Neutralise spills with sodium bicarbonate (acid) or citric acid (base) before cleaning. Report all incidents in the accident logbook.
