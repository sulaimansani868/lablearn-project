Basic Glassware Handling

Types of laboratory glass

Not all glass used in a laboratory is identical. Different glass compositions offer different resistance to thermal shock, chemical attack, and mechanical stress. Using the wrong type of glass for a task is a common source of breakage and injury.

Glass typeCommon brand namesKey propertiesLimitations
Borosilicate glassPyrex, Duran, KimaxLow coefficient of thermal expansion; excellent thermal shock resistance; good chemical resistance; the standard for laboratory glasswareAttacked by hydrofluoric acid (HF); slow attack by hot concentrated alkali
Soda-lime glass(often unlabelled)Cheap; adequate for non-heated applications (specimen jars, storage bottles)Poor thermal shock resistance — shatters readily when heated; not suitable for direct flame or autoclave
Quartz (fused silica)Vitreosil, GE 124Very high temperature resistance (up to ~1650°C); transparent to UV — essential for UV spectroscopy cuvettesVery expensive; fragile; attacks slowly by alkali
Borosilicate (low expansion)Supremax, RasothermHigher temperature resistance than standard borosilicate; used for high-temperature applicationsLess widely available; not required for routine laboratory work
Identifying borosilicate from soda-lime glass

In most laboratories, if glassware is labelled Pyrex, Duran, or Kimax, it is borosilicate. Unlabelled thick-walled glass items — common in storage jars, bottles, and inexpensive beakers — are often soda-lime and should not be heated directly. When in doubt, assume soda-lime and do not expose to direct heat or thermal shock.

Inspection before every use

Damaged glassware is one of the most common causes of laboratory injury. A crack, chip, or star fracture is a stress concentrator — it dramatically reduces the load the glass can bear and makes the vessel prone to sudden, unpredictable shattering, especially when subjected to heat, vacuum, or pressure.

Pre-use inspection checklist
  • Hold the item up to a light source and inspect all surfaces — look for chips, cracks, star fractures (spider-web cracks), and etching from previous chemical exposure.
  • Run a finger lightly around the rim of beakers and flasks — a chip that is invisible under poor lighting is often detectable by touch.
  • Check stoppers and ground glass joints for chips or cracks, and verify the stopper fits correctly — a mismatched stopper can shatter the neck under pressure.
  • Inspect round-bottom flasks at the base and at the neck join — these are high-stress areas most prone to cracking.
  • Any item with damage must be removed from service immediately. Do not return it to the cabinet — mark it visibly (e.g. with red tape labelled "DAMAGED") and place in the broken glass disposal container.
⚠ A scratched or etched flask can fail under vacuum

Any flask used under vacuum (rotary evaporation, vacuum filtration, vacuum distillation) must be free of surface etching, scratches, and cracks. A scratched surface creates stress concentration points that can cause implosion under even moderate vacuum. Always use round-bottom flasks — not Erlenmeyer flasks — for vacuum applications, and wrap them in mesh safety netting for added protection.

Carrying and supporting glassware

Most glassware breakage in the laboratory results not from chemical stress or thermal shock but from simple mechanical impact — dropping, knocking over, or carrying glassware improperly. Safe carrying habits reduce breakage incidents dramatically.

ItemCorrect carrying methodDo not
Beaker (empty or liquid-containing)Grasp body with one hand; support the base with the other if containing liquidCarry by the rim; carry multiple stacked beakers with liquid in the lower ones
Erlenmeyer flaskGrasp below the neck with one hand; support the base with the other for heavy flasksCarry by the neck alone, especially when containing liquid
Round-bottom flaskCup the body with both hands, or use a clamp; always place on a cork ring when setting downPlace directly on a flat bench without a cork ring — it will roll and fall
Volumetric flask (filled)Grip the neck firmly with one hand; support the base with the other; carry uprightCarry inverted or tilted; carry by the neck alone
Separating funnel (filled)Hold body with one hand; keep stopcock pointed away from people; carry short distances onlyCarry long distances filled; leave on a stand without the stopper secured
Large carboys / Winchester bottlesUse a dedicated carrier or trolley; always two people for bottles >2.5 L of hazardous liquidCarry by the neck; tilt at sharp angles; carry alone if significantly heavy

Using clamps and stands correctly

When glassware must remain in place unattended — during a reflux, distillation, or overnight reaction — it must be properly supported in a clamp and retort stand setup. A poorly clamped flask that falls during an unattended run can cause fire, chemical release, and injury.

Clamping glassware safely
  • Clamp at the neck of flasks, not at the body — clamping the body concentrates stress on the curved glass wall.
  • Use rubber-lined or PTFE-lined clamps — bare metal clamps can crack glass by concentrated point stress.
  • Do not overtighten — a clamp should grip firmly but not deform the neck. The glass should not creak or creak when tightened.
  • Ensure the retort stand is stable and will not tip. A stand supporting a heated flask or a flask containing hazardous liquid should be secured or weighted at the base.
  • Tall assemblies (reflux condensers, distillation columns) should be secured at multiple points to prevent swaying.
  • Always assemble glassware apparatus from the bottom up and disassemble from the top down.

Heating glassware safely

Thermal shock occurs when a large, rapid temperature difference is imposed on glass. The outer surface expands or contracts faster than the interior, creating stress that can cause immediate shattering or introduce invisible micro-cracks that cause delayed failure.

Heating situationSafe practice
Heating a beaker or flask on a hot plateAlways use a ceramic-centred wire gauze between the hot plate and the glass; never place glass directly on the heating element surface
Heating a test tube in a Bunsen flameUse borosilicate test tubes only; heat gently in the upper part of the flame; keep moving — do not hold stationary in the flame; point the opening away from people
Transferring hot glassware to a cold surfaceAllow hot glass to cool on a ceramic tile or wooden board — never place on a cold metal bench or in water
Pouring hot liquid into glasswarePre-warm the receiving vessel gently; do not pour boiling liquid into cold glass
Adding cold liquid to a hot flaskCool the flask first, or warm the liquid first — never add cold liquid to a flask much hotter than the liquid's temperature
Using an autoclaveUse borosilicate glass only; fill no more than two-thirds capacity; loosen caps before autoclaving; use heat-indicator tape to confirm sterilisation
The hot glass problem — it looks exactly like cold glass

Unlike metal, hot glass gives no visible indication of its temperature. A beaker that was recently on a hot plate looks identical to a cold one. Always assume any glassware that has been near a heat source is hot until you have confirmed otherwise, either by waiting an adequate time or by testing cautiously with the back of your hand held near (not touching) the glass. Place a "HOT" warning sign next to cooling glassware so others do not pick it up.

Stoppers, ground joints, and stopcocks

Ground glass joints connect sections of a glassware assembly (condensers, separating funnels, flasks) with a precise, interchangeable fit. Stoppers and stopcocks must be handled correctly to prevent both leakage and seizure (when the joint becomes permanently stuck).

ComponentCare and handling
Ground glass jointsKeep clean and free of grit; use the minimum necessary amount of appropriate grease for vacuum or stopcock applications; clean with acetone after use; do not mix joint sizes (sizes are standardised: 14/23, 19/26, 24/29, 29/32 — the numbers indicate taper diameter and length in mm)
Glass stoppersNever leave a glass stopper in a bottle containing concentrated alkali — NaOH etches the joint and fuses it permanently; use plastic stoppers for alkali storage; label stoppers so they are returned to their matching vessel
PTFE stopcocks (burettes)No grease required; clean regularly; check for leaks by filling and observing the tip; replace if PTFE becomes deformed or leaks
Glass stopcocks (separating funnels)Grease lightly and sparingly with appropriate stopcock grease; rotate to distribute evenly; if a glass stopcock seizes, apply gentle warming and solvent soaking — never force

Assembling glassware apparatus

Multi-piece glassware apparatus (distillation, reflux, extraction setups) must be assembled systematically to ensure all joints are secure, all clamps are in place, and all connections are correct before heating or applying vacuum begins.

General assembly checklist for glassware apparatus
1Inspect every piece of glassware before assembling — a crack discovered after the setup is complete means disassembling the entire apparatus.
2Build from the bottom up: secure the lowest component to the clamp and stand first, then add each piece upward.
3Check that all ground glass joints are correctly matched in size and seated fully — a partially inserted joint leaks and can disengage under pressure.
4Ensure that the assembly is never a closed system when heated — there must always be a path for vapour or gas to escape. A sealed heated system will pressurise and explode.
5Check that all clamps are secure but not overtightened, and that the entire assembly is stable — nudge the stand gently to confirm it will not tip.
6Connect any water supply (for condensers) and confirm flow before applying heat. A condenser without cooling water will allow vapours to pass uncondensed — a fire or exposure hazard.

Handling broken glass

Broken glass must never be handled with bare hands, regardless of how large or apparently safe the fragments appear. Small glass slivers are nearly invisible and will penetrate skin without any sensation. The consequences — infection, nerve damage, or embedded foreign body — can be severe.

Safe broken glass procedure
  • Never pick up glass fragments with bare hands. Use thick puncture-resistant gloves, tongs, forceps, or a dustpan and brush.
  • For large fragments: use tongs to collect the main pieces first, then use a damp paper towel to collect smaller fragments and slivers, and finally use tape to pick up the finest particles.
  • If chemicals were in the broken vessel, the cleanup must also address the chemical hazard — consult the SDS and use appropriate PPE before beginning cleanup.
  • Dispose of all broken glass — including fragments that appear harmlessly small — in the dedicated rigid sharps or broken glass container, never in a standard plastic waste bin (where it can injure waste handlers).
  • Report every broken glass incident to your supervisor, even if no injury occurred. Record it in the incident log.
  • If a cut occurs: remove gloves, flush the wound with clean water for several minutes, apply pressure, and seek first aid. Report as an incident — a glass sliver may be embedded even if not immediately visible.

Disposal of glassware

Damaged, worn, or obsolete glassware must be disposed of correctly. Glass in the general waste stream is a severe hazard to waste collection and sorting personnel.

Glassware conditionDisposal route
Clean, undamaged glassware no longer neededRetain for reuse; offer to other laboratory groups; donate to teaching labs
Cracked, chipped, or damaged glasswareBroken glass bin (rigid, puncture-proof, labelled) — never in general waste
Glassware contaminated with radioactive materialRadioactive waste stream only — consult radiation safety officer
Glassware contaminated with biological material (BSL-2+)Autoclave first, then broken glass bin; or into a dedicated biohazardous sharps container
Glassware contaminated with hazardous chemicalsDecontaminate according to chemical SDS; then broken glass bin if damaged; chemical waste disposal if the residue is the primary hazard
Soda-lime glass (non-lab origin)Broken glass bin — do not mix with borosilicate for recycling unless confirmed
Key Takeaways

What to remember from this tutorial

  • Use borosilicate glass (Pyrex, Duran) for all heating applications — soda-lime glass shatters under thermal stress.
  • Inspect every piece of glassware for chips, cracks, and star fractures before use — remove damaged items from service immediately.
  • Support glassware correctly: beakers by the body, flasks by the neck and base, round-bottom flasks on cork rings. Never carry glassware by the neck alone when it is filled.
  • Thermal shock is caused by rapid temperature changes — never place hot glass on a cold surface, and never add cold liquid to a hot flask.
  • Hot glass looks identical to cold glass — always treat recently heated glassware as hot until confirmed otherwise.
  • Never leave a glass stopper in a bottle of concentrated alkali — it will seize permanently.
  • Broken glass must always go into a rigid broken glass container, never into general waste or soft plastic bags.
  • Build glassware assemblies from the bottom up; never create a sealed heated system — vapour pressure will cause an explosion.
