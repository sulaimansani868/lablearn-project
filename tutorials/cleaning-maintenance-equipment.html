Cleaning and Maintenance of Lab Equipment

Why cleaning matters

A residue left in a flask from a previous experiment is a contaminant in the next one. In quantitative work, microgram-level contamination can shift absorbance readings, invalidate calibration curves, or introduce false signals. In biological work, even trace amounts of a previous sample can cause cross-contamination that ruins an entire experiment or produces a false positive result.

Proper cleaning also extends the life of expensive equipment, maintains accurate calibrations, and is a fundamental element of Good Laboratory Practice (GLP). Clean equipment is not just a courtesy to the next user — it is a requirement for producing valid data.

Cleaning glassware

The standard cleaning sequence for laboratory glassware moves from rinsing with the solvent of the contaminant, through a detergent wash, to multiple rinses with tap water and a final rinse with distilled or deionised water.

Standard glassware cleaning sequence
1Pre-rinse: Immediately after use, rinse with a small amount of the solvent that dissolves the residue (water for aqueous solutions; acetone or ethanol for organic residues). Do not allow residues to dry — dried material is far harder to remove.
2Detergent wash: Wash with laboratory-grade detergent (e.g. Decon 90, Extran) using a bottle brush appropriate for the vessel shape. Use warm water. Scrub all inner surfaces — do not miss the neck of flasks.
3Tap water rinse (×3): Rinse thoroughly at least three times with tap water to remove all detergent traces. Detergent residue can disrupt pH measurements, interfere with enzyme assays, and destabilise emulsions.
4Deionised/distilled water rinse (×3): The final three rinses must use deionised or distilled water (DI/DW) to remove ionic contaminants from tap water. This is critical for all analytical work.
5Inspection: Clean glass is hydrophilic — water should drain as a continuous sheet, leaving no droplets or patches. If water beads up, grease or organic contamination remains. Repeat the cleaning process.
The water sheeting test

After rinsing clean glassware with DI water, invert it and observe how the water drains. Truly clean borosilicate glass will sheet evenly with no water droplets clinging to the surface. Any beading indicates residual contamination — most commonly grease or skin oils from handling without gloves. Repeat the detergent wash step.

Selecting the right brush

Drying glassware correctly

How glassware is dried depends on what it will be used for. Incorrect drying introduces contaminants or causes damage.

MethodWhen to useWhen NOT to use
Air drying inverted on a rackMost routine glassware; preferred for calibrated wareWhen urgently needed; humid environments where drying is slow
Oven drying (110–120°C)Non-calibrated glassware (beakers, flasks, test tubes)Volumetric flasks, burettes, measuring cylinders — heat causes expansion and permanent volume change
Acetone rinse + air dryRapid drying of small volumes; non-critical glasswareIf residual acetone would interfere with the next experiment
Nitrogen or clean air blowRapid drying of analytical-grade glasswareWhen nitrogen could introduce moisture or particulates from a poorly maintained line
⚠ Never oven-dry volumetric glassware

Volumetric flasks, pipettes, and burettes are calibrated at 20°C. Heating them in an oven permanently changes their volume through thermal expansion — often irreversibly. The error introduced may be large enough to invalidate any subsequent concentration measurements. Always air-dry calibrated glassware.

Removing stubborn residues

Some residues resist standard detergent washing and require specialised cleaning solutions. Always use the least aggressive method first and escalate only if needed.

Residue typeCleaning approachCaution
Grease / silicone stopcock greaseSoak in acetone or chloroform; then standard detergent washDispose of organic solvent waste correctly
Inorganic salts (e.g. CaCO₃, metal hydroxides)Soak in dilute HCl (5–10%); rinse thoroughlyWear gloves and goggles; work in fume hood
Organic dyes and indicatorsSoak in dilute bleach or concentrated HNO₃ (chromic acid alternative)Do not mix bleach with acidic cleaners; HNO₃ requires fume hood
Protein residuesSoak in 1% SDS solution or enzymatic cleanerRinse very thoroughly — SDS inhibits many biological assays
RNA/DNA contaminationRinse with 0.1 M NaOH or use commercial RNase-away solutionFor molecular biology glassware — standard cleaning is insufficient
Radioactive contaminationDesignated decontamination protocol per your institution's radiation safety officerNever handle contaminated items without specific training
Chromic acid — historical practice to avoid

Chromic acid (concentrated H₂SO₄ + K₂Cr₂O₇ or CrO₃) was historically used as an aggressive glassware cleaner. It is now deprecated in most laboratories because hexavalent chromium (Cr VI) is a potent carcinogen and a regulated environmental contaminant. Do not use chromic acid. Modern alternatives (Nochromix, dilute HNO₃, alkaline detergents) are effective and far safer.

Sterilisation methods

Cleaning removes visible contamination. Sterilisation eliminates viable microorganisms. Both steps may be required for biological work, but they are distinct processes — clean first, then sterilise.

MethodMechanismSuitable forNot suitable for
Autoclave (121°C, 15 psi, 15–20 min)Moist heat; protein denaturationGlass, metal, heat-stable plastics, liquids, mediaPolystyrene, many plastics, heat-sensitive reagents
Dry heat oven (160–180°C, 2 h)Oxidative destructionGlassware, metal instruments, powders, oilsPlastics, rubber, heat-sensitive items
UV irradiationDNA damageSurfaces in biosafety cabinets; pipettesItems with UV-opaque coatings; not a substitute for autoclave
70% ethanol or isopropanolProtein denaturationExternal surfaces; bench wipe-down before useItems that must be sterile internally; spores
Filter sterilisation (0.22 µm)Physical exclusion of bacteriaHeat-sensitive solutions (antibiotics, serum)Virus-free preparation (requires smaller pore); particulate-heavy solutions (clog)

Cleaning analytical instruments

Analytical instruments require careful, instrument-specific maintenance. Never use solvents or cleaning agents near electronic components unless the manufacturer specifies it. Always consult the instrument manual before cleaning.

InstrumentRoutine cleaningKey notes
Analytical balanceWipe pan with a soft cloth or brush; remove spilled material with a dry brush firstNever use liquid directly on the pan mechanism; close draft shield after use
pH meter electrodeRinse electrode with DI water after each use; store in KCl storage solutionNever wipe the bulb membrane — blot gently with lint-free tissue only
MicropipetteWipe exterior with 70% ethanol; autoclave the tip cone if contaminatedRefer to manufacturer guide — many pipettes can be disassembled for cleaning
Spectrophotometer cuvettesRinse immediately with appropriate solvent; never use abrasive cleanersQuartz cuvettes for UV work; never scratch optical faces; store dry
Hot plate / magnetic stirrerWipe surface with damp cloth when cool; remove stir bar residue with DI waterEnsure fully cool before cleaning; do not immerse in water
Water bathDrain and clean monthly with mild bleach solution; rinse thoroughlyChange water regularly; add anti-algal tablet for long-term use; keep lid closed

Preventive maintenance

Preventive maintenance (PM) is scheduled upkeep performed to prevent equipment failure rather than to respond to it. Many laboratory instruments have manufacturer-specified PM schedules. Following these schedules is essential for maintaining calibration status and warranty validity.

Common preventive maintenance tasks
  • Analytical balance: Clean draft shield glass weekly; check levelling bubble and adjust feet; run span calibration with certified weights monthly.
  • pH meter: Calibrate with at least two buffer standards before each use; condition a new electrode in KCl for 24 hours before first use.
  • Micropipettes: Calibration verification every 6 months using gravimetric method (weigh water dispensed); annual full service by qualified technician.
  • Centrifuge: Inspect rotor for corrosion and cracks before every use; clean rotor chamber after any spillage; lubricate brush contacts per manual.
  • Water bath: Change water and clean reservoir monthly; verify temperature accuracy with a calibrated thermometer weekly.
  • Autoclave: Run biological indicator (spore test) monthly; verify temperature and pressure gauges against calibrated instruments.

Storage of clean equipment

Clean glassware must be stored in a way that prevents re-contamination before use. Dusty or poorly stored clean glassware must be re-rinsed with DI water before use.

  • Store inverted on a clean rack or peg board to prevent dust settling inside.
  • Cover the openings of stored flasks with clean aluminium foil or glass stoppers — not rubber stoppers for long-term storage (rubber degrades and contaminates).
  • Store volumetric flasks with their glass stoppers loosely inserted — never grease-sealed during routine storage.
  • Keep burettes vertical, drained, and with taps open (to prevent tap seizing), in a protective cover.
  • Store cleaned glassware in a designated clean area, physically separated from dirty or used items.
  • Label any glassware that has been treated with special cleaning agents (e.g. acid-washed, silanised) to prevent mix-up with routine glassware.
Key Takeaways

What to remember from this tutorial

  • Clean glassware immediately after use — dried residues are exponentially harder to remove.
  • The standard sequence: pre-rinse with appropriate solvent → detergent wash → ×3 tap water → ×3 DI water.
  • Use the water sheeting test: clean glass drains evenly with no droplets. Beading means contamination remains.
  • Never oven-dry volumetric glassware — heat permanently changes calibrated volumes.
  • Chromic acid is deprecated — use Nochromix, dilute HNO₃, or alkaline detergents instead.
  • Cleaning removes visible contamination; sterilisation kills microorganisms — both steps may be needed, in that order.
  • Preventive maintenance schedules must be followed and recorded — document all calibration and maintenance in the equipment log.
