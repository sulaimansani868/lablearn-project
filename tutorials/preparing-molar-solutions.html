Preparing Molar Solutions: Step-by-Step Bench Guide

Introduction

Understanding molarity is one thing — actually preparing an accurate molar solution at the bench is another. Many errors in analytical and biochemical work originate not in the calculation but in the physical preparation step: weighing incorrectly, dissolving directly in the flask, or making up to volume before the solution has cooled.

This tutorial covers the complete bench procedure for preparing a molar solution from both solid and liquid reagents. It assumes you already understand the molarity formula. If not, read the Molarity, Molality & Normality tutorial first.

How this tutorial differs from the concentration theory tutorial

The Molarity, Molality & Normality tutorial teaches what molarity is and how to calculate it. This tutorial teaches how to physically make the solution. The distinction matters because a correct calculation applied with poor technique still produces an inaccurate solution.

Equipment and materials required

Checklist before you start
  • Analytical balance (4 decimal places, ±0.1 mg)
  • Calibrated volumetric flask (Class A) — correct volume for your preparation
  • Weighing boat or glass weighing dish
  • Spatula and stirring rod
  • Wash bottle with distilled or deionised water
  • Glass funnel (fits neck of your volumetric flask)
  • Beaker for initial dissolution (250 mL or 500 mL)
  • Pasteur pipette or dropper for final volume adjustment
  • Desiccator with fresh silica gel
  • Oven (if reagent requires drying)
  • Permanent marker and label tape

Step 1 — Calculate the mass required

Before touching any glassware, calculate exactly how much reagent you need using the molarity formula:

Formula
mass (g) = Molarity (mol/L) × Volume (L) × Molar Mass (g/mol)
Example: 0.1 mol/L NaCl in 500 mL → 0.1 × 0.500 × 58.44 = 2.922 g
Worked example — 0.5 mol/L NaOH, 250 mL
1
Molar mass of NaOH = 23.00 + 16.00 + 1.01 = 40.00 g/mol
2
Volume in litres = 250 mL ÷ 1000 = 0.250 L
3
Mass = 0.5 × 0.250 × 40.00 = 5.000 g
Weigh out 5.000 g of NaOH pellets.

Use the Molarity Calculator to verify your calculation before weighing.

Step 2 — Dry and weigh the reagent

Many reagents absorb moisture from the atmosphere. Using a damp reagent gives you less pure compound per gram than you calculated, producing a solution that is less concentrated than intended.

  • Dry the reagent in an oven at the temperature specified in its SDS (typically 100–150°C for 1–2 hours) unless it is a liquid or thermally sensitive.
  • Transfer to a desiccator and cool to room temperature before weighing. Hot weighing is inaccurate and stresses the balance.
  • Tare the balance with the weighing boat in place. Add the reagent carefully until you reach your target mass.
  • Record the exact mass shown on the balance display — for example, 5.003 g rather than 5.000 g. Use this actual mass to calculate the true molarity of your solution.
Important — weigh the actual mass, not the target

Never assume you achieved exactly the target mass and report nominal molarity. Weigh as close as practical, then calculate exact molarity from the actual mass. This is the correct approach in quantitative analysis.

Step 3 — Dissolve the reagent in a beaker

Always dissolve the reagent in a separate beaker — never directly in the volumetric flask. Dissolution can be exothermic (NaOH releases significant heat), and a hot solution in a volumetric flask expands, so making up to the graduation mark while warm gives the wrong volume when cool.

⛔ Never dissolve directly in the volumetric flask

Heat from dissolution causes the solution to expand. If you fill to the mark while hot, the actual volume at room temperature will be less than intended, making your solution more concentrated than calculated. Always dissolve in a beaker first.

  • Transfer the weighed reagent from the weighing boat to a clean beaker.
  • Add approximately 60% of the target volume of distilled water.
  • Stir with a glass rod until fully dissolved. For slow-dissolving reagents, gentle warming on a hotplate is acceptable — but cool to room temperature before transferring.

Step 4 — Quantitative transfer to the volumetric flask

Every molecule of the weighed reagent must reach the volumetric flask. Any loss at this stage makes your solution less concentrated than calculated. The technique of transferring everything without loss is called quantitative transfer.

Quantitative transfer procedure
1
Place a clean glass funnel in the neck of the volumetric flask.
2
Pour the dissolved solution through the funnel into the flask.
3
Rinse the beaker with a small volume of distilled water (10–20 mL) and add the rinse through the funnel.
4
Repeat the rinse at least three times. Each rinse must go into the flask.
5
Rinse the funnel and stirring rod into the flask as well.
All three rinses ensure no measurable amount of solute remains in the beaker or funnel.

Step 5 — Make up to volume

This step requires patience and care. The goal is to have the bottom of the meniscus sitting exactly on the graduation mark, viewed at eye level, at room temperature.

  • After the quantitative transfer and rinses, add distilled water until the solution level is approximately 1 cm below the graduation mark.
  • Stopper the flask and invert gently several times to mix. Allow to return to room temperature if any residual warmth remains.
  • Using a dropper or Pasteur pipette, add water drop by drop until the bottom of the meniscus sits precisely on the graduation line. View at eye level — looking down causes parallax error and overfilling.
  • If you accidentally overshoot the mark, the solution must be discarded and prepared again. There is no way to correct an overfilled volumetric flask.
Reading the meniscus correctly

For aqueous solutions, the meniscus curves downward (concave). Read from the bottom of the curve. Keep your eye exactly at the level of the graduation mark. For solutions that do not wet glass (e.g. some organic solvents), the meniscus curves upward (convex) — read from the top.

Step 6 — Mix, label, and store

  • Stopper the flask firmly and invert at least 20 times, rotating the flask between inversions, to ensure complete mixing.
  • Transfer to a clean, dry storage bottle of appropriate material (glass for most solutions; amber glass for light-sensitive solutions).
  • Label immediately with: reagent name, exact concentration (calculated from actual mass), preparation date, expiry date, and your initials.
  • Store according to the reagent's requirements (cool, dark, sealed).

Preparing molar solutions from liquid reagents

Concentrated liquid reagents (HCl, H₂SO₄, acetic acid, ammonia) require a different calculation because you measure volume rather than mass. The molarity of the stock reagent is calculated from its density and percentage purity, then C₁V₁ = C₂V₂ is used to find how much to measure.

Volume of liquid reagent to measure
V₁ (mL) = [M₂ × V₂(L) × MW] / [density × (purity/100) × 1000]
Example: 1 mol/L HCl in 500 mL from 37% HCl (density 1.18 g/mL, MW 36.46): V₁ = (1 × 0.5 × 36.46) / (1.18 × 0.37 × 1000) × 1000 = 41.7 mL
⛔ Always add acid to water — never water to acid

Adding water to concentrated acid, especially sulfuric acid, causes violent spattering due to rapid heat release. Always pour the measured acid slowly into water already in the beaker, with stirring. The same rule applies to concentrated bases.

Key takeaways

What to remember from this tutorial

  • Calculate mass = M × V(L) × MW, then weigh carefully and record the exact mass.
  • Always dry hygroscopic reagents before weighing; cool in a desiccator first.
  • Dissolve in a beaker first — never directly in the volumetric flask.
  • Transfer quantitatively: rinse beaker and funnel at least three times and add all rinses to the flask.
  • Make up to volume drop by drop with a dropper, reading the meniscus at eye level.
  • For liquid reagents: always add acid or base to water, never the reverse.
  • Label with reagent name, actual concentration, date, expiry, and initials before storing.
