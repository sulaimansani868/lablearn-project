Enzyme Activity Assays: Principles and Protocol

Introduction

Enzyme activity assays measure the catalytic activity of enzymes by monitoring the rate at which a substrate is converted to product under defined conditions. Unlike protein concentration assays (which measure how much enzyme is present), activity assays measure how much functional enzyme is present — a distinction that matters whenever enzyme inhibition, denaturation, or post-translational modification may have occurred.

In clinical biochemistry, enzyme activity measurements are essential diagnostic tools. Elevated serum ALT and AST indicate hepatocellular damage; elevated amylase and lipase point to pancreatitis; elevated CK and LDH suggest myocardial or skeletal muscle injury. In research biochemistry, enzyme assays are used to characterise purified proteins, measure enzyme kinetics (Kₘ, Vₘₐₓ), and quantify specific activity during purification.

Enzyme activity units

Key enzyme activity definitions
International Unit (IU or U): amount of enzyme catalysing conversion of 1 µmol substrate per minute under standard conditions. Katal (kat): SI unit — 1 kat = conversion of 1 mol substrate per second. 1 IU = 16.67 nkat Specific activity: Units per mg of total protein (U/mg or µmol/min/mg). Used to track purity during enzyme purification. Specific activity = Total activity (U) / Total protein (mg)
Example: if a sample contains 50 U of enzyme activity and 2.5 mg total protein, specific activity = 50 / 2.5 = 20 U/mg protein.

Critical assay conditions

Enzyme activity is exquisitely sensitive to assay conditions. Small deviations from the optimal conditions produce large changes in measured activity. The following parameters must be controlled precisely and reported alongside every result.

ParameterWhy it mattersTypical control
TemperatureRate doubles approximately every 10°C (Q₁₀ effect); enzyme denaturation above optimumThermostatted waterbath or spectrophotometer cuvette holder at 25°C or 37°C
pHEach enzyme has a narrow optimal pH; ±0.5 pH units may reduce activity by 20–50%Calibrated buffer appropriate to the enzyme; verify pH at assay temperature
Substrate concentrationMust be at Vₘₐₓ (saturating) to ensure zero-order kinetics; sub-saturating [S] gives rate dependent on [S], not enzymeUse [S] ≥ 10 × Kₘ whenever possible
Cofactors and metal ionsMany enzymes require Mg²♠, Zn²♠, NAD♠, PLP etc. for activityInclude required cofactors in assay buffer at specified concentrations
Enzyme concentrationRate must be proportional to [enzyme] — verify by linearity checkDilute sample to fall in linear range; use only the linear portion of the curve
Reaction timeRate must be constant during the measurement window (zero-order kinetics)Confirm linearity of product formation over time before reporting results

Linear range and assay validation

Before reporting enzyme activity, you must confirm that the assay is measuring in its linear range — the region where the rate of reaction is constant over time (time linearity) and where the measured rate is directly proportional to enzyme concentration (concentration linearity).

Checking linearity — two essential tests
1
Time linearity: Run the reaction for longer than usual (e.g. 10 minutes instead of 5). Plot absorbance vs time. Activity should increase linearly — a curve or plateau means the reaction has deviated from zero-order kinetics (substrate depletion or product inhibition). Use only the linear portion for rate calculation.
2
Dilution linearity (sample linearity): Dilute the sample 1:2, 1:4, 1:8, and 1:16. Measure activity in each dilution. The measured activity should halve with each 2-fold dilution (after correcting for dilution). If activity is disproportionately low in undiluted samples, inhibition is present — use the most dilute sample that still gives a measurable rate.
Only results from the linear range are valid. Non-linear results must be reported with a note explaining why and what dilution was used.

Clinical example — ALT (Alanine Aminotransferase) assay

ALT (formerly GPT) catalyses the transamination of alanine and α-ketoglutarate to pyruvate and glutamate. Pyruvate is then reduced to lactate by lactate dehydrogenase (LDH) with concomitant oxidation of NADH to NAD♠. The decrease in NADH absorbance at 340 nm (NADH has strong absorbance at 340 nm; NAD♠ does not) is monitored continuously and is proportional to ALT activity.

ALT coupled reaction (IFCC method)
Alanine + α-Ketoglutarate → Pyruvate + Glutamate (ALT) Pyruvate + NADH + H♠ → Lactate + NAD♠ (LDH, indicator reaction) Measured: Decrease in A₃₄₀ ∝ ALT activity
Temperature: 37°C | pH: 7.15 (phosphate/Tris buffer) | Wavelength: 340 nm Reference range (serum): Male 7–56 U/L; Female 7–45 U/L (IFCC, 37°C)
ALT kinetic assay procedure
1
Pre-warm all reagents (substrate buffer containing L-alanine, α-ketoglutarate, NADH, LDH, and Tris-phosphate buffer) in a 37°C waterbath for 10 minutes.
2
Pipette 1,000 µL of working reagent into a cuvette. Set the spectrophotometer to 340 nm, temperature 37°C (if available), kinetics mode.
3
Add 100 µL of serum sample. Mix by pipetting up and down once. Start the timer immediately.
4
Read absorbance at 340 nm every 60 seconds for 4–5 minutes. A lag period of 1–2 minutes may occur while the coupled reaction equilibrates — use only the linear portion of the absorbance decline.
5
Calculate the rate: ΔA/min = (A₁ − A₂) / time interval between readings (minutes).
Calculate ALT activity using the formula below.
ALT activity calculation
Activity (U/L) = (ΔA/min × Vᵇ × 1000) / (ε × l × Vₛ)
Vᵇ = total assay volume (mL, e.g. 1.1 mL) Vₛ = sample volume (mL, e.g. 0.1 mL) ε = molar absorptivity of NADH at 340 nm = 6.22 L · mmol¹ · cm¹ l = light path length (cm, usually 1.0) 1000 = conversion from mmol to µmol Example: ΔA/min = 0.030, Vᵇ = 1.1 mL, Vₛ = 0.1 mL, ε = 6.22, l = 1.0 Activity = (0.030 × 1.1 × 1000) / (6.22 × 1.0 × 0.1) = 52.9 U/L

LDH assay (Lactate Dehydrogenase)

LDH is measured similarly to ALT — it is an NADH-linked kinetic assay at 340 nm. LDH catalyses the reversible interconversion of lactate and pyruvate. The forward (lactate → pyruvate) direction using NAD♠ as cofactor is favoured for clinical measurement.

LDH reaction (forward, clinical measurement)
Lactate + NAD♠ → Pyruvate + NADH + H♠ (LDH) Measured: Increase in A₃₄₀ ∝ LDH activity Temperature: 37°C | pH: 9.4 (glycine buffer) Reference range (serum): 140–280 U/L (method-dependent)

Note that in the forward direction the absorbance increases at 340 nm (NADH is produced), unlike ALT where it decreases (NADH is consumed). Always verify the direction expected for each assay and confirm your observed signal is consistent with that direction.

Specific activity — purification table

During enzyme purification, specific activity is calculated at each step to track enrichment and yield. A purification table documents the progressive removal of contaminating proteins and the concentration of the enzyme of interest.

Factors affecting enzyme assay results

FactorEffectHow to detect/prevent
HaemolysisFalsely elevated ALT, AST, LDH (erythrocytes contain these enzymes)Inspect sample visually; reject or report haemolysis index; use plasma instead of serum
Sample storage temperatureEnzyme activity decreases on storage; CK and LDH are especially labileAnalyse within 4–8 hours; freeze at −20°C for longer storage; thaw only once
Inhibitors in sampleApparent activity lower than true activityCheck with dilution linearity test; inhibition suspected if diluted sample gives higher specific activity
Substrate depletion during assayRate falls during measurement — non-linear kineticsUse saturating [S]; verify time linearity; dilute sample if rate is too fast
High bilirubin (icteric sample)Spectral interference at 340 nm and 405 nmRun a sample blank (without substrate); subtract from test absorbance
Incorrect temperatureActivity 10–20% per °C difference from stated assay temperatureVerify waterbath or instrument temperature with calibrated thermometer before every session

Quality control for enzyme assays

  • Commercial control sera: Use assayed control sera with established enzyme activity ranges at two levels (normal and abnormal). Run with every analytical batch. Results must fall within the stated acceptable range before patient results are released.
  • Levey-Jennings charts: Plot QC results chronologically. Patterns such as gradual drift (reagent degradation), sudden shift (new reagent lot), or increased scatter (precision problem) are visible on the chart before they cause clinically significant errors.
  • Linearity verification: Verify assay linearity with a linearity panel (serial dilutions of a high-activity sample) when changing reagent lots or after instrument service.
  • Temperature verification: Confirm the cuvette/waterbath temperature with a calibrated probe thermometer at the start of every session. A 1°C error at 37°C introduces approximately 7% error in ALT activity.
Key takeaways

What to remember from this tutorial

  • Enzyme activity (U or IU) measures catalytic function — 1 U converts 1 µmol substrate per minute. Specific activity (U/mg) normalises activity to protein content and tracks purification progress.
  • Kinetic assays measure rate continuously in the linear phase and are more accurate. Endpoint assays stop the reaction at a fixed time and require careful validation that the reaction was linear throughout the incubation.
  • Always confirm time linearity (rate is constant over the measurement window) and sample dilution linearity (rate is proportional to enzyme concentration) before reporting results.
  • ALT and LDH are NADH-linked assays measured at 340 nm (NADH absorbs; NAD♠ does not). ALT consumes NADH (absorbance falls); LDH (forward) produces NADH (absorbance rises).
  • Haemolysis is the most common pre-analytical interference in clinical enzyme assays — erythrocytes are rich in ALT, AST, LDH, and potassium. Always inspect and record the haemolysis index.
  • Run QC control sera with every batch. Monitor with Levey-Jennings charts to detect drift, shift, and precision problems before they affect patient results.
