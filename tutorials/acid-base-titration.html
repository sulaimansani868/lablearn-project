Acid-Base Titration: Technique, Endpoints, and Calculation

Introduction and principle

Titration is a quantitative analytical technique in which a solution of known concentration (the titrant, held in the burette) is gradually added to a solution of unknown concentration (the analyte, held in the conical flask) until the reaction between them is complete. The volume of titrant required to reach this completion point — the equivalence point — is used to calculate the unknown concentration.

In acid-base titration, the reaction is neutralisation: an acid donates protons to a base until stoichiometric equivalence is reached. At the equivalence point, moles of acid equal moles of base (adjusted for the number of protons involved).

Fundamental relationship at equivalence point
N₁ × V₁ = N₂ × V₂
Where N = normality (eq/L) and V = volume (L or mL, consistent units) Also written as: C₁ × V₁ × n₁ = C₂ × V₂ × n₂ where C = molarity and n = number of equivalents per mole

How this tutorial differs from acid-base theory

Understanding that an acid donates protons and calculating a theoretical equivalence point pH is not enough to perform a successful titration. This tutorial covers what happens at the bench: how to fill and read a burette without parallax error, why the half-drop technique matters near the endpoint, what a faint persistent colour change actually looks like, and how to handle a back titration when the analyte is insoluble. These are skills learned at the bench, and this tutorial describes them precisely.

Indicator selection

An indicator is a weak acid or base that changes colour at a specific pH range. The indicator must change colour as close to the true equivalence point as possible. Using the wrong indicator introduces a systematic error called indicator error.

IndicatorColour changepH transition rangeBest used for
PhenolphthaleinColourless → pinkpH 8.2 – 10.0Weak acid vs strong base; strong acid vs strong base
Methyl orangeRed → orange/yellowpH 3.1 – 4.4Strong acid vs weak base (e.g. Na₂CO₃, NH₃)
Methyl redRed → yellowpH 4.4 – 6.2Weak base titrations requiring sharper endpoint
Bromothymol blueYellow → bluepH 6.0 – 7.6Near-neutral equivalence points
Screened methyl orangeRed → greenpH 3.1 – 4.4Sharper endpoint than methyl orange — preferred in many labs
How much indicator to add

Use the minimum amount necessary — typically 2 to 3 drops from a standard dropper (approximately 0.1 mL). Excess indicator consumes titrant at the endpoint (the indicator itself is a weak acid or base), introducing a small but measurable error. Never add indicator to the burette solution.

Equipment and preparation

Equipment checklist
  • 50 mL burette (Class A, graduated in 0.1 mL divisions)
  • Burette clamp and stand
  • 250 mL conical (Erlenmeyer) flask
  • 25 mL or 20 mL calibrated pipette (Class A)
  • Pipette filler (never mouth-pipette)
  • White tile or white paper (placed under flask to help see colour change)
  • Wash bottle with distilled water
  • Indicator solution
  • Funnel for filling burette
  • Beaker for waste titrant

Preparing glassware

  • Rinse the burette with a small volume (5 mL) of the titrant solution — not distilled water. Rinsing with water dilutes the first portion of titrant added. Drain through the tip. Repeat twice.
  • Rinse the pipette with the analyte solution before filling for the same reason.
  • The conical flask does not need rinsing with analyte — you are measuring moles transferred by the pipette, not concentration in the flask. Rinsing the flask with distilled water is fine and does not affect the result.

Standardising your titrant

NaOH and other base solutions absorb CO₂ from the air over time, lowering their effective concentration. Before any quantitative titration, the titrant must be standardised against a primary standard — a substance that is highly pure, stable, has a high molar mass (reducing weighing errors), and reacts stoichiometrically with the titrant.

TitrantPrimary standardMW (g/mol)Indicator
NaOHPotassium hydrogen phthalate (KHP)204.22Phenolphthalein
HCl or H₂SO₄Anhydrous Na₂CO₃105.99Methyl orange or screened methyl orange
HCl or H₂SO₄TRIS (tris(hydroxymethyl)aminomethane)121.14Methyl red
KMnO₄Oxalic acid dihydrate or sodium oxalate126.07 / 134.00Self-indicating (purple to colourless)
Standardising NaOH against KHP
1
Dry KHP at 110°C for 1 hour. Cool in a desiccator.
2
Weigh accurately approximately 0.4–0.5 g of KHP into a clean conical flask.
3
Dissolve in 50 mL of freshly boiled (CO₂-free) distilled water. Add 2–3 drops of phenolphthalein.
4
Titrate with NaOH to a faint, persistent pink colour (30 seconds). Record the volume used.
5
Calculate: C(NaOH) = mass of KHP (g) / (MW of KHP × V of NaOH in litres)
Repeat at least 3 times. The titre values should agree within 0.1 mL. Use the mean of concordant results.

Titration procedure

Standard titration procedure
1
Fill the burette: Close the stopcock. Use a funnel to fill with titrant above the 0 mL mark. Open the stopcock briefly to flush air from the tip into a waste beaker.
2
Set the zero: Drain titrant until the meniscus sits at or just below 0.00 mL. Wait 30 seconds for the film on the burette wall to drain. Record the exact initial reading.
3
Transfer the analyte: Pipette a precise volume of the analyte solution into a clean conical flask using a calibrated pipette and filler.
4
Add indicator: Add 2–3 drops. Place the flask on a white tile.
5
Rough titration first: Add titrant at approximately 1 mL per second while swirling the flask. Note the approximate volume at which the colour change becomes temporary (returns on swirling). This is your rough endpoint estimate.
6
Repeat accurately: Refill the burette to 0.00 mL. Add titrant rapidly until 1–2 mL before the rough endpoint. Then slow to drop-by-drop addition.
7
Half-drop technique: Within 0.5 mL of the endpoint, add half-drops by briefly opening the stopcock. Wash the drop into the solution with distilled water from the wash bottle.
8
Record the endpoint: Stop when the colour change persists for at least 30 seconds. Read and record the final burette volume.
Titre volume = final reading − initial reading. Perform at least 3 accurate titrations and use the mean of results concordant within 0.1 mL.

Burette reading technique

Incorrect burette reading is one of the most common sources of error. All readings must be taken at eye level with the meniscus.

  • For colourless solutions, read from the bottom of the meniscus. A white card held behind the burette helps visibility.
  • For coloured solutions (e.g. KMnO₄, which is dark purple), read from the top of the meniscus because the bottom cannot be seen through the colour.
  • Burettes are graduated top to bottom (0 at top, 50 at bottom). A reading of 23.45 mL means 23.45 mL has been dispensed if the initial reading was 0.00.
  • Always estimate to the nearest 0.01 mL even though the smallest graduation is 0.1 mL.
  • Wait 30 seconds after each addition before reading — the film of solution on the burette wall takes time to drain.
Parallax error

If your eye is above the meniscus level, the meniscus appears higher than it is (you read a smaller volume). If your eye is below, it appears lower (you read a larger volume). Always position your eye exactly level with the meniscus. Some burettes have a black band behind the graduation to help — align the top of the band with the bottom of the meniscus.

Detecting the endpoint accurately

The endpoint is the point at which the indicator changes colour and stays changed. The equivalence point is the theoretical point where moles of acid equal moles of base. Ideally these coincide, but a small indicator error always exists.

  • Phenolphthalein endpoint: A single drop of NaOH should turn the solution from colourless to a very faint pink that persists for 30 seconds. A bright pink or red colour means you have gone past the endpoint.
  • Methyl orange endpoint: The solution changes from red/pink (in acid) to orange at the endpoint. Full yellow means the endpoint has been passed. The orange colour is the transition — it can be difficult to judge. Screened methyl orange (green endpoint) is easier to read precisely.
  • Rinse the inside walls of the flask with distilled water from a wash bottle during and just before the endpoint — this washes down any solution splashed on the walls without affecting the result.
  • If you overshoot the endpoint (colour too deep), note the volume and use it as the rough estimate for the next, accurate titration. Do not try to back-titrate with the other reagent — start fresh.

Calculating the result

Concentration of analyte from titration data
C₂ = (C₁ × V₁ × n₁) / (V₂ × n₂)
C₁ = concentration of titrant (mol/L) | V₁ = mean titre volume (mL) V₂ = volume of analyte taken (mL) | n = stoichiometric ratio from the balanced equation
Worked example — finding concentration of HCl solution
1
25.00 mL of HCl solution is titrated with 0.1000 mol/L NaOH. Mean titre = 23.45 mL. Reaction: HCl + NaOH → NaCl + H₂O (1:1 ratio).
2
Moles of NaOH used = 0.1000 × (23.45/1000) = 0.002345 mol
3
At equivalence, moles HCl = moles NaOH = 0.002345 mol
4
Concentration of HCl = 0.002345 / (25.00/1000) = 0.09380 mol/L
The concentration of the HCl solution is 0.09380 mol/L (93.80 mmol/L).
Worked example — H₂SO₄ (diprotic acid) titrated with NaOH
1
20.00 mL of H₂SO₄ titrated with 0.1000 mol/L NaOH. Mean titre = 31.20 mL. Reaction: H₂SO₄ + 2NaOH → Na₂SO₄ + 2H₂O (ratio n₁=2, n₂=1 for NaOH).
2
Moles NaOH = 0.1000 × (31.20/1000) = 0.003120 mol
3
Moles H₂SO₄ = 0.003120 / 2 = 0.001560 mol
4
Concentration H₂SO₄ = 0.001560 / (20.00/1000) = 0.07800 mol/L
0.07800 mol/L H₂SO₄ — note how the diprotic nature halves the moles relative to a monoprotic acid.

Back titration

A back titration is used when the analyte cannot react directly with the titrant — for example because it is insoluble, volatile, or reacts too slowly. A known excess of one reagent is added, the excess is then titrated with a second standard solution, and the amount consumed by the analyte is calculated by difference.

Back titration — determining purity of CaCO₃ (limestone)
1
Weigh approximately 0.25 g of powdered CaCO₃. Add exactly 50.00 mL of 0.1000 mol/L HCl (excess). Allow reaction to complete: CaCO₃ + 2HCl → CaCl₂ + H₂O + CO₂
2
Boil gently to expel CO₂. Cool to room temperature.
3
Titrate the remaining HCl with 0.1000 mol/L NaOH. Suppose 18.60 mL NaOH is required.
4
Moles HCl added = 0.1000 × 0.0500 = 0.005000 mol
5
Moles HCl remaining = 0.1000 × 0.01860 = 0.001860 mol
6
Moles HCl consumed by CaCO₃ = 0.005000 − 0.001860 = 0.003140 mol
7
Moles CaCO₃ = 0.003140 / 2 = 0.001570 mol → mass = 0.001570 × 100.09 = 0.1571 g
% purity = (0.1571 / 0.25) × 100 = 62.84% CaCO₃

Key takeaways

What to remember from this tutorial

  • At the equivalence point: N₁V₁ = N₂V₂. For molar concentrations, account for stoichiometric ratios (n-factor).
  • Choose your indicator so its colour transition range includes the equivalence point pH of your specific acid-base pair.
  • Standardise your titrant against a primary standard before quantitative work. NaOH absorbs CO₂ and degrades over time.
  • Always rinse the burette with titrant, never with water. The conical flask may be rinsed with distilled water freely.
  • Perform a rough titration first, then accurate titrations. Use the mean of at least 3 concordant results (agreeing within 0.1 mL).
  • Use the half-drop technique within 0.5 mL of the endpoint. Phenolphthalein endpoint is a faint, persistent pink — not bright pink.
  • Read the burette meniscus at eye level. Always record readings to 0.01 mL.
